# LinkedIn Personal Newsletter: Price Transparency and Variance

*Spoke type: Personal profile newsletter (Aaron's voice). Goal: human story + compressed insight, driving to the full white paper. Post from Aaron's personal LinkedIn. ~750 words.*

---

**Headline:** Your staff member handed over the insurance card and the plan paid more than the cash price. That happens about half the time, and nobody on your account is checking.

---

A staff member needs an MRI. They do exactly what they were taught to do. They show their insurance card.

The plan gets billed the network rate, pays its share, the member pays a copay, and everyone walks away assuming the card did its job. That is what the card is for, right? That is the whole pitch.

A few miles down the road, a clinic posts a cash price for the identical scan. Same machine, same read, same everything. The cash number is lower than what your plan just paid through the network. The card did not save anyone money. It cost the plan more, and because the member's share gets calculated off the bigger number, it cost the member more too.

I used to think that was a fluke, the kind of thing that happens once and makes a good story. Then I started reading the data hospitals are now required to publish, and it stopped being a fluke. It is the pattern.

Let me give you the mechanics in plain English, because this is the heaviest line in your plan and most school leaders have never been shown the numbers.

First, there is no single price for a medical service. Not close. Researchers digging through the published price files have found the same procedure varying more than tenfold inside one hospital depending on who is paying, and more than thirtyfold across hospitals in the same state. Same scan. Same blood test. The price swings by a factor of ten, twenty, thirty, based mostly on who is paying and where.

Your instinct hears that and assumes the expensive place must be the better place. You are paying for quality. That instinct is where a lot of plan money goes to die. The link between price and quality in American healthcare is weak at best and in plenty of studies it is just not there. A higher price tag does not reliably buy you a better surgeon or a lower complication rate. It buys location and leverage.

So if price does not track quality, what is the network "discount" actually discounting? A fake number. A hospital's list price lives in a document called the chargemaster, and chargemaster prices are set high on purpose as an opening position. When your PPO advertises a 40 or 50 percent discount, that comes off the chargemaster. You are getting a real percentage off an unreal number. Cut a rigged number in half and you can still land far above what the care is worth.

Then the finding that should stop a busy administrator cold. A study in Health Affairs looked at seventy shoppable services across more than two thousand hospitals. For nearly half of them, roughly 47 percent, the posted cash price was lower than or equal to the median price your insurance negotiated, at the same hospital, for the same service. The card, the thing everyone treats as protection, is the more expensive option close to half the time.

For a self-funded school plan, that is not a trivia question. Every inflated negotiated dollar is your plan's dollar, which is your budget's dollar, which is money that was supposed to go toward teachers and kids.

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch, where if you did a job halfway somebody got hurt or something broke, and you owned it. A broker doing the job pulls the transparency data for the facilities your staff actually use, lays the cash price next to the negotiated price on your highest-volume services, and shows you where the plan is paying the big number for no extra quality. A generalist re-ups the same network, repeats the line about the discount, passes along the increase, and never opens the files. The numbers sit published on a public website nobody on your account bothered to read.

I wrote the full breakdown up on our site. No jargon, no gate, free to read and free to forward to your board or your business manager: **[LINK TO WHITE PAPER]**

If you do one thing with this, do this. Ask whoever handles your insurance one question.

On the handful of procedures your staff use most, has anyone ever pulled the published cash price and set it next to what your plan actually paid through the network? If no one has put those two numbers side by side, how would you know whether your network is buying you lower prices, or just charging you for access to higher ones?

If they cannot answer that, you do not have a verdict yet. You have a question nobody bothered to ask, on data that has been public for years.
