**Headline:** A group medical captive is the funding endgame for the right charter school. It is also real risk transferred onto your balance sheet. Here is how to tell where your school actually belongs, and what has to be true before you move.

A group stop-loss captive can return underwriting surplus and investment income to your school instead of a carrier's shareholders. It can also cost you more than a flat premium in a brutal claims year. The difference between those two outcomes is readiness, not luck. Below is the funding spectrum staged from lowest control to highest, the readiness guardrails that have to be in place, and the checklist we run before recommending any move up the ladder.

## The funding spectrum, staged

| Stage | Who carries the risk | Healthy-year upside | Bad-year exposure | Data you get | Best fit |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Fully insured** | Carrier keeps premium and profit | None. Premium is gone either way | Capped at premium, but you overpay in good years | Little to none | Schools not ready to manage a plan |
| **Level-funded** | You self-fund claims; stop-loss caps the downside | Healthy year pays you back a share | Capped close to your budgeted monthly | Real monthly claims data, first time | The runway. Prove your claims story here first |
| **Group captive** | You + member pool own the stop-loss layer | Underwriting surplus + investment income returned to members | Higher than a flat premium in a bad year, ceiling set in advance | Full claims + pharmacy transparency | Clean claims history, cash to ride a swing, multi-year commitment |

The order matters. We do not jump a school from fully insured straight into a captive. Level-funded is the runway that proves the numbers and builds the habit of managing a self-funded plan before ownership risk goes on the table.

## The three readiness guardrails (non-negotiable before a captive)

| Guardrail | Why it protects your result | What "ready" looks like |
|---|---|---|
| **A tight plan document** | In a self-funded captive, every dollar paid out is partly yours and partly the pool's. A loose document leaks claims that should have been questioned | Drafted and maintained on purpose, eligibility and terms current, not a carrier's stale boilerplate |
| **An independent pass-through PBM** | A bundled PBM profits off spread pricing and retained rebates, so it earns more when you spend more | Flat-fee, pass-through pricing, full rebate and cost visibility |
| **Real cost-control mechanisms** | Controlling losses is what earns the dividend back. A passive captive is a pool waiting to overspend | Care management on high-cost claimants, specialty drug strategy, reference-based pricing options, freedom to carve out and switch vendors |

## The readiness checklist

- [ ] Confirm the school is off fully insured and has run level-funded long enough to produce a credible claims story (generally one to two years).
- [ ] Pull and review monthly claims data; identify cost drivers, not just the bottom-line number.
- [ ] Confirm a cash position that can absorb a bad-year additional contribution before reinsurance attaches.
- [ ] Draft or refresh the plan document so eligibility and payment terms are deliberate and current.
- [ ] Carve out an independent, pass-through PBM with full rebate visibility.
- [ ] Stand up real cost controls: high-cost claimant management, specialty drug strategy, network/RBP decisions.
- [ ] Model the captive in dollars at both ends, the ownership upside in a healthy year and the honest additional-contribution exposure in a bad one.
- [ ] Confirm leadership understands and accepts a genuine multi-year commitment before binding.
- [ ] Match the operator (Pareto, Captive Resources, Roundstone, Evergreen and others) to the school's size, state, and appetite, not the other way around.

## Why this is standard practice for us

A generalist broker keeps schools fully insured because re-quoting the same plan is the easy button, and then calls a captive too advanced for a school your size, even though the operators who built this model open it to employers as small as twenty-five lives. We build the runway on purpose: level-funded first to prove the numbers, then a captive when and only when the guardrails are in place, with the downside modeled in dollars before anyone takes on the risk.
