# LinkedIn Personal Newsletter: The Health Plan That Owns Its Own Insurance Company

*Spoke type: Personal profile newsletter (Aaron's voice). Goal: human story + compressed insight, driving to the full white paper. Post from Aaron's personal LinkedIn. ~750 words.*

**Headline:** Two charter schools, same size, same town. One mails its premium off forever. The other gets a check back with its own name on it.

---

I sat across from a school business manager last spring who had just opened her renewal. Same fully insured plan, bigger number, like clockwork. She asked me a question I hear all the time: "Is this just what it costs now?"

So I drew her two schools on a napkin.

Both self-funded. Both the same size, same town, same kind of staff. The first one hands its stop-loss premium to a carrier every year and watches it vanish into someone else's profit. The second one pools that same slice of risk with a few dozen employers like it. In a healthy year, a check comes back to the second school with its own name on it, plus a share of the interest the money earned while it sat in reserve.

Same plan underneath. Same employees, same claims, same coverage. The only thing different is who owns the insurance company sitting on top.

That second structure is a group medical stop-loss captive, and for the right school it is the endgame.

Here is the mechanic in plain English. A captive does not replace your plan. It sits on top of it. Your school self-funds, paying claims out of your own account instead of buying a fixed premium. To cap the downside, you buy stop-loss. A group captive changes who carries that stop-loss layer. Instead of renting it from a carrier that keeps every dollar of profit, a group of similar employers form a member-owned entity and pool a slice of the risk inside it. Each school funds its routine claims at the bottom. A shared middle layer cushions the mid-sized claims across all the members, so one school's rough year gets absorbed by the pool. Reinsurance catches the truly catastrophic claims at the top.

When that pooled layer collects more than it pays out, the surplus comes back to the members, plus the investment income earned along the way. You stop being a premium payer and start being an owner.

Now the part I am stubborn about. I do not put a school into a captive on day one. I think that is reckless, and I think most brokers who try it are chasing a story instead of protecting a client.

The path I believe in starts with level-funded. You step off fully insured into a plan where a healthy year pays you back, you get real monthly claims data for the first time, and you prove out your own claims story over a year or two with the budget predictability of a set monthly payment. Stop-loss is still doing the heavy lifting, so your downside stays capped close to what you already budgeted. Then, when the data says you are a good risk and your leadership is comfortable, you graduate into the captive. Going level-funded first is not a detour. It is the runway that lets you walk in with your eyes open and your numbers proven.

And let me be honest about the downside, because anybody who sells a captive as guaranteed savings is lying to you. A captive moves real risk onto your school. In a genuinely brutal claims year you can owe more than your best case before reinsurance kicks in. The ceiling is set in advance, but it is higher than a flat premium in a bad year. That is exactly why I will not put a school near one without three things locked in first: a tight plan document, an independent pass-through PBM, and real cost controls running on the high-cost claims. Skip those and a captive can cost you more, not less.

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch, where a job done halfway meant somebody got hurt or something broke. That is the standard I expected when I got into insurance. Building the runway from level-funded to a captive is work, and the generalist who runs benefits as a side dish never builds it. So they keep you fully insured and pass along the increase, then tell you a captive is too advanced for a school your size, when the operators who built this model open it to employers as small as twenty-five lives.
