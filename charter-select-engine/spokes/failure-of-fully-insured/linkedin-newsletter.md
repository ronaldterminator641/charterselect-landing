# LinkedIn Personal Newsletter: The Failure of Fully Insured

*Spoke type: Personal profile newsletter (Aaron's voice). Goal: human story + compressed insight, driving to the full white paper. Post from Aaron's personal LinkedIn. ~750 words.*

---

**Headline:** Your school had a healthy year. Your reward was a 12% premium hike. Here's where that money went.

---

A school leader walked me through her renewal last year and she was confused. Her staff had been healthy. No big surgeries, no catastrophic claims, twelve quiet months. By any honest read, her health plan had made money that year.

Then the renewal landed with a double-digit increase on it, and her broker told her that was just the market.

So she asked me the obvious question. If we had a good year, where did that money go? I had to tell her the truth: it went to the insurer, and she was never getting it back. Not a refund, not a credit, not even a heads up that it happened. That wasn't a glitch in her plan. That was her plan working exactly the way it was built to work.

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch. If you did a job halfway out there, somebody got hurt or something broke, and you owned it. That's the standard I expected when I got into insurance. It is not the standard most charter schools are getting, and the fully insured health plan is one of the clearest examples I know.

Here's the mechanic, in plain English.

The plan most charter schools carry is called fully insured. You pay a fixed premium every month and the insurance company takes on the risk of paying claims. On the surface that sounds fair and predictable. The unfair part comes down to one question: who keeps the money in a good year?

In a fully insured plan, if your people stay healthy and claims come in well below what you paid in premium, the insurer keeps the difference. All of it. The surplus your healthy staff generated becomes their profit. Now flip it. Bad year, high claims, the insurer covers it, then comes back at renewal and raises your rate to recover the cost and protect their margin.

Read those two outcomes together and you'll see the trap. Good year, they keep the surplus. Bad year, they raise your rate. You carry the downside in both directions and capture the upside in neither.

There's a quieter problem stacked on top of that one. A fully insured plan hands you almost no usable claims data. You can't see whether your costs are being driven by a few large claims, a pharmacy spend problem, or overuse of the emergency room. You get a renewal number and you're told to take it or leave it, with no window into how it was built. You can't manage what you can't see, and staying blind keeps you dependent on whatever number the insurer hands you.

For a school this lands harder than almost anywhere. Every premium dollar is a dollar with somewhere better to be. A classroom aide. A counselor. A raise that keeps a good teacher from leaving for the district down the road. And you're already competing for staff against public districts with deep benefits and pension systems behind them. When your health plan eats your budget and still raises rates every year, you lose ground on the exact tool you'd use to keep good people.

Here's what your generalist broker probably never told you: the surplus from a healthy year does not have to vanish into the insurer's pocket. There are funding models built so a good claims year puts money back into your budget instead of theirs, and they are not reserved for giant corporations. Schools your size use them. Those models carry their own structure and their own honest tradeoffs, and that's a separate paper. The point here is narrower. If your school is fully insured, you are in the one arrangement where you are guaranteed to never benefit from your own people's good health. You should at least know that's the deal you're in.

A broker doing the actual job walks you through where your premium dollars went, tells you plainly whether you had a good or bad claims year, and lays the alternatives on the table with real numbers. A generalist renews you fully insured every year because it's the easy button. No data to pull, no analysis to run. They re-up the same plan, pass along the increase, and call it service. That's the part I can't stomach, and it's most of why Charter Select exists and works only with charter schools.

If they can't answer that clearly, you just learned something about both your plan and your broker.
