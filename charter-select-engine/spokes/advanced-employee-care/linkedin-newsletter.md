**Headline:** You didn't lose your best teacher on salary. You lost her on the part of the offer you never built.

---

A school leader I talked with last spring spent two years developing a teacher she thought she'd keep for a decade. Then the teacher put in her notice and took a job at the district down the road.

The money was a little better, sure. But that wasn't the headline when the teacher explained it. Her husband had waited five weeks for a primary care appointment. Her kid's inhaler cost more than a car payment. The new place just made all of that easier.

So the leader didn't lose her on salary. She lost her on health benefits, which is the part of the offer most charter schools never actually design.

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch, where a job done halfway meant somebody got hurt or something broke, and you owned it. That's the standard I expected when I got into insurance. It is not the standard most charter schools are getting on benefits, and it costs them people.

Most leaders are never shown what's possible. There are two upgrades that change both the care your staff actually receives and the money your school actually spends. Most charter leaders have never been offered either one.

The first is Direct Primary Care, or DPC.

In the normal model, every doctor visit is a billable event. The practice files a claim, codes it, waits to get paid, and the whole machine rewards volume. A doctor on that treadmill carries two thousand patients and books seven-minute visits to keep the lights on. That's why your teacher's husband waited five weeks and then felt rushed out the door.

DPC drops the billing treadmill. The plan pays a flat monthly fee per member straight to a primary care practice. In exchange, the member gets unlimited access: longer visits, same-day or next-day scheduling, direct messaging with the actual doctor, a smaller patient panel. Many DPC practices run labs in house and dispense common generic drugs at wholesale, so a strep test or a blood panel costs the member little or nothing at the counter.

To be precise, because this is where leaders get nervous: DPC is not insurance and does not replace it. It covers the routine, high-frequency care. So you wrap a DPC membership together with an insurance plan sitting behind it for specialists, surgery, and the catastrophic events insurance actually exists for. Pair DPC with a leaner insurance layer, and the rich front-end access can offset a cheaper plan, because so much care never touches insurance at all. Your people get more. The plan can cost less. Both at once.

The second upgrade is at the pharmacy counter, where some of the strangest money in American health care moves.

Your drug benefit is run by a Pharmacy Benefit Manager. In theory the PBM negotiates good prices for you. In practice, the way most traditional contracts are written, the middleman profits from keeping you in the dark. There's spread pricing, where the PBM pays the pharmacy one price, bills your plan a higher one, and pockets the difference you never see. And there's rebate retention, where manufacturer rebates that should flow back to your plan stay with the PBM instead, which quietly steers the system toward pricier drugs that pay bigger rebates.

The fix is a transparent, pass-through model. The PBM charges your plan exactly what the pharmacy was paid, with a disclosed fee instead of a hidden spread, and rebates flow back. Once the true cost is visible, the games stop paying, and the spend usually comes down on its own.

None of this is reserved for giant corporations. Schools your size run DPC and transparent pharmacy programs right now. The reason yours doesn't isn't cost and isn't eligibility. It's that nobody offered it. A generalist broker who carries restaurants and law firms and your school in the same book shows up near renewal, hands you the same fully insured plan with a new premium stapled on, and calls that service. That's selling a renewal, not designing a benefit.
