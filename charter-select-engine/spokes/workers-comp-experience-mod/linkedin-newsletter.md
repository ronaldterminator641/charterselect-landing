**Headline:** Two charter schools, same size, same payroll. One pays thousands more for workers' comp every year. The difference is a number nobody hands you.

---

A few years back I sat with a school leader who was proud of how tight she ran her budget. She watched every line. When her workers' comp renewal came in, she did what she always did. Checked that the premium hadn't jumped, confirmed the headcount looked right, signed it, moved on. There was a school to run.

What she never saw was the worksheet underneath that premium. On it was a number built from her own past claims, quietly multiplying every dollar she paid. It had been set high years earlier, and it had been riding her renewal ever since. Nobody had ever pulled it. Nobody had ever checked it for errors. Her broker had treated the renewal as a form to process, and the meter had been running the whole time.

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch. If you did a job halfway, somebody got hurt or something broke, and you owned it. That's the standard I expected when I got into insurance. It is not the standard a lot of charter schools are getting, and workers' comp is one of the clearest examples I know.

Here's the mechanic, in plain English.

Your workers' comp premium starts with payroll. Then it sorts that payroll into job classification codes, each with its own rate, because grading papers carries less injury risk than running a table saw. Then it does one more thing that catches everyone off guard. It multiplies the whole bill by a factor built from your school's own claims history. That factor is the experience modification, the e-mod, and it behaves almost exactly like a credit score for your insurance.

A clean history earns a mod below 1.0, a discount on every dollar. A rough one pushes the mod above 1.0, a surcharge on every dollar. And like a credit score, it follows you, it compounds, and it's frequently wrong in ways nobody catches until somebody looks.

Two quiet leaks feed it.

The first is class codes. Every job at your school maps to a classification, and when a code gets assigned wrong, it tends to get assigned high, then never gets revisited. Picture an office administrator filed under a hands-on, higher-rated code years ago by someone who grabbed the nearest one. That mistake doesn't announce itself. It charges you the higher rate on that salary every single year while the renewal sails through.

The second leak is the mod itself, and the sneakiest part of it is reserves. When a claim is open, the insurer sets aside an estimate of what it expects to pay. That estimate, not just the dollars actually paid out, is what feeds your e-mod. An open claim sitting on a fat, conservative reserve can inflate your mod even if it later settles for far less. The calculation pulls those figures as of a specific date each year. If a bloated reserve is on the books when that snapshot gets taken, the inflated number locks into your mod and rides your premium for years, long after the claim closed cheap.

Nobody challenges the reserve, because the generalist broker who placed the policy has never called the adjuster, doesn't know your open claims, and isn't going to start the week your mod gets calculated. That's slow, unglamorous work, and it doesn't help them close the next account.

The fixes aren't exotic. Audit your class codes against your real roster. Manage open claims toward closure instead of letting them drift. Stand up a return-to-work program so an injured employee can come back to light duty instead of sitting home while the claim swells. And review open-claim reserves before the snapshot date locks them in. That's the whole job. It just requires someone who treats your school like a client instead of a renewal to clear off the desk.
