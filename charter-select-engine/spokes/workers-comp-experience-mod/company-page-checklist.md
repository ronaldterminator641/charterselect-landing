**Headline:** Your workers' comp premium is built on three inputs you control. Most schools have never checked any of them.

Workers' comp premium is not a single quoted number. It is payroll, sorted into classification codes, multiplied by an experience modifier built from your own claims history. Each layer carries an error that compounds quietly across renewals, and a generalist broker processing the renewal as a form will not catch any of them. Here is how the inflators compare, and the audit we run on every workers' comp renewal.

## The four levers that move your workers' comp bill

| Lever | What goes wrong | What it costs | Who fixes it |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Class-code assignment** | Staff filed under a higher-rated code than their real work, then carried forward unrevisited | The higher rate on that salary, every year, until corrected | Line-by-line audit against the real roster |
| **Claims management** | Open claims left to drift with no school contact run longer and cost more | Higher claim cost feeds a higher mod | Prompt reporting, active adjuster contact, drive to closure |
| **Return-to-work** | No light-duty plan, so injured staff stay home collecting indemnity | Indemnity cost climbs and lands in the mod | A defined modified-duty program in place before it's needed |
| **Open-claim reserves** | Conservative reserves snapshot into the mod before claims settle low | Inflated mod rides the premium for years after the claim closes | Reserve review with adjuster before the unit statistical date |

You control all four. Left unmanaged, all four run against you at once.

## The renewal checklist

- [ ] Pull the e-mod worksheet for the current and prior rating years, in writing.
- [ ] Audit every position's class code against the actual work that person performs.
- [ ] Flag any administrative or clerical role filed under a hands-on, higher-rated code.
- [ ] Confirm whether prior overpayments from misclassification may be recoverable under the rules in play.
- [ ] Identify every open claim and its current reserve.
- [ ] Know your unit statistical date, the cutoff when claim figures get pulled into the mod.
- [ ] In the weeks before that date, review each open-claim reserve with the adjuster and push down any reserve set higher than the facts justify.
- [ ] Confirm a return-to-work program with defined light-duty roles exists before an injury occurs.
- [ ] Document the verified mod, codes, and reserve positions for the board record.

## Why this is standard practice for us and rare elsewhere

A busy generalist working a renewal at volume has no incentive to audit codes line by line or to pick a fight with an adjuster over a reserve sitting too high. That work is slow and unglamorous, and it does not help them close the next account.
