# LinkedIn Personal Newsletter: SRO Legal Liability

*Spoke type: Personal profile newsletter (Aaron's voice). Goal: human story + compressed insight, driving to the full white paper. Post from Aaron's personal LinkedIn. ~750 words.*

---

**Headline:** You put an officer on campus to keep kids safe. The two policies you trust most are both written to walk away from what happens next.

---

A school leader I talked with last year had done the responsible thing. She put a trained, sworn officer on her campus. A real safety decision, the kind a board feels good signing off on.

Then a hallway incident happened. A student taken to the ground, an arm twisted, a parent recording the last ten seconds on a phone. Eighteen months later a federal civil rights complaint landed, and it named the officer, the police department, and her school.

She called her broker, sure this was exactly what insurance was for. That call did not go the way she expected.

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch, where doing a job halfway meant somebody got hurt or something broke, and you owned it. That is the standard I carried into insurance. It is not the standard a lot of charter schools are getting, and the School Resource Officer gap is one of the cleanest examples I know.

Here is the mechanic, in plain English.

A school usually carries two main liability lines. General liability handles the slip-and-fall side of running a building. Educators legal liability, ELL, handles the professional decisions a school and its board make. Both are useful. Neither one was built for law enforcement, and the standard forms tend to say so out loud.

General liability is written for normal business operations, and law enforcement activity is a recognized professional exposure that gets carved out, often by a specific endorsement that names it. ELL has its own exclusions, and claims tied to intentional or criminal acts, which is the ground a use-of-force or false-arrest claim stands on, frequently sit outside it.

Read those two facts together and the gap comes into focus. The coverage you assume protects you against an officer incident is usually the exact coverage written to exclude it. A use-of-force suit can land right in the seam between the two policies, where neither one was designed to answer.

People assume that if the officer technically works for the police department, the school stays clean. It does not work that way reliably. When a plaintiff's attorney looks at an incident inside your building, on your students, they do not name only the officer. They name the department, and they name the school. Whether the school ultimately owes anything is a question for the litigation. Getting pulled into the litigation is close to automatic.

There is a line built for exactly this. Law Enforcement Legal Liability, sometimes called LELL or police professional liability. It exists precisely because general liability shuts law enforcement out. It responds to use-of-force claims, wrongful arrest, improper search, and the civil rights claims filed under that federal framework, and it funds the defense costs that pile up long before anyone reaches a verdict.

And there is one document that ties the whole thing together: the Memorandum of Understanding, the MOU, between the school and the department. It spells out who supervises the officer, how complaints get handled, and how insurance and indemnity get allocated. It is the single most important risk document in the arrangement, and it routinely gets signed without the one person who should read it ever seeing it. An administrator and a police chief sign. The broker, the person who would know whether the indemnity promise is backed by an actual policy, is usually never in the room.

The reason this gets missed is not that it is exotic. It is that a generalist broker treating your school as a quick renewal never asked the one question that changes everything: is there an officer on this campus, and what happens when a claim comes out of what that officer does? A broker who actually knows schools asks it on the first call, reads the MOU against your policies, and tells you plainly if there is a real exposure with no line built to catch it. That is most of why Charter Select only works with charter schools and reads the whole policy every renewal.

So here is the one worth sitting with, today, while it is still a quiet question and not a lawsuit.

If an incident involving your officer turned into a civil rights claim next month, do you actually know which policy responds, and have you confirmed it is not one of the two that are written to exclude it? Or has nobody on your side ever lined up the MOU you signed against the coverage you are paying for?

If you cannot answer that with confidence, you do not have a coverage problem yet. You have a question no one has checked.
