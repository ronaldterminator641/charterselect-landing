**Headline:** Crime and cyber answer two different disasters. The right question is not which one to buy, but what your school is trying to protect against, because you need both plus the seam between them.

Start with the exposure, not the product. A school faces two separate categories of financial disaster. Someone takes your funds, which is a crime loss. Or your network and data get hit, which is a cyber loss. Each has its own policy, the two barely overlap, and a third loss sits in the seam where both can deny. Here is which loss each one pays for, and the checklist we run on every program.

## Which loss each coverage pays for

| Loss type | Crime policy | Cyber policy | Needs the social-engineering endorsement |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Employee theft / embezzlement** | Pays | Denies (no network event) | No |
| **Forgery / altered checks** | Pays | Denies (no network event) | No |
| **Robbery / stolen money or securities** | Pays | Denies (no network event) | No |
| **Ransomware / system shutdown** | Denies (no theft of funds) | Pays | No |
| **Breach of student or staff records** | Denies (no theft of funds) | Pays | No |
| **Notification to families / regulatory** | Denies (no theft of funds) | Pays | No |
| **Business interruption from downtime** | Denies (no theft of funds) | Pays | No |
| **Wire to a fake vendor (deception fraud)** | Often denies (employee had authority) | Often denies (no breach) | Yes |
| **Fraudulent instruction to your bank** | Often denies | Often denies | Yes |

The crime column and the cyber column barely overlap, and that is by design. A school carrying only one is fully exposed to every loss in the other column. The bottom rows are the seam: a fraudulent-instruction or social-engineering wire that both base policies can deny, which is why it needs its own endorsement. That endorsement attaches to the crime policy or the cyber policy depending on the carrier, it never rides along for free, and its sublimit is frequently capped well below the main policy limit.

## The coverage checklist

- [ ] Confirm the program carries a crime policy with real limits, not a placeholder amount.
- [ ] Confirm the program carries a cyber policy with real limits, not a placeholder amount.
- [ ] Confirm both are in force at once; one does not stand in for the other.
- [ ] Confirm a funds transfer fraud and social engineering fraud endorsement is actually on the program, in writing.
- [ ] Verify which policy the endorsement sits on; it can be the crime policy or the cyber policy depending on the carrier.
- [ ] Pull the endorsement sublimit and compare it against the largest payment your school could realistically send, not the main policy limit.
- [ ] If the sublimit is a small fraction of a realistic wire, request a higher one and price it.
- [ ] Put the vendor call-back control in writing: any change to banking details is confirmed by phone to a known number, never the number in the email.
- [ ] Confirm whether the carrier requires that call-back verification as a condition of payment.
- [ ] Document the confirmed policies, endorsement, and sublimit for the board record.

## Why this is standard practice for us

A school is protected when something responds to each disaster, not when one policy gets called good enough for all of it. We put crime and cyber side by side, show you which loss lands on which policy, point out the seam between them, and confirm the funds transfer and social engineering endorsement is on one of the two with a sublimit that means something. Then we check the call-back control that some carriers require before they pay. That is the review we run as a matter of course, because a school should never learn which column a loss lives in on the day it files.
