**Headline:** You carry both crime and cyber coverage. The most common financial loss a school suffers falls in the gap between them. Here is how to close it.

When a criminal tricks an employee into wiring money to a fake vendor account, that loss has a name: social engineering fraud, sometimes written as fraudulent instruction or deception fraud. It is one of the most common and most expensive financial crimes any organization faces, schools included, and a standard crime policy and a standard cyber policy can both deny it. Here is how the three coverages compare, and the checklist we run on every program.

## What each coverage actually responds to

| Coverage | What it responds to | The wire-fraud verdict |
|---|---|---|
| **Crime policy** | Theft of money or securities: employee dishonesty, embezzlement, forgery, robbery, certain computer fraud where a thief breaks in and moves funds | Denies. The employee had authority and sent the money voluntarily. No one broke in and took it. |
| **Cyber policy** | Network or data events: ransomware, data breach, family/staff notification costs, regulatory exposure, business interruption | Denies. No system was breached. An employee read an email and authorized a payment. |
| **Social engineering fraud endorsement** | An employee tricked into voluntarily sending money to a criminal on a fraudulent instruction | Responds. This is the only one of the three that pays the fake-invoice wire. |

Without the third row, you are exposed no matter how much crime and cyber coverage you carry. The endorsement usually bolts onto the crime policy, and its sublimit is frequently capped well below your main crime limit.

## The coverage-gap checklist

- [ ] Confirm a social engineering fraud endorsement (fraudulent instruction / deception fraud) is actually on the program, in writing.
- [ ] Verify which policy it sits on; it is usually added to the crime policy.
- [ ] Pull the sublimit and compare it against the largest payment your school could realistically send, not the main crime limit.
- [ ] If the sublimit is a small fraction of a realistic wire, request a higher one and price it.
- [ ] Put a verification habit in writing: any change to vendor banking details is confirmed by phone to a known number, never the number in the email.
- [ ] Confirm whether the carrier requires that callback control as a condition of payment.
- [ ] Document the confirmed endorsement and sublimit for the board record.

## Why this is standard practice for us and rare elsewhere

A generalist broker chasing a small account quotes the crime policy and the cyber policy, says "you're covered for crime and cyber," and moves on. Technically true. The single most likely financial crime against a school is the one neither base policy covers. We map the seam between the two out loud, confirm the social engineering endorsement is in place, and pressure-test the sublimit against the actual size of your wires, because that is the loss a lean back office is most likely to walk into.
