**Headline:** Crime and cyber cover two different disasters. Carrying one and assuming it does the other's job is how a school ends up with the wrong coverage for the loss it actually suffers.

A school filing on its cyber policy for an embezzling employee gets denied. A school filing on its crime policy for a ransomware shutdown gets denied. The two policies respond to separate events, and a third loss sits in the seam between them where both can deny. Here is which loss each one pays for, and the checklist we run on every program.

## Which loss each coverage pays for

| Loss | Crime policy | Cyber policy | FTF / social engineering endorsement |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Employee theft / embezzlement** | Pays | Denies (no network event) | n/a |
| **Forgery / altered checks** | Pays | Denies (no network event) | n/a |
| **Ransomware / system shutdown** | Denies (no theft of funds) | Pays | n/a |
| **Breach notification to families** | Denies (no theft of funds) | Pays | n/a |
| **Wire to a fake vendor (deception fraud)** | Often denies (employee had authority) | Often denies (no breach) | Pays |

The crime column and the cyber column barely overlap. A school carrying only one is fully exposed to every loss in the other column. The bottom row is the seam: a fraudulent-instruction wire that both base policies can deny, which is why it needs its own endorsement. That endorsement attaches to the crime policy or the cyber policy depending on the carrier, it never rides along for free, and its sublimit is frequently capped well below the main policy limit.

## The coverage checklist

- [ ] Confirm the program carries both a crime policy and a cyber policy, each with real limits, not one standing in for both.
- [ ] Confirm a funds transfer fraud / social engineering fraud endorsement is actually on the program, in writing.
- [ ] Verify which policy the endorsement sits on; it can be the crime policy or the cyber policy depending on the carrier.
- [ ] Pull the endorsement sublimit and compare it against the largest payment your school could realistically send, not the main policy limit.
- [ ] If the sublimit is a small fraction of a realistic wire, request a higher one and price it.
- [ ] Put a verification habit in writing: any change to vendor banking details is confirmed by phone to a known number, never the number in the email.
- [ ] Confirm whether the carrier requires that callback control as a condition of payment.
- [ ] Document the confirmed policies, endorsement, and sublimit for the board record.

## Why this is standard practice for us and rare elsewhere

A generalist broker sells the shiny cyber policy, says the word "covered," and leaves the older half of the picture blank. The cyber sales rush of the last several years did exactly that across the market: schools got the new product and were never asked the obvious follow-up about employee theft, a forged check, or a wire to a fake vendor. We put crime and cyber side by side, show you which loss lands on which policy, point out the seam between them, and confirm the funds transfer and social engineering endorsement is on one of the two with a sublimit that means something. That is the review we run as a matter of course.
