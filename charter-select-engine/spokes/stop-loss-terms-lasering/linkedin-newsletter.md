# LinkedIn Personal Newsletter: Stop-Loss Terms and Lasering

*Spoke type: Personal profile newsletter (Aaron's voice). Goal: human story + compressed insight, driving to the full white paper. Post from Aaron's personal LinkedIn. ~750 words.*

---

**Headline:** The school had stop-loss insurance for exactly this claim. The carrier still sent the bill back to the school.

---

A while back I sat with a school leader whose plan had taken a hit nobody saw coming. A teacher's spouse got a cancer diagnosis, and the treatment ran past half a million dollars over the following eighteen months. The school self-funded its health plan, which is a smart setup for a lot of schools. And it had bought stop-loss insurance for one reason: so a single catastrophic claim could never sink the budget.

The claim came in. It got denied at the stop-loss level. Not because the health plan didn't cover the treatment, it did, but because of where the dates landed and what the contract said about them. The school ate the difference. And the contract that did it had been sitting in the file the whole time, signed, with terms nobody ever read out loud.

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch, where doing a job halfway meant somebody got hurt or something broke, and you owned it. That's the standard I carried into insurance. It is not the standard a lot of charter schools are getting on the one policy that's supposed to catch them when everything else fails.

Here's the mechanic, in plain English.

When your school self-funds, you pay employee medical claims out of your own money instead of handing a fixed premium to an insurer. Stop-loss is the policy that caps your downside. It comes in two parts. Specific stop-loss is your per-person cap, the one that catches the single huge claim. Aggregate is your whole-plan cap, the one that catches a year where the pile of claims together blows past budget. A school carrying only one of them is half-covered and usually doesn't know it.

But the part that quietly denies real claims is the contract basis. Every medical claim has two dates: when the care happened, and when the claim actually paid out. Those can land months apart. Your stop-loss contract covers claims using a window written as two numbers, like 12/12 or 12/15. The first is the window for when a claim was incurred. The second is the window for when it gets paid. Switch carriers without lining up run-in or run-out coverage, and a claim incurred in your old year that hasn't finished paying falls into a seam between two contracts where neither one pays. The care was covered. The school still writes the check.

Then there's lasering. If you have an employee already in expensive treatment, the stop-loss carrier can single that one person out at renewal and assign them a much higher deductible than everyone else. Your exposure on the exact member you most need protected goes up, sometimes by a lot. Lasering is a legitimate underwriting tool, not a scam. The problem is when it shows up as a surprise at renewal, right when you have the least leverage. Provisions exist to manage it: a no-laser provision, a no-new-laser provision, a rate cap. They cost a premium load. Whether you buy them should be your decision, made on purpose, not something you discover after a laser lands.

So why does this keep happening? A broker who runs benefits as a side dish does the easy version of the job. They collect a few quotes, hand you the lowest premium, and call it a placement. The premium is the one number that fits on a spreadsheet, so it's the only number they sell on. The contract basis, the run-in and run-out terms, the laser provisions, that's where the real protection lives, and reading it takes an afternoon. So it gets skipped. You find out the terms were wrong the day a big claim drops through the gap, and by then the renewal is signed.

That's not a knowledge problem on your end. You run a school. It's the job you were paying someone else to do.

If you can't answer that with confidence, you don't have a claim problem yet. You have a contract nobody read to you.
