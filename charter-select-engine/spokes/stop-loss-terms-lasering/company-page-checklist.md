**Headline:** Self-funded with stop-loss? The terms decide whether your safety net catches a catastrophic claim. The premium quote never tells you that.

When a charter school self-funds or level-funds, stop-loss insurance is the policy that caps the downside on a single catastrophic claim or a brutal claims year. The protection you actually get is defined entirely by the contract terms, not the premium. Here is how the terms that matter compare, and the checklist we run on every stop-loss renewal.

## The stop-loss terms that decide whether you are covered

| Term | What it does | Why it matters to a school |
|---|---|---|
| **Specific stop-loss** | Per-person cap; carrier reimburses claims above your specific deductible on any one individual | Catches the single catastrophic claim (cancer, NICU, transplant) |
| **Aggregate stop-loss** | Whole-plan cap, commonly set near 125% of expected claims | Catches a year where many mid-size claims together blow past budget |
| **Contract basis (e.g. 12/12, 12/15, 24/12)** | Defines which claims are covered by when they were incurred and when they were paid | A carrier switch with no run-in or run-out leaves claims in a seam neither contract pays |
| **Run-out (e.g. 12/15)** | Covers claims incurred in your year but paid after it ends | Protects the big claim that happens late in the year and settles months later |
| **Run-in (e.g. 24/12, 15/12)** | Covers claims paid in your year even if incurred before the contract started | Protects you when switching carriers with old claims still settling |
| **No-laser / no-new-laser provision** | Carrier agrees not to laser anyone, or not to add new lasers at next renewal | Stops a surprise deductible spike on your highest-cost member |
| **Rate cap** | Ceiling on how much renewal premium can jump | Blocks the carrier from repricing you into the same pain a laser would cause |
| **Aggregating specific deductible** | Lowers specific premium by adding a second pooled retention layer the school pays first | Sold as savings; can cost more out of pocket in a heavy claims year |

A laser is when the carrier singles out a known high-cost claimant at renewal and assigns them a higher individual deductible than everyone else. It is a legitimate underwriting tool. The damage comes when it lands as a surprise, after the leverage to negotiate is gone.

## The renewal checklist

- [ ] Confirm you carry both specific and aggregate stop-loss, not just one.
- [ ] Pull the contract basis on the current policy in writing (the two-number window, e.g. 12/12).
- [ ] On any carrier switch, confirm run-in or run-out coverage so no claim falls into a seam between contracts.
- [ ] Identify any known high-cost claimants the carrier can see coming before renewal terms arrive.
- [ ] Confirm whether the renewal carries lasers, and price no-laser, no-new-laser, and rate-cap options.
- [ ] Check for an aggregating specific deductible and model the worst-case out-of-pocket, not just the premium savings.
- [ ] Read the full contract before binding, not the quote summary.
- [ ] Document the confirmed basis and laser terms for the board record.

## Why this is standard practice for us and rare elsewhere

A generalist broker chasing a lower premium collects a few quotes, hands over the lowest number, and calls it a placement. The basis, the run-in and run-out terms, and the laser provisions take an afternoon to read, explain, and negotiate, so they get skipped.
