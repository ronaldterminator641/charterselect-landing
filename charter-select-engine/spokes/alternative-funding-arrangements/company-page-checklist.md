**Headline:** Fully insured isn't your only option. Before you renew, see how the funding models actually compare, including the downside.

Health plan funding runs along a spectrum, and where a charter school sits on it decides whether a healthy year returns money to the budget or stays with the insurer. Here is how the four funding models compare on the points that matter to a school: who carries the risk, who keeps the surplus, the data you get back, and the honest downside. Below the matrix is the checklist we run before recommending any change.

## The funding spectrum, model by model

| Model | Who carries the risk | Surplus in a good year | Claims data you get | The honest downside |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Fully insured** | Insurer carries all of it | Insurer keeps it | Little to none; you get a renewal number | None on claims, but you never share in a healthy year |
| **Level-funded** | Shared; stop-loss caps your exposure | Returned to the school as a refund | Real monthly claims data (ER usage, Rx, cost drivers) | Pay more than best case in a bad year, up to the stop-loss ceiling; more compliance; not sold in every state |
| **Group captive** | Pooled across member employers; reinsurance on top | Returned to members as dividends | Deep data; underwritten on your own performance | Variable cost in a bad year up to the reinsurance layer; requires scale and active management |
| **Full self-funding** | Employer carries it directly | Employer keeps all of it | Full data and control | Real volatility; a single catastrophic claim can blow a hole in a smaller budget |

The two middle models, level-funded and group captive, are the ones built for the small-to-midsize employer a charter school usually is. A single school may be too small to self-fund alone, but charter schools share a risk profile, which is exactly what makes captive pooling reach a scale none of them could reach solo.

## The funding evaluation checklist

- [ ] Pull your actual claims history, not a generic benchmark, before comparing anything.
- [ ] Confirm your state allows level-funded plans (stop-loss regulation varies by state).
- [ ] Model a level-funded plan against your real claims and quantify the refund in a healthy year.
- [ ] Read the stop-loss contract terms; the plan lives or dies on them.
- [ ] Confirm the stop-loss ceiling so you know your maximum exposure in a bad year before you sign.
- [ ] If size or appetite supports it, evaluate a group captive and its three layers (self-funded, shared, reinsurance).
- [ ] Confirm you will receive monthly claims data and that someone is committed to managing it.
- [ ] Document the modeled downside, not just the upside, for the board record.
