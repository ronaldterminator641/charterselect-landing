**Headline:** Your school had a healthy year. So where did the money go?

---

A charter leader walked me through her benefits renewal a while back. Claims had been light all year. Nobody had a major health event, the staff stayed healthy, the numbers looked good from where she sat. Then the renewal landed with another increase on it, and she asked me the question I hear more than any other.

"If we barely used it, why does it keep going up?"

Here's the honest answer nobody had ever given her. On the plan she was on, a healthy year was never going to come back to her. She paid a fixed premium, the insurer carried the risk, and the insurer kept the surplus. That is how a fully insured plan is built. You can run a clean year, save the carrier real money, and watch every dollar of it stay with the carrier. She'd done everything right and the design of the plan meant she'd never see a cent of it.

What got to me is that her generalist broker had never told her there was another version. Not because it's exotic. Schools her size run on the alternatives right now. It's because renewing the same fully insured plan every year and passing along the increase is easy, and the alternatives ask the broker to actually do some work.

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch, where if you did a job halfway, something broke and you owned it. That's the standard I expected when I got into insurance. A lot of charter schools are not getting it, and benefits is where I see it most.

So here's the plain version of what she'd never been shown.

Health plan funding runs on a spectrum. Fully insured sits at one end: fixed premium, insurer keeps the upside. Full self-funding sits at the other: you pay claims directly and keep the savings, but on its own it carries real volatility, and a single catastrophic claim can blow a hole in a smaller employer's budget. Most individual schools don't have enough covered lives to make that math stable alone.

Between those two ends sit the models actually worth a charter school's attention.

A level-funded plan is the practical first step. You pay a set monthly amount, so you keep the budget predictability you're used to. That payment splits into three parts: a claims fund, the administrative fee, and the premium for stop-loss insurance. Stop-loss is the safety net. It caps your exposure, so if claims run high, the overage gets absorbed and your downside stays limited to what you already budgeted. And the part fully insured never gave her: if your group's claims come in below the funded amount, you get the surplus back as a refund. A healthy year finally pays you instead of the carrier. You also get real monthly claims data, so for the first time you can see what's driving your costs instead of being handed a number with no explanation.

A group captive is the next level of control. It's an insurance company owned by the employers in it. Like-sized employers pool together, self-fund their own routine claims, share a middle layer so one member's rough year is cushioned by the group, and put reinsurance on top for the truly catastrophic claims. Dollars not spent on claims come back to members as dividends instead of staying with an insurer. The charter angle is the one worth sitting with: one school may be too small to do this alone, but charter schools share a risk profile, and banding together reaches a scale none of them could reach solo.

Now the part any honest broker says out loud. These models move some risk onto the school. In a bad claims year you will pay more than your best case, up to the point where stop-loss or reinsurance takes over. That ceiling is defined and known in advance. The tradeoff is a capped downside in exchange for keeping the upside in a good year. Anyone who pitches this as free money is lying to you.
