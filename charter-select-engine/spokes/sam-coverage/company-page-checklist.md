**Headline:** Your abuse and molestation coverage is usually the smallest number on the policy and the largest claim a school can face. Here is how to read it before you ever need it.

Sexual Abuse and Molestation coverage, often called SAM, protects a charter school against one of the most severe claim types it can face: high in dollar value, frequently reported years after the event, and expensive to defend even when an allegation has no merit. On most school programs this exposure is insured as a small sublimit buried inside general liability, sometimes excluded outright, and rarely benchmarked against what these claims actually cost. Here is how the four ways it gets handled compare, and the checklist we run on every renewal.

## How SAM coverage gets handled, and what each version means for you

| How it shows up | What it means | The verdict |
|---|---|---|
| **Tiny sublimit inside general liability** | A small, separate cap for abuse claims sits underneath the large GL limit | The common trap. Headline GL limit reads high; the abuse cap is a fraction of it and can be far below what these claims cost |
| **Outright exclusion** | The GL policy carves abuse and molestation out entirely | The hidden gap. Coverage everyone assumes is there is, in plain reading, absent |
| **Monoline SAM bolt-on** | A separate abuse-only policy from another carrier, stapled onto the program | Red flag, not a fix. A second carrier and second set of terms that must reconcile by hand at the worst possible claim. If it is the only way your broker can cover this, that signals a distressed placement or the wrong markets |
| **Adequate SAM built into a real school program** | Abuse insured inside the program, at meaningful limits, with broader language and defense built for this claim | The answer. One coherent placement, written by a carrier that insures schools, at limits that match claim reality |

A charter school with reasonable prevention controls should be able to get adequate SAM built into a real program. Being pushed to a monoline form as the solution is a reason to question the placement, not a reason to feel solved.

## What adequate coverage looks like

| Feature | Weak version | Adequate version |
|---|---|---|
| **Limit structure** | Single shared pool one severe claim can drain | Both a per-victim limit and an aggregate limit |
| **Defense costs** | Paid inside the limit, eroding funds available to resolve the claim | Paid outside the limit, so the full limit stays available |
| **Trigger and date** | Default setting, never discussed | Claims-made vs occurrence and any retroactive date reviewed deliberately, given how late these claims surface |
| **Placement** | Exiled to a monoline bolt-on that reconciles by hand | Built into the school program, one coherent set of terms |

## The renewal checklist

- [ ] Locate the abuse and molestation provision and confirm in writing whether it is a sublimit, an exclusion, a monoline bolt-on, or coverage built into the program.
- [ ] Pull the actual abuse limit figure (not the headline GL limit) and read it in plain language.
- [ ] Benchmark that figure against what abuse claims realistically reach, not against the GL limit above it.
- [ ] Confirm whether the coverage carries both a per-victim limit and an aggregate limit.
- [ ] Confirm whether defense costs sit inside or outside the limit.
- [ ] Confirm the trigger basis (claims-made or occurrence) and, if claims-made, review the retroactive date.
- [ ] Confirm whether the abuse coverage lives inside the school program or has been exiled to a separate monoline policy, and if it is monoline, ask why the program will not insure it.
- [ ] Review prevention controls underwriters look for: background and registry checks refreshed on a schedule, a written abuse-prevention policy, recurring documented staff training, a two-adult rule, and clear mandatory-reporting procedures.
- [ ] Document the confirmed structure, limit, and defense treatment for the board record.

## Why this is standard practice for us and rare elsewhere

A generalist broker building off a standard package lets the abuse sublimit come in at whatever the package defaults to, often a low figure, sometimes an exclusion, and never raises it, because raising it means starting a hard conversation the easy-button renewal does not require. And when a generalist does notice, the reflex is often a monoline bolt-on that treats the symptom instead of fixing the placement. We name this exposure plainly and respectfully, read the abuse provisions out loud at every renewal, show the limit against what these claims realistically cost, and place adequate SAM inside a real school program at meaningful limits, treating a monoline policy as a last resort and a warning sign rather than a solution. Written properly, this coverage also tends to make the underlying harm less likely, because the controls underwriters want are the same ones child-safety experts recommend.
