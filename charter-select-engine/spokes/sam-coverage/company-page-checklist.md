**Headline:** Your abuse and molestation coverage is usually the smallest number on the policy and the largest claim a school can face. Here is how to read it before you ever need it.

Sexual Abuse and Molestation coverage, often called SAM, protects a charter school against one of the most severe claim types it can face: high in dollar value, frequently reported years after the event, and expensive to defend even when an allegation has no merit. On most school programs this exposure is insured as a small sublimit buried inside general liability, sometimes excluded outright, and rarely benchmarked against what these claims actually cost. Here is how the three structures compare, and the checklist we run on every renewal.

## How SAM coverage is structured, and what each structure means for you

| Structure | What it means | The risk to the school | What to do |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sublimit inside general liability** | A small, separate cap for abuse claims sits underneath the large GL limit | Headline GL limit reads high; the abuse cap is a fraction of it and can be far below claim reality | Find the actual sublimit, benchmark it, decide whether to raise it or move to standalone |
| **Outright exclusion** | The GL policy carves abuse and molestation out entirely | Coverage everyone assumes is there is, in plain reading, absent | Confirm whether an exclusion exists, then secure standalone or endorsed coverage |
| **Standalone coverage or built endorsement** | Abuse exposure insured on its own terms, meaningful limits, broader language | This is the target structure, not the gap | Confirm limits, defense treatment, and trigger are all adequate |

You need to know which of these you actually have, in writing, before a claim ever tests it.

## What adequate coverage looks like

| Feature | Weak version | Adequate version |
|---|---|---|
| **Limit structure** | Single shared pool one severe claim can drain | Both a per-victim limit and an aggregate limit |
| **Defense costs** | Paid inside the limit, eroding funds available to resolve the claim | Paid outside the limit, so the full limit stays available |
| **Trigger and date** | Default setting, never discussed | Claims-made vs occurrence and any retroactive date reviewed deliberately, given how late these claims surface |

## The renewal checklist

- [ ] Locate the abuse and molestation provision and confirm in writing whether it is a sublimit, an exclusion, or standalone coverage.
- [ ] Pull the actual abuse sublimit figure (not the headline GL limit) and read it in plain language.
- [ ] Benchmark that figure against what abuse claims realistically reach, not against the GL limit above it.
- [ ] Confirm whether the policy carries both a per-victim limit and an aggregate limit.
- [ ] Confirm whether defense costs sit inside or outside the limit.
- [ ] Confirm the trigger basis (claims-made or occurrence) and, if claims-made, review the retroactive date.
- [ ] Review prevention controls underwriters look for: background and registry checks refreshed on a schedule, a written abuse-prevention policy, recurring documented staff training, a two-adult rule, and clear mandatory-reporting procedures.
- [ ] Document the confirmed structure, limit, and defense treatment for the board record.

## Why this is standard practice for us and rare elsewhere

A generalist broker building off a standard package lets the abuse sublimit come in at whatever the package defaults to, often a low figure, sometimes an exclusion, and never raises it, because raising it means starting a hard conversation the easy-button renewal does not require. We name this exposure plainly and respectfully, read the abuse provisions out loud at every renewal, show the sublimit against what these claims realistically cost, and connect the coverage to the school's prevention practices so both get stronger together. Properly written, this coverage also tends to make the underlying harm less likely, because the controls underwriters want are the same ones child-safety experts recommend.
