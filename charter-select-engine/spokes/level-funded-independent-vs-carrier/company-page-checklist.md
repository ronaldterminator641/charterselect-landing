**Headline:** "Level funded" is a label, not a single product. Before you sign one, see what the independent programs do that the packaged carrier products do not.

Two plans can carry the identical phrase on the brochure and run on opposite machinery. Both split your monthly payment three ways (claims fund, admin fee, stop-loss premium) and both sit on the self-funded side of federal law. The difference is what the plan lets you keep, see, and control. Here is the side-by-side on the four places that decision actually gets made, followed by the checklist we run before we put any level-funded plan in front of a charter school.

## Independent level-funded vs. big-carrier level-funded

| Decision point | Independent program (e.g. Gravie, Benecon) | Big-carrier product (e.g. UHC, Cigna, Blues) |
|---|---|---|
| **Claims data access** | Itemized reporting you can read month over month; you own and can act on the cost drivers | Summary reporting formatted the carrier's way; pharmacy spend often carved in and hard to isolate |
| **PBM flexibility** | Unbundled; pharmacy can be carved out and re-shopped on pass-through pricing when the market shifts | Carrier's PBM bundled in by default (Optum Rx, Express Scripts); you wait for the carrier to change it |
| **Surplus return** | Designed around it; Benecon's VERIS advertises 100% surplus return in writing, with separate per-group claim accounts and no commingling | Returned too, but terms vary; often a share of savings paid as an admin-fee credit, and some require renewal to collect |
| **Portability / lock-in** | Built from interchangeable parts; a network or administrator change does not force a full teardown | Tied to that carrier's network and PBM economics; leaving means rebuilding the whole arrangement |

The honest counterweight: national carriers hold the widest provider networks in the country, and one carrier, one bill, one phone number is genuinely simpler to administer. Those advantages are real and a school should weigh them. The point of the matrix is that you weigh them against something, instead of inheriting the product that was easiest to quote.

## The level-funded evaluation checklist

- [ ] Confirm whether more than one level-funded structure was quoted, at least one independent program alongside any carrier product.
- [ ] Verify the plan design is built around the care your staff actually uses (first-dollar coverage on routine office visits, labs, generics, mental health, urgent care), not just a funding wrapper on a standard deductible plan.
- [ ] Confirm the claims reporting is itemized and usable, with pharmacy spend visible separately from medical.
- [ ] Confirm whether the pharmacy benefit can be carved out and re-shopped, and whether pricing is pass-through (clear fee, rebates passed back).
- [ ] Get the surplus-return terms in writing: what percentage, paid as cash or admin credit, and whether collecting it requires renewal.
- [ ] Verify claim funds are held per group with no commingling, so a healthy year is yours.
- [ ] Check ERISA treatment of surplus where employee contributions funded part of the premium (a slice may be plan assets owed back to participants, not free budget).
- [ ] Confirm the provider network actually covers your staff's geography before trusting an independent program's savings.
- [ ] Document the side-by-side comparison for the board record.

## Why this is standard practice for us and rare elsewhere

A generalist broker sells the carrier's packaged product because the quoting portal is familiar and the proposal arrives prefilled. Putting an independent program next to it is hours of work: knowing the administrators, reading a stop-loss contract he was not handed, and comparing pharmacy and surplus terms line by line. We do that work on every benefits placement, because for a charter staff with no HR department to absorb the difference, the structure underneath the label is where the money and the control actually live.
