**Headline:** Two plans on my desk. Same name on both brochures. One handed the school its money back. The other sent a number once a year and called it a day.

---

A charter leader called me last spring, pretty pleased with herself. Her broker had brought her a level-funded plan. She'd heard the term, knew it was supposed to be smarter than the old fully insured setup, and she signed. Good instinct. I told her so.

Then I asked her the second question. Which level-funded plan? She went quiet. There was only one on the table.

That's the part nobody warns school leaders about. "Level funded" is a label, not a machine. Two plans can carry that exact phrase on the cover and run on opposite logic underneath. One is engineered so your teacher pays nothing at the pharmacy counter for the routine stuff, hands you your claims data in a form you can actually read, lets you re-shop the pharmacy when the market moves, and returns every unused dollar from a healthy year. The other folds all of that inside one big carrier and mails you a settlement number twelve months later with no map of how it got there.

Most charter leaders only ever get shown the second one.

Here's the mechanic in plain English. Every level-funded plan splits your monthly payment three ways: a claims fund, an admin fee, and stop-loss premium that caps your downside if claims run hot. That structure is identical no matter who builds it. The fight is over who builds it and what they let you keep, see, and control while they do.

When a generalist broker says level funded, he almost always means a packaged product from one of the big medical carriers. Real plans, ACA-compliant, fine work. But they're a funding wrapper around a standard deductible-and-copay design, the pharmacy benefit manager comes welded in, the claims reporting arrives formatted the carrier's way, and the surplus terms often pay you back as an admin credit you only collect if you renew.

The other machine is an independent program. Gravie and Benecon are two names worth knowing. Same three-part funding, same stop-loss net, but assembled from parts you can inspect and swap. Gravie's Comfort plan throws out deductibles and copays on roughly 85 percent of common care, office visits, labs, generics, mental health, urgent care, paid first dollar, so your staff uses the plan early instead of waiting until a small problem turns expensive. Benecon's VERIS consortium keeps each group's claim fund separate, no commingling, and advertises 100 percent surplus return in writing. A healthy year is fully yours, on purpose.

I'll be fair, because pretending there are no tradeoffs is how brokers lie. The big carriers earn their scale honestly. They have the widest networks in the country, and one bill with one phone number is genuinely simpler for a small school office. Those things are worth something. But in my experience placing these plans, the independents take the cake for a charter school often enough that they should never be left off the table.

So why did that leader only see one? Because the carrier box is the easy one. The broker has a quoting portal he already knows and a rep who feeds him the proposal. Showing an independent program is hours of work, reading stop-loss contracts and comparing pharmacy terms line by line, on an account he already thinks is too small. So he shows the box, calls it level funding, and lets the school assume that's the whole category.

It isn't. It's the easy corner of it.

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch, where halfway work got somebody hurt. That's the standard I expected when I got into insurance, and it's why Charter Select puts the independent programs and the carrier products on the same table, with the plan design, the data access, the pharmacy economics, and the surplus terms spelled out.

Or did one carrier's product show up alone, dressed as the entire option set?
