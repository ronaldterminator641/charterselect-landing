# LinkedIn Personal Newsletter: The Toolbox You Already Paid For

*Spoke type: Personal profile newsletter (Aaron's voice). Goal: human story + compressed insight, driving to the full white paper. Post from Aaron's personal LinkedIn. ~750 words.*

---

**Headline:** Two schools, same premium. One spent $600 on a problem the other one solved for free with a number it already had.

---

Last year I watched two charter schools handle the same headache two completely different ways.

Both needed to review a tricky termination before they pulled the trigger. One school called an outside HR consultant, paid around six hundred dollars, and got an answer a few days later. The other school dialed an employment-law hotline that came bundled into its insurance policy, talked to an attorney for forty minutes, and paid nothing extra. The hotline was already inside the premium they paid every year.

Same money going in. One school used what it bought. The other paid for it and let it sit in a drawer it never knew was there.

That second school is the one that bothers me, because there was nothing special about the first. They both had the tool. One of them had just never been shown the door.

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch, where you finished the job and you handed somebody the whole thing, not the parts that were easy to hand over. That stuck with me. So when I started seeing how often schools pay for services nobody ever turns on, it landed wrong.

Here is the mechanic, in plain English.

Insurance programs built for schools and nonprofits are rarely just a stack of coverage. The carriers that specialize in this space know their accounts are short on staff and long on risk, so they bundle in services. Risk-management portals. Employment-law and HR hotlines. Online training libraries with completion tracking. Background-screening discounts. Free loss-control site visits. Employee assistance programs. Telemedicine. Cyber breach-response hotlines, the number you call in the first chaotic hour of a suspected breach. These are real, they are useful, and a chunk of their cost is already baked into the premium on your invoice.

You funded all of it. The only question is whether anyone ever walked you through it.

Most school leaders I talk to have no idea what is included with their program. Not because they are careless. Because the policy got placed, the binder got emailed, and the value-adds went unmentioned after the proposal. The login credentials died in somebody's inbox. The hotline number never made it onto a sheet anyone could find at nine on a Friday night. The staff never heard the EAP existed.

This is where the generalist broker shows up, or rather does not. A broker who treats a charter school like a small commodity account sells the perks exactly once, in the proposal, as bullet points that make the program look richer. Then they move on. Activating a portal, circulating hotline numbers, scheduling the site visit, telling staff about the EAP, none of that pays the broker anything. Onboarding a small account is time they would rather spend chasing a bigger one. So the perks sit there, funded and dormant, until your renewal recycles the same unread bullets into next year's quote.

The fix is not complicated. It is an afternoon of work nobody bothered to do:

- Ask your broker or carrier for a written inventory of every value-added service on every policy you hold.
- For each portal and training tool, get the live login, the credentials, and the name of who holds the master account. Reset anything tied to a staffer who left.
- Put every hotline number on one sheet and give it to the people who might dial in a crisis.
- Run the screening-discount math against what you pay retail now.
- Request the loss-control visit on purpose instead of waiting to be offered it.
- Promote the staff-facing perks at a meeting and at open enrollment.
- Re-audit at renewal, because programs quietly add and drop services.

That is what onboarding the thing you already bought looks like. The reason it rarely happens is that someone decided your school was too small to walk through the box. That is most of why Charter Select only works with charter schools and hands you the inventory before anyone asks for it.

So here is the one question worth sitting with. Picture your last insurance proposal, the one with the bullets listing everything the program "includes." Of all those services, how many have you actually logged into, called, scheduled, or told your staff about in the past year? And if the honest answer is closer to none than to all, was that because the services had no value, or because nobody ever opened the box and showed you what you already paid for?

Forward this to the school leader who needs it. The afternoon it takes to ask is the only cost of finding out.
