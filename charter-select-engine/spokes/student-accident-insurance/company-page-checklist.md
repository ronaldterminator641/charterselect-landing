**Headline:** Most student injuries involve no negligence, which means your liability policy will not pay them. Here is the coverage that does, and how to confirm you actually have it.

When a student gets hurt at school, two very different policies could be in play, and only one of them responds when nobody was at fault. General liability is fault-based and pays the injured party only if the school is found legally negligent. Student accident insurance is no-fault and pays toward the student's own medical bills because the injury happened. Since the majority of school injuries carry no negligence, the second policy is the one that keeps a family whole and keeps an unpaid medical bill from turning into a lawsuit. Here is how the two compare, the structures student accident coverage comes in, and the checklist we run on every renewal.

## Liability vs. student accident: who pays when a kid gets hurt

| Dimension | General Liability | Student Accident |
|---|---|---|
| **Coverage type** | Third-party | First-party |
| **Trigger** | School found legally negligent | Injury in a covered activity, no fault required |
| **Who gets paid** | The injured party, on the school's behalf | The student's own medical bills |
| **Routine no-fault injury** | Does not respond | Pays toward the bills |
| **Net effect** | Defends a claim once it starts | Heads off the claim before it starts |

## The structures, matched to what your school runs

| Structure | What it covers | Best fit |
|---|---|---|
| **Blanket** | Every enrolled student during school hours and school-sponsored activities | The clean base layer; an answer the moment any student is hurt |
| **Interscholastic athletics** | The heaviest injury exposure, written with a catastrophic injury limit | Any school fielding competitive teams, especially higher-contact sports |
| **Voluntary** | Offered to parents to buy directly, often low cost | A supplement, not a substitute for a blanket plan |

The one number that decides whether the athletics plan is real or decorative is the catastrophic injury limit. The one term that decides how much any plan does is whether it pays **primary** (from the first dollar) or **excess** (only after the family's own health plan pays). An excess plan does nothing for a family with no health insurance to go first.

## The renewal checklist

- [ ] Confirm whether the program includes a student accident plan at all, in writing.
- [ ] Identify the structure in force (blanket, athletics, voluntary, or a combination).
- [ ] Confirm whether the plan pays primary or excess, and whether that fits the families you serve.
- [ ] Pull the catastrophic injury limit on the athletics plan and confirm it is adequate for your contact level.
- [ ] Map the coverage to the activities the school actually runs: athletics, playground, PE, field trips.
- [ ] For a community with uninsured or underinsured families, verify the plan is not excess-only.
- [ ] Document the structure, the primary/excess basis, and the catastrophic limit for the board record.

## Why this is standard practice for us and rare elsewhere

A generalist broker places the package, checks the box on general liability, and never raises student accident, so the gap rides along invisibly until a student gets hurt and a family asks who is paying. We treat student injuries as the routine event they are, we map the coverage to the activities the school runs, and we confirm the primary/excess basis and the catastrophic limit in writing every renewal, because the cost of skipping it shows up as a lawsuit the school could have prevented.
