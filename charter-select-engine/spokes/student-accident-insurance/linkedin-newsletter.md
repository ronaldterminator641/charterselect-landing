**Headline:** A seventh grader broke his wrist in gym class. Nobody did anything wrong. The school still got sued.

---

Picture a normal Tuesday. A seventh grader is running a standard PE drill, jumps, comes down at a bad angle, and snaps his wrist. No broken equipment. No coach looking at his phone. Just a kid landing wrong the way kids have been landing wrong since the first gym floor got laid down.

The family files on their own health plan. It runs through a deductible and leaves them a few thousand dollars in bills. Then the questions start at the kitchen table. Why are we paying for something that happened at the school. Weren't they supposed to be watching him. Three weeks later a demand letter shows up with the school's name on it.

Nobody was careless, and the school is now the defendant.

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch, where a job done halfway meant somebody got hurt or something broke, and you owned the outcome either way. That is the lens I bring to insurance. So when I watch a family go to court over a routine playground injury because nobody handled the bill, it lands as a failure that was completely preventable.

Here is the mechanic, in plain English.

Your general liability policy is fault-based. It only pays when a family can prove the school was negligent, a broken railing left for months, a known hazard ignored, supervision that wasn't there. The trigger is the word negligence. And most school injuries have no negligence in them at all. Sports, playground, PE, the ordinary collisions of childhood. When a kid gets hurt and nobody was careless, the liability carrier looks at the claim and correctly says the school isn't legally liable, so there is nothing to pay. The policy did its job. It was never built for this.

So the bill lands on the family. And a family staring at a bill they didn't budget for, for an injury that happened on your watch, does not care about the legal definition of negligence. They care that their kid got hurt at your school and they are out of pocket. That is where the lawsuit comes from. Not because the school was careless, but because a lawsuit is the only lever a frustrated family has left to get those bills covered.

Student accident insurance is the coverage built for exactly this. It is first-party, no-fault medical coverage. Strip the jargon and it means a covered student who gets hurt in a covered activity has their medical expenses paid toward, regardless of who was at fault. No blame. No negligence to prove. A kid got hurt at school, the plan pays toward the bills.

That no-fault piece is the whole point. It keeps a family whole after an injury that was genuinely nobody's fault, and a family whose bills got handled rarely goes looking for a lawyer. The coverage that pays the bill on the front end is usually the same coverage that prevents the liability claim on the back end. Handle the medical expense early, and the demand letter often never gets written.

A couple of details decide how much the plan actually does. On the athletics side, the number that matters most is the catastrophic injury limit, because a serious head or spinal injury can run into the hundreds of thousands of dollars over a lifetime of care. And every plan is either primary or excess. A primary plan pays first, from the first dollar. An excess plan pays only after the family's own health insurance has gone, which does nothing for a family that has no health insurance to go first.

Here is the honest reason most charter schools have a gap here. A generalist broker placed the package, checked the box on general liability, and never raised student accident at all. It wasn't on the renewal. It wasn't in the conversation. The easy-button renewal rolls the same package forward every year and the missing coverage stays invisible until a kid gets hurt. Raising this isn't upselling. It is doing the part of the job a school assumed was already being done. That is most of why Charter Select works only with charter schools and looks at whether the coverage actually matches the activities your kids are doing.
