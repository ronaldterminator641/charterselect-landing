**Headline:** Lending your gym, field, or auditorium to an outside group? Confirm these four pieces before you hand over a key, or the loss from their activity lands on your school's policy.

When a charter school lets an outside group use its space (a sports league, a church, a camp, a festival vendor), the goal is risk transfer: the risk of their activity should ride home on their policy, not stay parked on yours. Four pieces make that work, and they only work together. The single most common failure is treating a certificate of insurance as if it gives the school coverage. It does not. Here is how the pieces compare, and the checklist we run before any outside use is granted access.

## The four pieces of facility-use risk transfer

| Piece | What it actually does | What it does NOT do | Who provides it |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Facility use agreement** | Defines who, when, for what, and on what terms; houses the other protections | Nothing on its own without the insurance pieces inside it | The school issues; the user signs |
| **Certificate of insurance (COI)** | Proves the outside group carries its own general liability, with limits and dates | Gives the school zero rights under the user's policy; it is evidence, not coverage | The user's insurer / agent |
| **Additional insured by endorsement** | Makes the school a protected party on the user's policy; their insurer defends and pays first for claims from their use | Does not exist just because the certificate's description box says so | The user's insurer (policy endorsement) |
| **Hold-harmless / indemnification clause** | User contractually agrees to defend and indemnify the school for losses from their use | Not a substitute for insurance; it is the legal backbone behind it | Written into the agreement |

The certificate tells you they have insurance. The endorsement is what puts that insurance to work for the school. Confirm the endorsement, not just the certificate that references it.

## The facility-use checklist (run before access is granted)

- [ ] Signed facility use agreement for the specific dates and activity. No agreement, no access.
- [ ] Current certificate of insurance showing general liability, with limits the school set in advance, and dates that actually cover the use. An expired COI is the same as no COI.
- [ ] Additional insured status naming the school, confirmed by endorsement, not just a line typed into the certificate. Non-negotiable for sports leagues and camps.
- [ ] Hold-harmless and indemnification clause in the agreement, written so the user defends and indemnifies the school for losses arising from their use.
- [ ] Scope and limits matched to the activity. A chess club is not a tackle football camp; require coverage that fits the risk in front of you.
- [ ] A named, responsible contact for the using organization, on the agreement, accountable for their people.
- [ ] A file someone actually keeps: agreement, COI, and endorsement stored together, refreshed when a group returns next season.
- [ ] When the school is the guest (offsite practice, rented hall), the school issues the same documents to the host promptly, so programs never stall at someone else's front desk.

## Why this is standard practice for us and rare elsewhere

A generalist broker processes the renewal and never asks who uses the building when students are not in it. So the gym keeps getting lent on a handshake and the school's liability policy quietly subsidizes every outside group. We ask that question up front, build the facility use process, and confirm the endorsement (not just the certificate) every time, because the loss from an outside group chases the building, and the building is the school.
