# LinkedIn Personal Newsletter: Facility Use Agreements

*Spoke type: Personal profile newsletter (Aaron's voice). Goal: human story + compressed insight, driving to the full white paper. Post from Aaron's personal LinkedIn. ~750 words.*

---

**Headline:** A youth league rented the gym for one Saturday. The injured kid's lawyer went straight for the school's policy.

---

A travel basketball league rents a school gym for a weekend tournament. Forty kids, a few hundred parents in the bleachers, referees nobody at the school has ever met. Routine. The kind of thing a good school says yes to without thinking twice.

Third game of the day, a player chases a loose ball, hits the wall padding wrong, and breaks a wrist that does not heal right. The family hires a lawyer. The lawyer sues everyone with a name on the building, and the school owns the building, so the school gets named.

Here is the part that catches leaders off guard. The league had its own insurance. Everyone assumed that meant the league's insurance would handle it. So why did the school's carrier open the claim file?

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch, where if you did a job halfway, somebody got hurt or something broke, and you owned it. That is the standard I expected when I got into insurance. It is not the standard a lot of charter schools are getting, and lending out the building is one of the clearest places I see it fail.

Let me give you the mechanic in plain English.

When an outside group uses your space, you want the risk of their activity to ride home with them, not stay parked on your policy. Four pieces make that happen, and they only work together. A signed facility use agreement that says who, when, and on what terms. A current certificate of insurance proving the group carries its own general liability. Additional insured status naming your school on their policy. And a hold-harmless clause inside the agreement, where the group agrees to defend and cover the school for losses from their use.

Skip any one and the structure leaks. Most schools have one or two, usually the wrong ones.

The piece almost everyone gets backward is the certificate. A certificate of insurance proves the outside group has a policy. That is all it does. It is a snapshot, a piece of evidence. It gives your school no rights under their policy. If a COI is all you are holding when a claim hits, you are a bystander looking at proof that someone else has coverage you cannot actually use.

Additional insured status is a different animal. When the user's policy is endorsed to name your school, your school becomes a protected party on their policy for claims arising out of their use of your space. Their insurer defends and pays first, on their dime. Their policy, their limits, their lawyers, ahead of yours. And here is where schools get hurt: that status comes from an endorsement on the user's policy, not from a line typed into the certificate description box. The certificate may reference it. The certificate is not it. When you can, ask to see the endorsement, not just the paper that claims it exists.

The certificate tells you they have insurance. The endorsement is what puts that insurance to work for you.

This lands harder on schools than on a business lending a conference room, for two reasons. The activities are higher-energy and involve kids: sports, camps, performances, festivals. And schools say yes a lot, because being a community hub is part of the mission. That generosity is good. Without the paperwork behind it, your liability policy is quietly subsidizing every outside group that walks through the door.

So why does the gap exist? Walk into most schools and ask who reviews the insurance on the groups using the building. The honest answer is usually nobody. That is not the school's failure. It is the broker's. A broker who knew charter schools would have asked, years ago, a simple question: who uses your building when students are not in it? Then handed you a facility use process and a checklist so the answer was handled every time, not improvised by whoever was at the front desk. The generalist does not ask, because asking means understanding how your building actually gets used, and that is more work than processing a renewal. That is most of why Charter Select only works with charter schools.

If you do one thing with this, do this. Think about the last outside group that used your building. The league, the church, the camp, the vendor in the parking lot.

Did your school sign an agreement with them, hold a current certificate, and get named as an additional insured on their policy by endorsement? Or did someone just hand over a key because the group seemed legitimate and the calendar said the space was open?

If you cannot answer that with confidence, you do not have a coverage problem yet. You have a question nobody has checked.

Forward this to the school leader who needs it. The check is cheap. The Saturday it goes wrong without it is not.
