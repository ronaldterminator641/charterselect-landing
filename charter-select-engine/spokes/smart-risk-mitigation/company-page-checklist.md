**Headline:** Water is the most frequent and most preventable property loss at schools. Here is what to monitor, where to put it, and what your carrier may pay you to install it.

Most six-figure water losses at schools happen the same way: a small failure runs unnoticed into an empty building over a weekend or a break. The defect is rarely the story. Time to discovery is. Connected sensors collapse that variable to near zero, and a growing number of carriers reward the install with credits or loss-control support. Here is how the three sensor types map to the exposures they cover, followed by the checklist we run with every school.

## The three sensor types and where each one earns its keep

| Sensor type | What it catches | Where it goes | Pairs with |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Leak / water sensor with automatic shutoff** | Active leaks and abnormal continuous flow; the shutoff closes the main or zone on its own | Under sinks, behind water heaters, at the boiler, base of HVAC air handlers, server and electrical closets | Auto shutoff valve on the main or high-stakes zone |
| **Temperature / freeze sensor** | A space dropping toward freezing before a pipe can split | Mechanical rooms, attics, exterior wall cavities, any unheated space with pipe runs | A response plan to add heat or shut off before the freeze |
| **Humidity sensor** | The slow leak with no dramatic burst: roof leaks, hidden pipe weeps, HVAC trouble | Under the roof and anywhere a slow leak would hide before pooling | Early facilities inspection on a rising-humidity alert |

Detection without response is theater. Every sensor above is only as good as what happens in the ninety seconds after it trips, which is why the response plan column is not optional.

## The mitigation checklist

- [ ] Walk the building with a facilities lead and map where water lives and where a leak would be most expensive (mechanical room, water heater, server and electrical closets, restrooms, HVAC air handlers).
- [ ] Install leak sensors at the high-volume failure points first; add automatic shutoff on the main or the highest-stakes zones.
- [ ] Place freeze sensors in every unheated or poorly heated space with a pipe run.
- [ ] Place humidity sensors under the roof and in normally dry spaces where a slow leak would hide.
- [ ] Build the response plan before buying devices: alerts route to multiple reachable people (facilities lead plus a backup), each knows where the shutoff is and who to call, and escalation is defined.
- [ ] Test the alert chain on a weekend, not just a weekday, to confirm someone actually receives and acts on it.
- [ ] Ask the carrier, in writing, what they currently offer for monitored leak detection or auto shutoff: premium credits, subsidized devices, and risk-engineering or loss-control visits.
- [ ] Schedule the carrier's loss-control walkthrough if one is available, and document what qualifies the school for a credit.
- [ ] Re-confirm available mitigation credits at every renewal, since carrier programs change.

## Why this is standard practice for us and rare elsewhere

A generalist broker treats insurance as a reactive product. Loss, claim, renewal, repeat. Mitigation programs and carrier loss-control resources never surface, because finding them means reading the carrier's current offerings and walking your building, and a small account gets priced as not worth the effort. We renew early, ask the carrier what mitigation it rewards right now, and help stand up the sensors and the response plan, because the cheapest claim a school will ever have is the one that never happens.
