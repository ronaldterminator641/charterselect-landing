**Headline:** A supply line let go on a Friday night. It ran for sixty hours into an empty building. Nobody was there to hear it.

---

A custodian opened a school the Tuesday after a long weekend and found the first floor under an inch of water.

A supply line behind a second-floor utility sink had failed Friday night. It ran all weekend into a locked, empty building. Drywall, flooring, two classrooms of materials, the server closet directly below. By the time anyone walked in, the small leak had become remediation crews, mold testing, a displaced wing, and a number with the comma in the wrong place.

The hard part to swallow is that nobody did anything wrong. The doors were checked. The alarm was set. The building was locked up tight. The water just had a clear weekend to work, and no one was there to catch it.

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch, where a slow drip you ignored on Friday was a real problem by Monday, and you owned the cleanup. That stuck with me. So when I see a six-figure loss that a fifty-dollar sensor would have stopped, it bothers me more than it probably should.

Here is the mechanic, in plain English.

When school leaders picture property risk, they picture fire or a storm. Those are real, and they announce themselves loudly. Water does not announce anything. Across school property claims, water damage is consistently among the most frequent and most expensive causes of loss, and most of it is preventable. A burst pipe in a cold snap, a failed water heater, an HVAC line that weeps for days, a roof leak, a restroom valve that gives out over a break. Notice the pattern. The damage almost never happens while the building is full of people who would hear the drip or step in the puddle. It happens overnight, over a weekend, over winter break.

So the leak is not really the problem. The clock is. A pipe that fails at 6 p.m. Friday in an occupied building is a mop and a phone call. The same pipe failing into a building no one will enter until Monday is a catastrophe. Same defect, same water pressure. The only thing that changed is how long it ran before someone shut it off. That single variable, time to discovery, is where the whole loss lives.

It is also the one thing cheap technology can now collapse to almost nothing. Puck-style leak sensors under sinks and near the water heater. A shutoff valve that closes the main on its own when it detects an abnormal flow, without waiting for a human to drive in at 2 a.m. Freeze probes in unheated spaces with pipe runs. Humidity sensors that catch the slow roof leak before water ever pools. None of it is exotic. Most of it sticks in place, runs on a battery, and talks to a hub over Wi-Fi.

One caution, because people skip it. A sensor that fires an alert into an inbox nobody reads on a Sunday has prevented nothing. The device is half the system. The other half is a plan: alerts go to several reachable people, someone knows where the shutoff is and who to call, and the highest-stakes spots get the automatic valve so the response does not depend on a human being awake and nearby.

Here is the part most schools never hear. Your carrier does not want to pay your water claim either. Preventing the loss is far cheaper for them than indemnifying it, which is why a growing number of carriers reward this. Some offer premium credits for monitored leak detection or auto shutoff. Some provide the devices free or subsidized. Some send a real person to walk your building and tell you where your exposures are. The specifics vary by carrier, and I will not quote you a percentage. What stays true everywhere is the direction of the incentive.

So why has nobody mentioned it? Because a generalist broker treats insurance as reactive. Loss happens, claim gets filed, renewal repeats. Finding the credits and explaining them is work, and your account got priced as too small to bother with. The credit sits unused. The loss-control visit never gets scheduled. Then one Tuesday the first floor is underwater and everyone acts surprised.
