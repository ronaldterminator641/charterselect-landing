**Headline:** A property claim is a documentation contest you win before the loss, or you do not win it at all. Here is the readiness most schools discover they are missing the morning after.

When disaster hits a charter school, the carrier does not pay because the building is wrecked. It pays because the school can prove what it owned, what it was worth, the extent of the damage, and that reasonable steps were taken to limit it. The school that hands that proof over in week one gets paid in month one. The school reconstructing records from memory gets a year-long fight. Below is what "ready" actually looks like, and the checklist we build with every school before a storm ever tests it.

## Ready vs. unprepared, item by item

| Readiness element | Prepared school | Unprepared school |
|---|---|---|
| **Asset inventory** | Current, photographed, valued, refreshed yearly | Reconstructed from memory while the wreckage sits in a dumpster |
| **Policy & dec page** | Stored off-site, retrievable in the first hour | Locked inside the taped-off building it was insuring |
| **Deductibles & sublimits** | Known in plain language before the loss | Discovered after the loss, often a percentage-of-value surprise |
| **Contact tree** | One page, named roles, cell numbers, off-site copy | "Someone should call the carrier," nobody does |
| **Mitigation duty** | Documented, receipts kept, damage photographed first | Standing water sits, mold spreads, payment shrinks |
| **Loss log & timeline** | Contemporaneous record from day one, deadlines watched | Fuzzy recollection, missed proof-of-loss window |

You do not need every box perfect to recover well. You do need the foundation built before the alarm goes off, because none of it can be created after the building floods.

## The pre-loss readiness checklist

- [ ] Build a current asset and property inventory: photos, rough age, and value for every significant item or category, serial numbers on the expensive electronics.
- [ ] Walk every room, closet, and storage area; capture a narrated video as a backup record.
- [ ] Refresh the inventory once a year so recent purchases are actually in it.
- [ ] Store a full copy of every policy and the declarations page off-site (cloud drive, email, broker-held folder).
- [ ] Keep the claims phone number and broker's direct line in that same off-site folder.
- [ ] Read and document your deductibles, including any percentage-based wind or named-storm deductible.
- [ ] Identify your top sublimits (flood, water backup, debris removal, equipment categories) and what they cap.
- [ ] Confirm your extra expense and business income terms, the coverage that funds trailers and temporary leases.
- [ ] Write a one-page contact tree: who calls the carrier, who documents, who mitigates, who is the single voice to families and media, who pulls the off-site folder.
- [ ] Confirm the policy's mitigation duty is understood: protect the property from further damage, keep receipts, photograph before cleaning.
- [ ] Start a loss log the day a loss occurs and keep every receipt.
- [ ] Note the prompt-notice and proof-of-loss deadlines, and assign someone to watch the clock.

## Why this is standard practice for us and rare elsewhere

A generalist broker chasing a renewal emails the dec page once and disappears until the next invoice. We build the disaster blueprint with a school on a calm Tuesday, confirm the deductibles and sublimits in plain language, and show up at claim time to push back on a lowball adjustment and keep the proof-of-loss timeline from quietly running out. Presence at the loss is the entire reason a broker exists, and it is the part the cheap ones skip.
