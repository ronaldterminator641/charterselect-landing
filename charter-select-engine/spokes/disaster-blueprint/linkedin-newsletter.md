# LinkedIn Personal Newsletter: The Disaster Blueprint

*Spoke type: Personal profile newsletter (Aaron's voice). Goal: human story + compressed insight, driving to the full white paper. Post from Aaron's personal LinkedIn. ~750 words.*

---

**Headline:** Two schools took the same storm. One reopened in three weeks. The other fought its carrier for a year.

---

Same county. Same week. The same kind of water came through the same kind of roof at two schools a few miles apart.

One was back in session inside a month. The other was still arguing with its insurer the following spring, classes scattered across a borrowed church basement and a couple of trailers, payroll bleeding out, families quietly pulling their kids and not coming back.

Same damage. Same coverage on paper. I keep coming back to that pair because the gap between them had almost nothing to do with the storm. It was decided long before the wind picked up.

People assume the fast recovery is about the policy, that the lucky school just bought better coverage. Sometimes that is true. More often both schools had perfectly adequate policies, and only one was ready to use it.

Here is the part nobody explains to a school leader until it is too late. A property claim is a documentation contest. The carrier does not cut a check because your building is wrecked. It pays because you can prove what you had, prove what it was worth, prove the damage, and prove you did your part to limit it. The school that hands all of that over in week one gets paid in month one. The school rebuilding its records from memory while the mold spreads gets a fight.

And you build the winning side of that contest before the loss, or you do not build it at all. After the alarm goes off is too late to start photographing equipment that is already underwater.

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch. If you did a job halfway out there, somebody got hurt or something broke, and you owned it. That is the standard I expected when I got into insurance. It is not the standard a lot of charter schools are getting, and the disaster blueprint is the clearest example I know.

None of it is expensive. Most of it is an afternoon of work you could start this week.

A current inventory of what the school owns, with photos and rough values. Walk every room with your phone, closets and storage included, narrate a video as you go, and redo it once a year so it actually includes the forty Chromebooks you bought last fall. When a fire takes a wing, the adjuster asks what was in it. "A lot of expensive stuff" is not a number they can pay against. A dated photo and a value is.

Your policy and declarations page stored somewhere the disaster cannot reach. Not the filing cabinet inside the building you are insuring. A cloud drive, an email to yourself, a folder your broker holds. Put the claims line and your broker's cell in there too, so you are not hunting for a number on a sticky note inside a taped-off office.

Your deductibles and sublimits, read and understood before a storm tests them. Wind and named-storm deductibles are often a percentage of the building's value, not a flat dollar figure, and that number surprises people. Sublimits quietly cap flood, water backup, and debris removal far below your total limit. If you cannot explain your wind deductible and your top three sublimits in plain language right now, that is this week's assignment.

A one-page contact tree. Who calls the carrier, who shoots the photos before anything gets touched, who arranges the board-up and water extraction, who is the single voice to families and press. Names and cell numbers. A loss happens on a Saturday or at 2 a.m., not during a calm staff meeting.

Most of this never gets mentioned to a school because the broker who sold the policy treats the account like a renewal to process, not a client to protect. They emailed the dec page once, two years ago, buried in a PDF nobody opened. Then the loss comes, and they are hard to reach, slow to call back, and nowhere near the room where the adjuster decides what your damage is worth. Showing up at claim time is the whole reason a broker exists. It is also the moment the cheap, absent ones disappear.

I wrote the full blueprint up on our site. No jargon, no gate, free to read and free to forward to your board or your business manager: **[LINK TO WHITE PAPER]**

So picture the worst Tuesday. A fire, a flood, a storm that opens the roof, tonight.

Could you, tomorrow morning, hand someone a current inventory of what the building held, a copy of your policy from outside the building, and a one-page plan of who does what? Or would the honest answer be that you would be starting all of it from scratch, in the wreckage, on the worst day your school has had?

Better to close that gap on a quiet afternoon than discover it the morning after.
