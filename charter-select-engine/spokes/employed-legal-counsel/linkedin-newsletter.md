**Headline:** Your network hired its own lawyer. The day you signed that offer letter, you created a risk none of your policies were built to catch.

---

A network leader walked me through his growth last year, and he was rightly proud of it. Three campuses. A real central office. And a recent hire he was especially happy about: an in-house attorney. No more renting legal help by the hour. His own counsel now read the charter, reviewed the contracts, and sat with the board. Smart, by every measure.

I asked him one question. When you brought that lawyer on staff, did anyone look at whether the legal advice now coming from inside your building was actually covered?

He went quiet. Then he said what most leaders say. "That's what we have insurance for, right?"

Here is the part that should bother you, because it bothered me. He was paying for three policies that each had a reason to say no to that exact claim.

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch, where doing a job halfway meant somebody got hurt or something broke, and you owned it. That is the standard I expected when I got into insurance. It is not the standard a lot of growing charter networks are getting, and in-house counsel is one of the cleanest examples I know.

Let me give you the mechanic in plain English.

For years, your legal work came from outside firms. If one of them blew it, that was their malpractice problem, sitting on their professional liability policy. The risk lived somewhere else. The day a lawyer becomes your employee, that risk walks through your front door and sits down at your conference table. The professional exposure that used to belong to an outside firm now belongs to you.

So you reach for what you already carry, and you find three closed doors.

Your general liability policy is the workhorse for slip-and-falls and ordinary property damage. Every standard form also carries a professional services exclusion, and legal malpractice is the textbook example of what it carves out. When a claim says your lawyer's advice was negligent, that policy is built to step back.

Your D&O policy protects your board and your leadership for how they govern. People assume it covers the general counsel too. The trouble is the kind of claim. A D&O form responds when leaders are sued for management decisions, not for practicing law, and many of these policies carry their own professional services exclusion. Worse, the associates and staff attorneys below your general counsel often do not count as covered executives at all.

Then the policy that sounds like the obvious fix: a lawyers professional liability policy, the malpractice coverage law firms buy. It is built for a firm serving outside clients who hired it, paid it, and can sue it. Your in-house counsel has one client, the employer who signs the paycheck. Different relationship. The law-firm form was never written for it.

So the risk came in the door, and all three doors it tried were the wrong one.

There is a line built for precisely this gap. It is called Employed Lawyers professional liability. Its whole job is to cover the attorneys you employ for claims arising out of the legal services they provide to you. It responds where the others decline. It typically covers the general counsel and the legal team beneath them, including the staff attorneys a D&O form tends to skip, and it usually advances defense costs, which matters because these allegations get expensive long before anyone decides whether the claim has merit.

This is not obscure coverage. Major carriers write it. Any broker who treated your network as a risk to understand, rather than a renewal to process, would have flagged it the moment you mentioned hiring an attorney. Most never do. They run the easy-button renewal, roll the same package forward, and never ask the one question that matters after you scale: what changed about your operation since last year? That is the entire job, and it went undone. That is most of why Charter Select only works with charter schools and networks, and reads the whole operation, not just the address.

When your network hired its own attorney, did anyone look at whether the legal advice now coming from inside your organization was actually covered? Or did the renewal roll forward unchanged, while a brand-new professional exposure quietly took a seat at your conference table?

If you cannot answer that with confidence, you do not have a claim problem yet. You have a question nobody checked.
