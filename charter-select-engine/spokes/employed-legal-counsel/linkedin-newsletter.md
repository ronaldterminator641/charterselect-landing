# LinkedIn Personal Newsletter: Employed Lawyers Coverage

*Spoke type: Personal profile newsletter (Aaron's voice). Goal: human story + compressed insight, driving to the full white paper. Post from Aaron's personal LinkedIn. ~750 words.*

**Headline:** You hired in-house counsel. Your broker tried to sell you a whole new policy. That was the wrong move.

---

A network leader called me last year, half proud and half annoyed. Proud because his network had grown enough to bring an attorney on staff. No more renting legal help by the hour. His own counsel now read the charter, reviewed the contracts, and sat with the board. Annoyed because the moment he mentioned the hire to his broker, the broker came back with a quote for a brand new standalone policy.

He asked me if he needed to buy it. I asked him one question back. Did the broker read what you already own before they quoted you something new?

He went quiet. He had no idea, because the broker never mentioned the program already on his desk.

Here is the part that should bother you, because it bothered me. Reaching for a separate policy the second you hear the word "lawyer" is the tell. It usually means the person handling your account did not stop to think about what your attorney actually does, or how the program you already carry is built to cover it.

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch, where doing a job halfway meant somebody got hurt or something broke, and you owned it. That is the standard I expected when I got into insurance. It is not the standard a lot of growing charter networks are getting, and this one is a clean example.

Let me give you the mechanic in plain English.

Picture what your general counsel actually does. They review vendor agreements, interpret the charter, walk the board through a governance question, check a termination before it happens. That is advisory work. Legal judgment applied to your own operation, for one client, the employer who signs the paycheck. Your attorney is not running a litigation practice. They do not have a roster of outside clients who hired them, paid them, and can turn around and sue them for malpractice. The exposure they create is the narrower risk of an employee giving your organization professional advice, not the risk a law firm carries.

That distinction is the whole game, and it is exactly the distinction a generalist skips.

Now, the underlying concern is legitimate, so give it its due. Two of the policies you already carry were not built to answer a claim about your lawyer's advice. Your general liability policy carries a professional services exclusion, and legal malpractice is the textbook thing it carves out. A plain, off-the-shelf nonprofit D&O form sometimes covers the general counsel, but the staff attorneys under them frequently do not show up in the grant of coverage at all. So the exposure is real. The mistake is in the fix.

A standalone Employed Lawyers policy is a real product, built for organizations that run a substantial in-house legal department, a team practicing law at volume as a core function. Your charter network is not that profile. Your counsel advises. Buying a separate policy engineered for a large practicing department means a second premium, a second renewal, and a second set of terms, all to insure a risk your management liability program is already positioned to pick up.

The carriers that actually write for schools fold employed lawyers coverage right into the definition of the insured on the management liability side, or add it by endorsement for little or nothing. It sits inside the program you already carry. It covers your general counsel and the legal team beneath them for the legal services they provide to you. One program, one renewal, one carrier who understands the account.

That is the point a generalist misses. They hear "we hired a lawyer" and pattern-match to "sell lawyer insurance," because selling a new policy is a transaction they know how to run. Confirming that your existing definition already reaches your attorneys takes reading the form and understanding your operation. One is faster. The other is the job. That is most of why Charter Select only works with charter schools and networks, and starts by reading what you already own.

If you only do one thing with this, do this. Ask whoever handles your insurance one question.

When you told your broker you had brought an attorney on staff, did they confirm that your existing management liability program already covers your lawyer's work, and show you the language that does it? Or did they reach for a separate policy and a separate premium, without ever looking at what you already own?

One of those answers means someone read your program. The other means they saw the word "lawyer" and reached for the checkbook.
