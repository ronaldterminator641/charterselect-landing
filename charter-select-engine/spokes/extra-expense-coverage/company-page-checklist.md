**Headline:** Your property policy rebuilds the building. Two other lines decide whether the school survives the months it takes. Most school policies set them by guess.

When a covered loss takes your building out of service, the rebuild check is the part that works as expected. The part that decides whether you keep students, staff, and funding through the closure lives in two coverages that are usually undersized or skipped: Business Income and Extra Expense. Here is how the pieces compare, and the checklist we run on every property renewal.

## The four coverages that carry a school through a building loss

| Coverage | What it does | Why a charter school can't treat it as an add-on |
|---|---|---|
| **Business Income** | Replaces lost revenue and keeps paying ongoing costs (payroll, lease, utilities) while operations are interrupted | Your revenue is per-pupil funding tied to actual attendance; a casually chosen round number burns through fast |
| **Extra Expense** | Funds the added costs of staying open: leased space, portable classrooms, moving, IT and phone setup, busing to the temporary site | For a school this is often the coverage that saves the institution, because getting students back into a room protects enrollment and funding |
| **Restoration period** | The window the insurer will pay for, running until the property is (or reasonably should be) restored | Tracks a reasonable rebuild timeline, not your real one; large school rebuilds routinely run past a year and past the default window |
| **Extended Business Income** | Keeps coverage running for a defined stretch after you reopen, while you rebuild enrollment | Families who left during the closure come back gradually; this tail covers the gap between reopening and full enrollment |

You need all four sized to a real displacement scenario, not pulled off a shelf. A thin Extra Expense limit and a guessed Business Income number are the two failures we see most.

## The property renewal checklist

- [ ] Confirm the Extra Expense limit is sized to a real relocation, not a token flat number.
- [ ] Model the interim cost month by month: leased space, portables, moving, IT and phone and device setup, busing.
- [ ] Tie the Business Income limit to your actual budget, payroll, fixed costs, and per-pupil funding, not a renewed round number.
- [ ] Stress the Business Income limit against a restoration period long enough for a serious rebuild, not a hopeful one.
- [ ] Pull the restoration period in writing and check it against a realistic school rebuild timeline (permits, design, construction, inspection).
- [ ] Confirm Extended Business Income is present and sized for the time it takes to rebuild enrollment after reopening.
- [ ] Document the modeled displacement scenario and the resulting limits for the board record.

## Why this is standard practice for us and rare elsewhere

A generalist sells the property policy, points at the building limit, and renews it every year without ever asking how the school would keep operating if it couldn't use the building. So Extra Expense stays thin, the Business Income limit stays a guess, and the restoration period stays whatever the carrier defaulted to. We start from the displacement, map how you'd relocate students and how long it would take, size Extra Expense and Business Income to that reality, and check the restoration period against a real rebuild.
