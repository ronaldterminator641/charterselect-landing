**Headline:** The fire didn't close her school. The eight months without a classroom almost did.

---

A pipe let go over a long weekend. By the time anyone walked in on Tuesday, the water had been running for three days and the lower floor was gone. Carpet, drywall, the network closet, half the classrooms.

The school leader I'm thinking of did everything right in that first week. She called her broker, the claim got filed, and the property carrier agreed to rebuild. That part actually worked. A check was coming for the building.

Then she asked the question that mattered more. Where do the kids go on Monday?

The rebuild was going to take eight months. Her families were not going to wait eight months. She needed leased space, portable classrooms, busing to the temporary site, phones and internet and student devices stood up somewhere new, and she needed it inside of two weeks. None of that was a cost she'd ever carried before. All of it landed at once.

She went back to the policy to see what would pay for the scramble. Extra Expense, the coverage built for exactly this, was a small flat number somebody had set years earlier and never touched. It covered maybe a few weeks of what she was about to spend across eight months.

The building was insured. The school almost wasn't.

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch, where a job done halfway meant somebody got hurt or something broke, and you owned it. That's the standard I expected in insurance. A lot of charter schools are not getting it, and this is one of the clearest places it shows.

Here's the mechanic, in plain English.

Your property policy has two parts that decide whether a building loss is a hard year or the end of the school. **Business Income** replaces the revenue you stop bringing in while you can't operate, and keeps covering the costs that don't stop, payroll, the lease, utilities. **Extra Expense** pays the added costs you take on to keep running anyway: the leased annex, the portables, the moving, the rewiring, the busing.

One backfills lost money. The other funds the scramble to stay open. A serious loss triggers both.

For an ordinary business, Business Income is the headline and Extra Expense is a minor add-on. Customers come back when you reopen. That's how most commercial policies get built and sold, and a charter school gets handed that same shape.

Your school does not work that way. Your revenue is per-pupil funding, and it follows students who are actually showing up and being counted. If you can't give a family a classroom, that family doesn't wait. They enroll somewhere else, and the funding walks out with them. It doesn't automatically come back when your doors reopen.

So the real threat of a long closure isn't only the rebuild bill. Every week without a room is a week families decide whether to stay. Lose enough of them and you lose next year's enrollment and the footing the whole school stands on.

That flips the priority. For a school, Extra Expense, the money that gets students back into a room somewhere fast, is often the coverage that saves the institution, because keeping students enrolled is the same thing as protecting your funding.

There's a clock on all of it, too. The restoration period is the window the insurer will pay for, and it tracks how long a rebuild *should* reasonably take, not how long permits and contractors actually drag it out. On a lot of policies that window is shorter than a real school rebuild. If it runs out before your doors are truly back open, the coverage stops carrying you at the worst possible moment.

The fix isn't exotic. It's a number you build instead of guess. You walk through the displacement before it happens: how you'd relocate students, how long it'd realistically take, what every step costs month by month, and how long to win enrollment back. Run that, and the right limits stop being a guess.
