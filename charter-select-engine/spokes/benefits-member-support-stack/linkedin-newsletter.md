**Headline:** I asked a superintendent four questions about her health plan. She went quiet on all four. That silence cost her staff and her budget for six straight years.

---

A charter school superintendent told me she had the best plan on the market. She knew it because her broker had told her so, every spring, for six years running. Same carrier. Same bundle. Premium up a little each time, but that was just the market, she'd been told.

So I asked her four questions. Who negotiated your pharmacy contract. Who picked your network. Who is watching the order of payment for the staff member who turned 65 and kept teaching. Who does a teacher actually call the morning she's handed a cancer diagnosis.

She went quiet on all four. Not because she didn't care. Because nobody had ever built any of it. She'd been sold one product, six times, and told it was a strategy.

That conversation is most of why I do this work the way I do.

Here's the mechanic, in plain English. Most charter schools get handed a finished thing. One carrier, one network, one pharmacy manager, one set of rules, all welded together and sold as a single package. It's easy to buy. It renews itself. And it quietly costs the school more every year while handing staff care that's slower and shallower than it should be.

The schools that break that cycle stop buying a bundle and start assembling a stack. Every piece gets chosen on its own, priced on its own, swapped out when something better shows up. The funding base flips from fully insured to self-funded or level-funded, so a good claims year saves the school money instead of padding a carrier's margin. The pharmacy benefit gets carved out to a transparent, pass-through manager that returns the rebates instead of pocketing them. The plan design anchors on free primary care, because funding the front door of medicine stops the expensive failures downstream. An independent administrator runs the whole thing answering to the school, not to a parent carrier.

That's the cost-control half. It's real, and it's available to a school your size right now.

But here's where almost every build I see falls apart. Once you own the risk, you own things a carrier used to handle quietly, and getting them wrong is expensive. The teacher who's 65 and still working triggers federal Medicare rules about who pays first. Get the order backwards on a self-funded plan and your fund pays claims Medicare should have covered, with real penalties behind it. The employee who leaves and stays on COBRA can load your fund with high-cost claims from someone who no longer works for you. Somebody has to stand guard over both, deliberately, at every renewal.

And then there's the part that's actually about the human being. When a staff member hears the word cancer, does a real person connect her treating oncologist with sub-specialist expertise from a top cancer center, so she gets the right read without uprooting her life? When a teacher gets a baffling bill or a scary referral, is there one number to call and a real guide on the other end, steering her to a high-quality provider and the right site of care? Prices for the same procedure in the same town can differ by 400 percent. A guide saves the member money and saves the fund money in the same motion.

You can build the best stack in the country and waste it if nobody on staff understands what they have. A benefit nobody uses is money you spent for resentment.

So why doesn't your school have all of this? Because assembling a stack and standing up a support layer is a different job than selling a renewal. A generalist who carries restaurants and law firms and your school in the same book shows up once a year with the same bundle and a higher premium, walks you through the increase, and calls it service. Building the real thing is slow, technical, ongoing work. It only happens when someone treats your school as a real client instead of a file to clear off the desk.

Picture the plan your staff have right now, and ask yourself: can you name who built each piece of it, who's watching the Medicare order and the COBRA exposure, and who a teacher actually calls the day she's handed a serious diagnosis? Or did the whole thing just renew itself again while everyone was busy running a school?

If you can't answer that with confidence, you don't have a benefits problem yet. You have a stack nobody assembled.
