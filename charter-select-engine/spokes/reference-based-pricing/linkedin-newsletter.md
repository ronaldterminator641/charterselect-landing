# LinkedIn Personal Newsletter: Reference-Based Pricing

*Spoke type: Personal profile newsletter (Aaron's voice). Goal: human story + compressed insight, driving to the full white paper. Post from Aaron's personal LinkedIn. ~750 words.*

---

**Headline:** A staff member had surgery. The hospital would have taken a fraction of what your plan paid. Nobody ever showed the school the smaller number.

---

A business manager at a charter school walked me through her health plan last year. Costs were up again. The renewal letter blamed claims, blamed the market, blamed everything except the one thing nobody had ever put in front of her.

So I asked her about a single surgery on her plan from the prior year. A real claim, a real staff member, a procedure with a lot of zeros attached. Her plan had paid it, minus the network discount, and everyone moved on.

Then I asked a different question. What would that same hospital have accepted for that same operation if the bill had gone to Medicare instead? Same operating room. Same surgeon. Same supplies. She didn't know. Nobody had ever told her there was another number, and the smaller one still keeps the hospital's lights on.

That gap is the whole story, and most school leaders have never been shown it exists.

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch, where you don't get to bill someone three times for the same job and call it market rate. That's the standard I expected when I got into insurance. It is not the standard most charter schools are getting on the single heaviest line in their health plan: hospital and facility costs.

Here is the mechanic, in plain English.

The plan most schools carry pays hospitals through a PPO network. The network negotiates a discount off the hospital's billed charges, and the plan pays the discounted amount. Sounds like a win. The trap is in the words "billed charges." A hospital's list price lives in a document called the chargemaster, and those prices are not tied to what care costs to deliver. They are set high on purpose. A 40 or 50 percent discount off a number that was inflated to begin with can still land far above what the service is worth. You can negotiate hard against a fake number and still overpay.

Reference-based pricing attacks that at the root. Instead of starting from the inflated list price and discounting down, the plan starts from a transparent benchmark and pays up from there. The benchmark is usually Medicare, which already calculates a defined rate hospitals accept every day. An RBP plan pays a set multiple of that rate, often somewhere around 120 to 200 percent of Medicare depending on the design. Commercial network pricing frequently runs several times Medicare, sometimes 300 percent or more. So you pay the hospital a fair, sustainable price and still pay far less than the network would have. You stop arguing about the discount and start defining the price.

I have to give you the tradeoff straight, because anyone selling this as free money is not being honest. Since an RBP plan pays off a benchmark instead of a signed network contract, a hospital can bill the member for the difference. That is called balance billing, and it is the real risk. A serious program manages it as a core feature, with member advocacy and a legal team that steps in on the employee's behalf when a bill shows up. Reputable administrators report most claims end with no balance bill to the member. Most is not never, and you should distrust anyone who promises never. This also only works on a self-funded chassis, where your plan, not an insurer, decides what it pays per claim.

So why was your school never shown this? Because RBP is more work. More work to design, more work to explain to staff, more work to support when a member calls about a bill. The easy-button renewal is to re-up the same network plan, pass along the increase, and move on. RBP never comes up, not because it wouldn't fit your school, but because it would ask something of the broker. That's the part I can't stomach, and it's most of why Charter Select reads your actual claims before recommending a single change.

If you do one thing with this, do this. Ask whoever handles your benefits one question.

On your largest hospital and facility claims last year, has anyone ever shown you what those services would have cost benchmarked against Medicare, next to what your plan actually paid? If no one has ever put those two numbers side by side, how would you know whether you have a cost problem, or just a broker who never looked?

If they can't answer that, you don't have a verdict yet. You have a question nobody on your account has bothered to ask on your behalf.
