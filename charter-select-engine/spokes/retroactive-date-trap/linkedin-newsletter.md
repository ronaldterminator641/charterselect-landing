# LinkedIn Personal Newsletter: Retroactive Date Trap

*Spoke type: Personal profile newsletter (Aaron's voice). Goal: human story + compressed insight, driving to the full white paper. Post from Aaron's personal LinkedIn. ~750 words.*

---

**Headline:** A single date on her policy quietly erased five years of coverage. Nobody told her until the claim came in.

---

A school leader told me last year that her renewal "looked fine." The premium had even come down a little. Her generalist broker had shopped it, moved carriers, saved her a few thousand dollars, and sent over the paperwork. She signed it the way all of us sign things during a week that's already on fire.

What nobody told her was that the switch had reset one date on her liability policy. Her retroactive date. And resetting it wiped out coverage for years she thought she'd already paid for.

She found out the way no one should. A claim came in for something from years earlier, the kind of claim that takes a long time to surface. She'd been insured the whole time. She still wasn't covered. One line of fine print did that, and it got moved without anyone walking her through what it meant.

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch, where if you did a job halfway, somebody got hurt or something broke, and you owned it. That's the standard I expected when I got into insurance. It is not the standard a lot of charter schools are getting, and this is the clearest example I know.

Here's the mechanic, in plain English.

A lot of the coverage that protects your school where you're most exposed, your board, your leadership, certain employment and abuse exposures, is written "claims-made." A claims-made policy only pays for events that happened on or after one specific date: the retroactive date. Keep that date frozen in place year after year and your protected history keeps stretching back. That's the whole point of staying continuously covered.

When a broker switches you to a new carrier to shave premium, the new carrier's default is to set your retro date to the day the new policy starts. Not your original date. The new one. In one renewal, five years of protected history can collapse to zero, and the paperwork looks completely normal.

For a school, this is worse than for almost anyone. The claims that can end an institution, abuse and molestation in particular, are also the ones most likely to be reported years after the fact. So the trap is aimed squarely at the exposure that bankrupts schools and drags board members' personal assets into a courtroom. It gets triggered by a routine money-saving switch that reads like good news on the invoice.

The fix isn't complicated. It's just work nobody bothered to do:

- Preserve the original retro date when you switch carriers, confirmed in writing.
- Or carry "nose" coverage, where the new carrier covers events back to your old date.
- Or buy "tail" coverage from the carrier you're leaving.
- And read the declarations page every renewal. The retro date lives there. It takes two minutes.

The reason this gets missed isn't that it's hard. It's that the broker decided the school wasn't worth the two minutes. That's the part I can't stomach, and it's most of why Charter Select only works with charter schools and reads the whole policy every single renewal.

If you only do one thing with this, do this: ask whoever handles your insurance one question.

When was the last time anyone actually pulled your claims-made policies and confirmed, in writing, whether the retroactive date moved at your last carrier switch? Or did the renewal just process itself while everyone was busy running a school?

If they can't answer that clearly, you just learned something about both your coverage and your broker.

Forward this to the school leader who needs it. This one's too expensive to find out the hard way.
