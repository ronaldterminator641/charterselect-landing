**Headline:** Two schools filled the same prescription the same month. One was billed a few dollars. The other a few hundred. Same pill.

---

I pulled two charter school plans side by side last year and found something that still bothers me.

Same drug. Same manufacturer. Same dose. Filled in the same month. One plan got billed a few dollars for it. The other got billed a few hundred. Nobody at either school had any idea. Neither principal had ever been shown the difference, and neither one had been told the difference even existed.

The thing that decided the price wasn't the medication. It was a contract sitting behind the pharmacy counter that nobody on either account had ever read.

Every drug benefit you offer runs through a company called a Pharmacy Benefit Manager. A PBM. It builds your covered-drug list, decides what each pharmacy gets paid, and decides what your plan gets charged. The sales pitch is that the PBM is the big buyer with scale, so it gets you better prices than you could get on your own. Sometimes that holds. The problem is how most of these contracts are written, because under the traditional structure the middleman makes more money when your drug spend goes up, not down. SmithRx, a transparent PBM, calls this inverse incentives. The vendor profits when your spending climbs. The incentive points straight at your budget.

Here is how the money actually moves.

There is spread pricing. The PBM pays the pharmacy one price for a drug, charges your plan a higher price, and keeps the difference. You never see it, because the contract shows you one number and hides the one underneath it. Fifty cents on a generic sounds like nothing. Run it across a staff of a hundred people filling maintenance scripts month after month and it becomes real money that leaves your plan with no line item attached.

There are rebates. Manufacturers pay PBMs to get their drug placed well on your formulary. A traditional PBM that keeps a share of those rebates has a quiet reason to favor the expensive drug over the cheaper one, because the pricier product pays the bigger rebate. So you can end up covering a costlier medication than your people needed, while the discount that justified it never reaches your school.

Then there are the fees you would never find on the invoice. Clawbacks. DIR fees charged to the pharmacy after the sale. Administrative fees advertised around four dollars a member that quietly land north of ten once every surcharge is counted. The whole thing is tuned to maximize what the middleman keeps, not to land your people on the right drug at the lowest real cost.

There is a structurally different way to run this. A transparent, pass-through PBM gets paid one way: a flat, disclosed fee. It charges your plan exactly what the pharmacy was paid, so there is no spread to hide. It passes one hundred percent of rebates back to the plan. And it steers the whole program toward one number, lowest net cost, which is total drug spend minus every rebate, measured per member per month. Chase that number and the formulary drifts toward low-cost generics and biosimilars instead of whatever paid the fattest rebate. The spend tends to fall, because nobody is being pushed toward the expensive option anymore.

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch, where a job done halfway got somebody hurt or broke something, and you owned it. That is the standard I expected when I got into insurance. A bundled PBM that quietly takes the spread, keeps the rebates that were yours, and buries the fees is the opposite of that standard, and it has been running on charter schools for years.

The reason yours probably looks like this comes down to who is on your account. The PBM came stapled inside the carrier's fully insured plan. Your broker handed you that plan, renewed it every year, walked you through the premium hike, and called it service. A generalist carrying restaurants and law firms in the same book has no reason to crack open a pharmacy contract and take it apart. That is most of why Charter Select only works with charter schools and actually does the dig.

If you do one thing with this, do this. Ask whoever runs your benefits a single question. When your plan pays for a prescription, does anyone on your account know the difference between what the pharmacy was paid and what your plan was billed, and can they show you in writing whether your rebates came back to your school or stayed with the middleman?

If the answer is a shrug, you don't have a drug-cost problem. You have a question nobody on your account has bothered to ask.
