# Company Page Spoke: Active Assailant Coverage Decision Checklist

**Headline:** Before you buy a standalone active assailant policy, map it against what your program already covers. A lot of what it advertises may already be in force.

"Active assailant coverage" is a label, not a single product, and it is often sold as the answer without anyone reading the trigger or inventorying what the school already carries. For a well-built charter school program, much of what the standalone advertises already sits in the package, the student accident policy, and the School Leaders management liability program. Below is how those pieces line up against the exposures a violent event creates, and the checklist we run before anyone talks about buying a new policy.

## What already covers each exposure, and what is genuinely left

| Exposure | What in your program often already responds | What is genuinely left to decide |
|---|---|---|
| **Lawsuit against the school and board** (negligent security, failure to protect) | School Leaders management liability program, built to defend the entity and its leaders | Confirm the form covers a negligent-security / failure-to-protect claim. Usually nothing new to buy. |
| **Crisis counseling and trauma therapists** | A well-built school package often funds this | Confirm the limit is sized for a large-scale event, not a routine one. |
| **PR and crisis communications** | Crisis-communications coverage inside the package | Confirm it triggers for a violent event, not only a data or reputational incident. |
| **Student medical expenses** | Student accident policy, written to pay student medical | Confirm limits. Largely covered. |
| **Student death benefits** | Student accident policy | Confirm the benefit amounts against what families would need. |
| **Physical remediation of the space** | Property policy, only if there is qualifying physical damage | Residual. Insure or self-fund. |
| **Enrollment-driven business interruption** | Usually not covered by standard property BI | The real standalone question. Insure or self-fund. |
| **Terrorism-linked event** | A terrorism form, priced separately with its own trigger | A separate lever worth pricing on its own. |

The pattern is consistent. Counseling, PR, student medical and death benefits, and board defense are frequently already owned. What tends to be left after honest subtraction is physical remediation and lost enrollment, and that residual is a risk-tolerance decision, not an automatic purchase.

## The decision checklist

- [ ] **Name the exposure.** Write down what you are actually trying to protect: the entity and board from suit, the injured from medical cost, families from loss, the space from closure, the budget from an enrollment drop.
- [ ] **Inventory what you already own.** Read the package for crisis counseling and PR. Confirm what the student accident policy pays for medical and death benefits. Confirm the School Leaders program covers a negligent-security or failure-to-protect suit. Mark each exposure covered, partially covered, or open.
- [ ] **Read the trigger on any standalone form.** Is it first-party property or extra expense, or third-party liability defense? Match that trigger against the fear that made you ask. A property-triggered form does not answer a lawsuit fear.
- [ ] **Decide insure versus self-fund on the residual.** For the exposures still open after subtraction, price the premium against the out-of-pocket cost, and put the buy-or-self-fund call in the school leader's hands. Price terrorism as its own lever if the campus profile warrants it.
- [ ] **Document the decision for the board record** so the residual is a choice that was made, not a gap that was defaulted into.

## Why this is standard practice for us and rare elsewhere

A generalist reaches for the standalone because a new policy is a clean transaction with a premium and a renewal. It looks like action. The harder work is reading the property form, the student accident policy, and the management liability program, then telling a school in plain language which risks are already handled and which are open, even when the honest answer costs a commission. We work charter schools only, we read the whole program first, and we frame the residual as the leader's decision to make with the full picture in view. That is the difference between managing a risk and selling a product.
