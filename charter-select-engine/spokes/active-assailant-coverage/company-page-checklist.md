**Headline:** A violent event on campus can cost a school everything and trigger no property claim. Here is which policy actually responds, and what to confirm before you assume you're covered.

Most school risk programs are built around physical damage. A standard property policy, and the business interruption coverage attached to it, only responds when something physically happens to the building. A violent event often causes catastrophic human and operational loss with little or no qualifying physical damage, so the policy stays shut. Below is how the relevant coverages compare on the trigger that matters, and the checklist we run when we review a school's exposure to a violent event.

## How the coverage responds to a no-physical-damage event

| Coverage | Trigger | Pays for crisis costs, care, lost enrollment? | Fits a violent event with no physical damage? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Commercial property** | Direct physical loss or damage to the building | No | No. No physical damage, no claim. |
| **Business interruption (attached to property)** | Income loss following a covered physical loss | No, the income loss must trace to physical damage | No. Enrollment drop without physical damage isn't a trigger. |
| **General liability** | Third-party bodily injury or property damage claims against the school | Defense and damages on covered claims only, not the school's own response costs | Partial and indirect. Doesn't fund crisis response, security, or lost enrollment. |
| **Active assailant / deadly weapon coverage** | The violent event itself, no physical damage required | Yes, built for exactly these costs | Yes. This is the coverage designed for the event. |

The single design difference that matters: active assailant coverage responds to the violent event, not to damage to the building. That is why it pays where the property program can't.

## The coverage review checklist

- [ ] Confirm whether the program carries any standalone active assailant / deadly weapon coverage at all, or only property and liability.
- [ ] Read the trigger definition: does it respond only to an actual event, or also to a credible threat that forces a closure and a response?
- [ ] Confirm the policy includes business interruption tied to enrollment and attendance, not just immediate crisis costs.
- [ ] Confirm covered cost categories: crisis management and PR, medical and psychiatric care, extra security, counseling and family support, remediation or relocation.
- [ ] Test the limit against the real cost: multi-month crisis response, long-term care, and a sustained enrollment dip, not a token figure.
- [ ] Confirm what the existing property and BI policies would and would not pay for a no-physical-damage event, in writing.
- [ ] Document the findings for the board record so the gap is a decision, not an accident.

## Why this is standard practice for us and rare elsewhere

A generalist broker places property and liability, calls the program complete, and never tests it against the one event that produces no broken window. We work charter schools only, we read the full program, and we pressure-test it against the no-physical-damage event specifically, because that is the scenario where a school is most exposed and least able to absorb the cost. The enrollment piece is the part most likely to be thin, and for a charter school it's the part that decides whether the doors stay open.
