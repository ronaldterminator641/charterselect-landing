# LinkedIn Personal Newsletter: Board Side-A Protection

*Spoke type: Personal profile newsletter (Aaron's voice). Goal: human story + compressed insight, driving to the full white paper. Post from Aaron's personal LinkedIn. ~750 words.*

---

**Headline:** A volunteer joined a school board to help. Years later her own savings were on the line, and the policy everyone promised her had quietly stopped covering her.

---

Picture the kind of person who ends up on a charter school board. A parent. A retired principal. A local accountant who is good with numbers and willing to give a few nights a month. They serve for free. Somebody hands them a folder, mentions the school carries Directors and Officers insurance, and tells them they are protected.

Most of the time that holds. A claim comes in, the policy defends the board, the volunteers never feel a thing. The system works the way everyone assumes it will.

Then comes the case it was never built for.

A school hits hard years. Enrollment slips, an authorizer fight turns ugly, the board makes the painful calls, and at some point the school runs out of money or closes. A former board member gets named, personally, in a lawsuit over a decision the board made back then. She reaches for the policy she was promised. The school is insolvent, so it cannot reimburse her. The school's own legal bills already drew the limit down, because the entity and the individuals were drinking from the same glass and the entity drank first. The protection she counted on was never built to stand up for her alone.

That is the moment her own house and her own savings walk into a courtroom. Not because she did anything wrong. Because nobody examined the policy from her side of the table before the claim came in.

Here is the mechanic, in plain English.

A standard D&O policy is really three coverages sharing one pot of money. Side A pays the individual directors directly. Side B pays the school back when it covers its people. Side C covers the school itself. Almost always, all three share one combined limit. So when a serious suit names the school and the board together, the entity's defense can burn through the money before the individual trustees are ever made whole. Side A is supposed to step in when the school cannot indemnify, is legally barred from indemnifying, or when the policy itself gets rescinded or refused. For a charter school none of that is remote. The decisions most likely to draw a lawsuit are the same ones made when the school has the least money to defend anyone.

So the individual exposure is real. The question is what you do about it, and this is where I watch brokers miss in two directions.

One broker sells the base D&O and stops. Nobody asks whether the individuals have their own protected limit or whether they are sharing one pot with the entity. The gap sits there, invisible, until a claim makes it visible.

The other broker knows about the shared-limit problem and overcorrects. He bolts a standalone individual-protection policy onto the program from a separate outside carrier. It looks sophisticated. In practice it means a second carrier, a second set of terms, and drop-down wording that has to reconcile by hand against a primary policy it was never written alongside. It is a fragile structure that fails at exactly the seams where the pieces are supposed to meet, sold as if stapling on more paper were the same thing as building protection.

Here is what a school actually needs. The individual director's protection should come from how the School Leaders program is built, not from a policy stapled on after the fact. The carriers that write for schools reserve dedicated limit for the individual directors and officers, so the entity's side of a shared-limit fight cannot drain what the people rely on. They write terms that respond when the school cannot indemnify. And they place it all with one carrier whose forms already agree with each other, because they were drafted together instead of stitched from two directions. One program that holds up the entity and the individual, confirmed in writing, instead of a base policy for the school and a bolt-on for the people that meet for the first time in a courtroom.

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch, where a job done halfway meant somebody got hurt or something broke, and you owned it. That is the standard I expected when I got into insurance. A lot of charter boards are not getting it.

That is most of why Charter Select works only with charter schools, and why we start from the individual, not the entity, on every management liability program we touch.
