**Headline:** A volunteer joined a school board to help. Years later her own savings were on the line, and the policy everyone promised her had quietly stopped covering her.

---

Picture the kind of person who ends up on a charter school board. A parent. A retired principal. A local accountant who is good with numbers and willing to give a few nights a month. They sign up for free. Somebody hands them a folder, mentions the school carries Directors and Officers insurance, and tells them they're protected.

Most of the time that's true. A claim comes in, the policy defends the board, the volunteers never feel a thing. The system works exactly the way everyone assumes it will.

Then there's the case it wasn't built for.

A school hits hard years. Enrollment slips, the authorizer fight turns ugly, the board makes the painful calls, and at some point the school closes or runs out of money. A former board member gets named, personally, in a lawsuit over a decision the board made back then. She reaches for the policy she was promised. The school is insolvent, so it can't reimburse her. The school's own legal bills already drew down the limit, because the entity and the individuals were drinking from the same glass and the entity drank first. And the protection she counted on was never actually built to stand alone for her.

That is the moment her own house and her own savings walk into a courtroom. Not because she did anything wrong. Because nobody examined the policy from her point of view before the claim came in.

Here's the mechanic, in plain English.

A standard D&O policy is really three coverages sharing one pot of money. Side A pays the individual directors directly. Side B pays the school back when it covers its people. Side C covers the school itself. Almost always, all three share one combined limit. So when a serious suit names the school and the board together, the entity's defense can burn through the money before the individual trustees are ever made whole. On top of that, Side A is supposed to step in when the school can't indemnify, can't legally indemnify, or when the policy itself gets rescinded or refused. For a charter school, those situations aren't remote. The decisions most likely to draw a lawsuit are the same decisions made when the school has the least money to defend anyone.

There's a layer built for exactly this. It's called Side-A DIC. It adds dedicated limit reserved for the individuals that the entity can't touch, and it drops down to pay when the underlying coverage fails, refuses, or runs dry. It's typically written with far fewer exclusions, and it's usually non-rescindable, which means a mistake somebody else made on an application can't wipe out a director's personal protection. It doesn't replace the main D&O program. It's the net under the trapeze for the day the everyday policy isn't enough.

I grew up around manual labor and a ranch, where a job done halfway meant somebody got hurt or something broke, and you owned it. That's the standard I expected when I got into insurance. It's not the standard a lot of charter boards are getting.

Because here is why the gap exists. A generalist broker treats a charter school as a small account to renew quickly. He sells the base D&O and stops. The conversation about what happens to an individual director when the shared limit is gone, or the school is insolvent, or the policy gets rescinded, never happens. Not because it's hard to explain. Because raising it means doing more than rolling last year's policy forward. So the volunteers walk around believing they're fully protected, based on a policy nobody has read from the individual's side. The reassurance is free. The gap is invisible. The only person who pays for the silence is the board member whose name ends up on the complaint.

That's most of why Charter Select works only with charter schools, and why we start from the individual, not the entity, on every D&O program we touch.
