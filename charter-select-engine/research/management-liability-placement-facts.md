## Mechanics

### What "management liability" means
"Management liability" is an umbrella *category* (not a single policy) that bundles the coverages protecting an organization and its leaders for their **management decisions and employment/governance conduct** — as distinct from the bodily-injury / property-damage world covered by general liability. For private companies and nonprofits it is commonly sold as a single **package policy** combining the lines below. (Hylant; Founder Shield; Acrisure; Insurance for Nonprofits)

The standard components:

- **Directors & Officers (D&O) liability.** Protects the personal assets of directors, officers, trustees and the entity itself against claims alleging *wrongful acts* in their governance/managerial capacity — breach of duty, mismanagement, misrepresentation, etc. Pays defense costs, settlements and judgments. For nonprofits, D&O is typically written to cover past, present and future directors, trustees, officers, employees, volunteers and committee members. D&O is almost always **claims-made** coverage (responds to claims *made* during the policy period, subject to a retroactive date), which matters for the umbrella discussion below. (Insurance for Nonprofits; Coyle Group; Hylant)
  - Structurally, traditional D&O is described in **three insuring agreements ("sides")**: **Side A** = non-indemnifiable loss, paid directly to individual directors/officers when the entity cannot or will not indemnify them; **Side B** = reimburses the entity when it *does* indemnify; **Side C** = "entity coverage" for claims against the organization itself. Sides A, B and C usually **share one aggregate limit.** (Amwins; Aon; Coyle Group)
- **Employment Practices Liability (EPLI).** Covers employment-related claims: wrongful termination, discrimination, harassment, hostile work environment, retaliation, failure to promote, etc. Claims-made. (Insurance for Nonprofits; Hylant)
- **Fiduciary liability.** Covers breach of fiduciary duty in the management/administration of employee benefit plans (e.g., 401(k)/403(b), health plans) — including imprudent investment selection, mismanaging plan assets, improperly denying or changing benefits, and ERISA exposures. Broader than EBL because it covers breach of *fiduciary duty*, not just administrative error. (Hylant; Horton; Encore Fiduciary; Moreton)
- **Crime / fidelity (often included or attachable).** Covers employee theft, fraud, social engineering, and can include the **ERISA bond** required for plan sponsors. Sometimes packaged into management liability, sometimes written separately. (Hylant; Moreton; Insurica)

### Adjacent / easily-confused lines (important for the school context)
- **Employee Benefits Liability (EBL).** *Narrower* than fiduciary — covers administrative errors/omissions in benefits administration (e.g., failing to enroll an employee), and **specifically excludes ERISA-violation claims.** Most organizations carry **both** fiduciary and EBL because they cover different things. (Horton; Encore Fiduciary; Baker Hopp)
- **Educators Legal Liability (ELL) / "school board liability."** A school-specific line described by IRMI as a **hybrid of D&O and E&O.** It covers non-bodily-injury/non-property-damage claims against administrators, employees and staff, including **failure to educate, violation of students' rights, civil-rights/Section 1983-type allegations, and failure to provide appropriate (e.g., special-education) services.** ELL forms frequently bundle or attach EPL coverage. This is the form that actually responds to *student/parent* claims a generic nonprofit D&O may not contemplate. (IRMI – ELL; Kinsale; Chubb; Charter School Insurance Alliance)

### How excess limits over these lines are actually obtained
Because management liability lines are claims-made specialty coverages, additional limits are **not** bought through the commercial umbrella (see next section). They are built as a **dedicated excess tower:**

- **Follow-form excess D&O / excess management liability** layers stacked on top of the primary policy, each layer adding limit. (Allied World; Old Republic; Bailey Cavalieri "Peeling the Onion")
- **Excess Side-A DIC (Difference-in-Conditions)** sitting at the top (or alongside) the tower. Side-A DIC does two jobs: (1) provides **additional, dedicated limit for individual directors/officers only** (not shared with the entity), and (2) **"drops down"** to fill gaps when an underlying insurer cannot or will not pay (e.g., insolvency, a carrier wrongfully denying, or coverage exhausted by entity/Side-C claims). It typically has **no retention** for the individuals. (Amwins; Aon; Coyle Group; Landesblosch)

This matters because Sides A/B/C share one limit; in multi-party litigation the entity's own (Side C) defense can burn the limit before individual directors are protected, which is the gap dedicated Side-A/Side-A DIC is built to close. (Amwins; Aon)

## The gap / failure mode

**The central misconception: a commercial (CGL) umbrella does NOT sit over D&O / EPLI / management liability.**

A commercial umbrella / excess liability policy (ISO form CU 00 01) provides excess limits over a *scheduled* set of underlying liability policies — almost always **Commercial General Liability, Commercial Auto liability, and Employers Liability (the Part B of workers' comp).** It generally assumes occurrence-based underlying coverage. (Travelers; Insureon; IRMI; Vouch)

It does **not** extend over management or professional lines:

- The umbrella **follows the form (and the exclusions) of its underlying base policies.** If the base layer is GL/Auto/EL, the umbrella has no D&O, EPLI, fiduciary, or professional liability to sit over. (multiple brokers; "umbrella follows the exclusions of the base policies")
- Industry guidance is explicit that umbrellas are **not a substitute for** professional liability/E&O, D&O, cyber, or other specialty lines, and that **D&O and professional liability "often require their own separate excess policies if higher limits are needed."** (Vouch; Nationwide; Kelly Insurance Group)
- Many umbrellas carry affirmative exclusions for professional services, employment-related practices, and ERISA/benefits, reinforcing that these exposures are intentionally routed outside the umbrella program. (Vouch; Kelly Insurance Group)

**How a generalist broker causes or misses this:**
1. **Assumes the umbrella protects the board.** Tells a school "you have a $5M umbrella, the board is covered" — when the umbrella never touches D&O at all.
2. **Conflates "umbrella" with "excess D&O."** Treats the commercial umbrella as if it adds limit to the D&O tower. It does not; excess D&O is a separate placement.
3. **Leaves management liability under-limited.** Buys a modest primary D&O/EPLI limit and builds *no excess tower*, leaving the board exposed once defense costs (which often erode the limit) eat into it.
4. **Ignores the shared-limit / Side-A problem.** Never places dedicated Side-A or Side-A DIC, so an entity-level or multi-defendant claim can exhaust the limit before individual trustees are protected.
5. **Buys the wrong school form.** Places a generic nonprofit D&O with no Educators Legal Liability, so student-rights / failure-to-educate / special-ed claims may fall outside coverage.
6. **Treats fiduciary/EBL/crime as interchangeable** or skips fiduciary entirely, leaving benefit-plan governance exposed.

## Why it hits schools specifically

- **Volunteer boards with personal assets at risk.** Charter boards are typically unpaid community volunteers (parents, local professionals). D&O claims name individuals *personally*, so without properly-limited D&O — and ideally dedicated Side-A — a trustee's personal net worth is exposed. The "we have an umbrella" assurance is exactly the trap, because the umbrella does nothing for them. (Insurance for Nonprofits; Coyle Group; Cragin & Pike)
- **Heavy EPLI exposure from staff.** Schools are people-intensive employers. Budget-driven layoffs are a documented driver of rising EPLI claims at charter schools nationwide; harassment, discrimination, and wrongful-termination claims are common. (Kelly Insurance Group; Kinsale)
- **Student / parent claims need ELL, not just D&O.** Charter-specific exposures — failure to educate, denial of FAPE/special-education services, student civil-rights and discipline disputes — are addressed by Educators Legal Liability, the D&O/E&O hybrid. A board relying on a plain nonprofit D&O may have a gap here. (IRMI – ELL; Chubb; Charter School Insurance Alliance)
- **Enrollment / funding pressure raises governance risk.** Charter funding follows enrollment; authorizer renewal/non-renewal, financial distress, and closure decisions are precisely the kinds of board actions that generate D&O claims.
- **Lean back office.** Small charter business offices rarely have a risk manager to catch that the umbrella excludes management liability or that the tower is under-built — so the generalist-broker error goes unnoticed until a claim.

---

## The fix

1. **Place management liability as its own program**, distinct from the GL/Auto/Umbrella package — typically a nonprofit/management liability package combining D&O, EPLI, and fiduciary (plus crime where appropriate).
2. **Use the school-appropriate form.** Confirm coverage extends to Educators Legal Liability exposures (failure to educate, student rights, special-education) and that the insured definition covers trustees, officers, employees, volunteers, committee members, and substitute/student teachers.
3. **Right-size the primary limit** to the school's governance, employment, and funding risk — recognizing defense costs typically **erode** the limit.
4. **Build a real excess tower when more limit is needed** — follow-form excess D&O/management liability layers — **never** the commercial umbrella.
5. **Add dedicated Side-A / Side-A DIC** to give individual trustees protected, non-shared limit that drops down when underlying coverage fails or is exhausted.
6. **Carry fiduciary AND consider EBL**, and confirm the ERISA bond requirement for any benefit plans is satisfied (often via the crime policy).
7. **Reconcile claims-made details** across the tower: matching retroactive dates, prior-acts coverage, and consistent terms layer-to-layer.

## Accuracy flags (state general, not absolute)

- **"Almost always," not "always."** Standard commercial umbrellas (ISO CU 00 01 and most carrier forms) exclude/omit management and professional lines. Bespoke or specialty programs *can* be built to wrap specialty lines, and some package products combine lines under one form. State this as the strong general rule, not a universal law. (Vouch; IRMI)
- **Form/edition and carrier wording vary.** Whether an exclusion is affirmative (e.g., an employment-practices or professional-services exclusion) or simply a function of follow-form depends on the specific form and edition. Don't quote exact exclusion language as if standardized.
- **Side-A DIC terms vary.** "No retention for individuals," "drop-down on insurer insolvency/wrongful refusal," and the exact list of DIC triggers differ by carrier form. Describe the *concept* as standard; flag specifics as carrier-dependent.
- **D&O is usually claims-made, but not universally.** State as the norm.
- **Fiduciary vs. EBL vs. crime vs. ERISA bond are four distinct things** that intersect; don't merge them. ERISA bond *limits/requirements* are set by federal rule for ERISA-qualified plans — but applicability to a given charter's plans is fact-specific.
- **"Management liability" packaging is a market convention, not a regulated definition.** What's bundled (and whether crime is included) varies by insurer and by program.
- **Charter governance/insurance is state-regulated.** Authorizer requirements, sovereign/governmental-immunity treatment, and any state-mandated coverage differ by state and by whether the charter is treated as a public entity, nonprofit, or hybrid. Keep charter-specific legal claims general unless tied to a named state.
- **No specific premiums, limits, or retentions are asserted here**; none were verified to a defensible single source. Writer should not invent dollar figures.
- **Sourcing note:** several primary pages (IRMI articles, Amwins, Hylant, ISO form overviews) returned 403 to automated fetching; claims above rest on multiple independent corroborating sources per point. The umbrella-exclusion and excess-tower mechanics are consistent across carrier (Travelers, Nationwide), industry (IRMI, Amwins, Aon), and broker sources, so they are high-confidence. Treat any single-source detail (specific Side-A trigger lists) as lower-confidence.
