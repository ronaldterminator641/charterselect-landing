# Your Best Teacher Just Took a Job Across Town, and Your Health Plan Helped Them Decide

*Why the plain health plan most charter schools hand their staff delivers slow, shallow care at a rising price, and how two upgrades give your people better care while bending the cost curve down.*

---

A teacher you spent two years developing puts in her notice. The district down the road offered a little more money, sure. But when she explains it, the money isn't even the headline. She says her husband waited five weeks for a primary care appointment, her kid's inhaler cost more than a car payment, and the new place just made all of that easier.

You didn't lose her on salary. You lost her on the part of the offer you never built.

---

## The lever you're not pulling

Charter schools live and die on talent. You're recruiting against districts with pension systems and deeper salary bands, and the one place you can actually compete is the total package: culture, mission, and the quality of the benefits your people use every week. Health benefits aren't a line item to that teacher. They're whether her family can see a doctor without taking a day off and gambling on the cost.

So look at what most schools actually offer. A standard fully insured health plan, renewed every year, with a network card and a phone number and a deductible that creeps up. It is mediocre care at a rising price, and your staff feels both halves of that. The visits are rushed. The appointments are weeks out. The pharmacy counter delivers a number nobody can explain. And every spring the premium climbs again.

There are two upgrades that change the actual experience your people have and the actual money your school spends. Most charter leaders have never been shown either one, for a reason I'll get to.

## Upgrade one: Direct Primary Care

Direct Primary Care, or DPC, changes how you pay for the front door of medicine.

In the normal model, every doctor visit is a billable event. The practice files a claim, codes the encounter, waits to get paid, and the whole machine rewards volume. A primary care doctor running that treadmill is carrying two thousand patients and booking seven-minute visits to keep the lights on. That's why your teacher waited five weeks and then felt rushed out the door in ten minutes.

DPC drops the billing treadmill entirely. The plan pays a flat monthly fee per enrolled member directly to a primary care practice. In exchange, that practice gives the member unlimited access: longer appointments, same-day or next-day scheduling, direct texting or messaging with the actual doctor, and a much smaller patient panel so the doctor has time to know your people. Many DPC practices run labs in-house and dispense common generic medications at their wholesale cost, so a strep test or a blood panel or a month of a basic prescription costs the member little or nothing at the point of care.

Here is the part that matters for both care and budget. When a parent can message a doctor on Tuesday morning instead of sitting in urgent care Saturday night, small problems get caught while they're small. Blood pressure gets managed before it's a heart attack. The diabetic gets coached before the ER visit. Better access on the front end quietly prevents the expensive failures on the back end.

## DPC works with your insurance, not instead of it

This is where leaders get nervous, so let me be precise. DPC is not health insurance and it does not replace it. It covers primary care, the routine and ongoing stuff. It does nothing for a specialist, a surgery, an MRI, or a hospital stay.

So a properly built program wraps a DPC membership together with an insurance plan sitting behind it for everything else. The DPC practice handles the high-frequency, low-cost care your people use constantly, the visits and the labs and the everyday prescriptions. The wrapped insurance plan stands behind it for specialists and for the catastrophic events that are the actual reason insurance exists. Pair DPC with a higher-deductible plan or an alternatively funded plan, and the rich front-end access can offset a leaner, cheaper insurance layer, because so much care never touches the insurance at all. Your people get more, and the plan can cost less. Both can be true at once.

## Upgrade two: stop overpaying for the prescriptions

Now the pharmacy counter, where some of the strangest money in American health care moves.

Your drug benefit is run by a Pharmacy Benefit Manager, a PBM. In theory the PBM is the middleman that negotiates good drug prices on your behalf. In practice, the way most traditional PBM contracts are written, the middleman profits from keeping you in the dark. Two mechanics do most of the damage.

The first is spread pricing. The PBM pays the pharmacy one price for a drug, charges your plan a higher price, and pockets the difference, the spread. You never see either number. You see only what your plan was billed, with no way to tell how much of it was the drug and how much was the markup riding on top.

The second is rebate retention. Drug manufacturers pay rebates to get their products favorable placement on the plan's covered-drug list. A meaningful share of those rebates can stay with the PBM instead of flowing back to your school and your members. Worse, because rebates reward high list prices, the system can quietly steer toward a pricier brand-name drug over a cheaper one that would have worked fine, because the expensive drug pays a bigger rebate. The incentive is pointed the wrong way, and it's pointed at your budget.

The fix is a transparent, pass-through pharmacy model. In a pass-through arrangement, the PBM charges your plan exactly what the pharmacy was paid, with a clear, disclosed administrative fee instead of a hidden spread, and rebates flow back to the plan. You see the true cost of the drugs. When the true cost is finally visible, the games stop paying, and the spend usually comes down on its own.

## Why your school doesn't already have this

None of this is reserved for giant corporations. Schools the size of yours run DPC arrangements and transparent pharmacy programs right now. The reason yours doesn't isn't cost, and it isn't eligibility. It's that nobody offered it.

Think about what your benefits broker actually does each year. They show up near renewal, hand you the same fully insured plan with a new premium stapled to it, walk you through the increase, and call it service. That is selling a renewal. It is the easy button: no design work, no pharmacy contract to dissect, no DPC practice to vet and wrap into the plan. A generalist who carries restaurants and law firms and your school in the same book of business has no reason to build any of this, and every reason to re-up what already exists.

Designing a benefit is a different job. It means looking at how your staff actually uses care, finding where the front-end access is failing them and where the pharmacy money is leaking, and assembling DPC plus a transparent Rx layer plus the right wrapped insurance into something that serves your people and your budget at the same time. That work doesn't happen by accident, and it never happens from a broker treating your school as a file to clear before the deadline.

## Before your next renewal

You don't need to become a benefits expert to find out where you stand. You need to look honestly at one thing and notice what it tells you.

So sit with this. When your best teacher weighs your offer against the district's, and her family's real experience of seeing a doctor and filling a prescription goes into that decision, is your health plan helping you keep her, or is it quietly part of why she leaves? And if you can't say for certain, who decided that the same fully insured renewal, year after year, was the best your people could be offered?

If the honest answer is that nobody ever sat down and designed the thing, then you don't have a benefits problem. You have a question nobody on your account has bothered to ask.
