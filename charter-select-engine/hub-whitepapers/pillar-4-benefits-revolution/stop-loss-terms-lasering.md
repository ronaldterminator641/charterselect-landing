# A Half-Million-Dollar Claim, a Policy That "Covered" It, and a Bill Your School Pays Anyway

*How the fine print in a stop-loss contract decides whether your safety net actually catches a catastrophic claim, and why the broker who only quoted you a premium never walked you through the part that matters.*

---

A teacher's spouse gets a cancer diagnosis. The treatment runs past half a million dollars over eighteen months. Your school self-funds its health plan, and you bought stop-loss insurance for exactly this reason.

The claim comes in. It gets denied at the stop-loss level. Not because the treatment wasn't covered by your health plan, it was, but because of where the dates landed and what your contract said about them.

Your school eats the difference. And the contract that did it was sitting in your file the whole time, signed, with terms nobody ever read to you out loud.

---

## What stop-loss is actually doing

When your school self-funds, you pay your employees' medical claims directly out of your own money instead of handing a fixed premium to an insurer and letting them carry the risk. Level-funding works the same way underneath, with the bills smoothed into steady monthly payments. Either way, the school is now on the hook for claims.

That's a smart move for a lot of schools, because in a healthy year the savings stay in your budget instead of becoming an insurer's profit. But it raises an obvious question. What happens when a single catastrophic claim, or a brutal claims year overall, blows past what you can absorb?

That's what stop-loss insurance is for. It's the policy that caps your downside so one sick employee or one bad year can't sink the plan. The protection sounds simple. The protection you actually get is defined entirely by the contract terms, and that's where schools get hurt.

## Two kinds of protection, and you need both

Stop-loss comes in two parts, and they guard against two different disasters.

Specific stop-loss is your per-person cap. You pick a specific deductible, say a fixed dollar amount per individual. The school pays each person's claims up to that number, and the stop-loss carrier reimburses you for anything above it on that one person. This is what catches the single huge claim, the cancer case, the premature baby in the NICU, the transplant.

Aggregate stop-loss is your whole-plan cap. The carrier estimates your expected claims for the year and sets an attachment point above that, commonly around 125 percent of expected. If your total claims for everybody combined blow past that point, aggregate kicks in. This is what catches death by a thousand cuts, a year where no single claim is catastrophic but the pile of them together runs way over budget.

Specific guards one person. Aggregate guards the whole pool. A school carrying only one of them is half-covered and usually doesn't know it.

## The dates that decide whether you're covered at all

Here is the term that quietly denies real claims, and the one a generalist almost never explains.

A medical claim has two dates that matter. The date it was incurred, meaning when the care actually happened, and the date it was paid, meaning when the claim got processed and money went out. Those can be months apart. A big hospital stay in November might not finish paying out until the following spring.

Your stop-loss contract has a basis that defines which claims it covers using those two dates. You'll see it written as two numbers, like 12/12, 12/15, 24/12, or 15/12. The first number is the window for when a claim can be incurred. The second is the window for when it can be paid.

- A 12/12 contract covers claims both incurred and paid inside the same twelve months. It's the tightest window, and it's common for a first plan year.
- A 12/15 contract covers claims incurred during the twelve-month year and paid within fifteen months, giving you three extra months of pay-out runway. This is run-out protection. It catches the claims that happened during your year but settle after it ends.
- A 24/12 or 15/12 contract is run-in. It covers claims paid during your year even if they were incurred before the contract started. This matters when you're switching into a new carrier and still have old claims settling.

Now picture a carrier switch with neither run-in nor run-out lined up. Your old policy was 12/12. It closes the day your year ends. A claim that was incurred in your old year but hasn't finished paying yet falls past the old contract's window. Your new carrier's contract only covers claims incurred on or after its start date, so it won't pick that claim up either. The claim is real, the care was covered by your health plan, and yet it lands in a seam between two stop-loss contracts where neither one pays.

The school covers it. That gap isn't bad luck. It's a contracting decision that should have been made on purpose, in advance, by someone whose job was to make it.

## Lasering, where the carrier moves your goalposts on one person

Say you have an employee already in treatment for something expensive, a known high-cost claimant the carrier can see coming. At renewal, the stop-loss carrier can single that person out and assign them a higher individual deductible than everyone else. That move is called a laser.

The mechanics are blunt. Everyone else on the plan might sit at one specific deductible, and the lasered individual gets set far higher. The school now absorbs much more of that one person's claims before stop-loss reimbursement starts. Your exposure on the exact member you most need protection for goes up, sometimes by a lot.

Lasering is a legitimate underwriting tool, not a scam. But it can wreck a school's budget if it shows up as a surprise at renewal, right when you have the least leverage to do anything about it. Which is why the contract terms around lasering are worth real money:

- A no-laser provision means the carrier agrees not to laser anyone for the coming term.
- A no-new-laser provision means they won't add new lasers at the next renewal, though existing ones can carry forward and they may laser again in later years.
- A rate cap puts a ceiling on how much your renewal premium can jump, often paired with a no-new-laser provision so the carrier can't simply reprice you into the same pain a laser would have caused.

These protections cost something. Carriers add a premium load to provide them. The point isn't that every school should buy every guarantee. The point is that whether you have them, and what they cost, is a decision your school should get to make with eyes open, not discover after a laser lands.

## The other lever: aggregating specific deductibles

One more structure worth knowing, because it's sold as savings and it shifts risk back to you. An aggregating specific deductible lowers your specific premium by adding a second layer of retention. After an individual passes the regular specific deductible, the school keeps paying those excess amounts until a separate pooled dollar amount is used up, and only then does the carrier reimburse as usual.

It can genuinely lower premium. It can also mean the school pays more out of pocket in a heavy claims year than a quick premium comparison would ever suggest. Whether it fits depends on your cash position and your appetite for that swing. It is not a free discount, and it should never be quoted as one.

## Why the generalist sells you the net with the holes in it

A broker who runs benefits as a side dish to everything else does the easy version of this job. They collect a few stop-loss quotes, hand you the lowest premium, and call it a placement. The premium is the one number they can compare on a spreadsheet, so it's the only number they sell on.

The contract basis, the run-in and run-out terms, the laser provisions, the aggregating specific, all of that is where the actual protection lives, and all of it takes time to read, explain, and negotiate. So it gets skipped. You find out the terms were wrong the day a big claim drops through the gap, and by then the renewal is signed and the leverage is gone.

That's not a knowledge problem on your end. You run a school. It's the job you were paying someone else to do, and they quietly did the half that fits on a quote sheet.

## Before your next stop-loss renewal

You don't need to become a stop-loss underwriter to protect your school here. You need to ask a few plain questions and watch whether the answers come back fast and clear or slow and vague.

So sit with this one for a second:

If a claim incurred this plan year doesn't finish paying until after you switch stop-loss carriers, who pays it, your old contract, your new one, or your school? And the harder question underneath it: has anyone on your account actually walked you through your contract basis and your laser terms, in plain language, or did you just get handed the lowest premium and a place to sign?

If you can't answer that with confidence, you don't have a claim problem yet. You have a contract nobody read to you. The reading takes an afternoon. What it costs to skip it doesn't show up until a half-million-dollar claim is already on your desk.
