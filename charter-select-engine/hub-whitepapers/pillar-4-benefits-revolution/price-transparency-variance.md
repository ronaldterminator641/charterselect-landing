# Your Staff Member Handed Over the Insurance Card and the Plan Paid More Than the Cash Price

*A plain-English look at hospital price transparency and price variance: why the same procedure costs wildly different amounts at different facilities, why paying more does not buy better care, and why the network "discount" your school renews every year is measured off a number nobody should pay.*

---

A staff member needs an MRI. They do the responsible thing and show their insurance card.

The plan gets billed the network rate, pays its share, and the member pays a copay. Everyone assumes the card saved money. That is what the card is for.

Down the road, a clinic posts a cash price for the exact same scan. It is lower than what your plan just paid. The card did not save anyone money. It cost the plan more, and it cost the member more.

That is not a rare glitch. Federal data now shows it happening across thousands of hospitals, and most school leaders have never been shown the numbers.

---

## The same procedure, a different price at every door

Start with the part that breaks most people's mental model of how healthcare pricing works. There is no single price for a medical service. Not even close.

Researchers digging into the price data hospitals are now required to publish have found enormous spread for identical care. One analysis documented price variation of more than ten times for the same procedure inside a single hospital, depending on which payer was paying, and more than thirty times across different hospitals in the same state. Same scan. Same blood test. Same outpatient surgery. The price swings by a factor of ten, twenty, thirty, based mostly on who is paying and where.

A school leader hears that and assumes the expensive facility must be the better one. You are paying for quality. That assumption is where a lot of plan money goes to die.

## Paying more does not buy better care

The link between price and quality in American healthcare is weak at best, and in plenty of studies it is not there at all. A higher price tag does not reliably mean a better surgeon, a lower complication rate, or a better outcome. Researchers who have tried to map cost against common quality measures keep landing in the same uncomfortable place: the correlation is murky, frequently absent, and nowhere near strong enough to justify treating a high price as a proxy for good care.

Read that plainly. Your plan can pay three times more at one facility than another for the same procedure and get the same result, or a worse one. The premium price is buying location and leverage, not skill.

So if price does not track quality, what is it tracking? Mostly negotiating power and a starting number that was rigged high from the beginning.

## The "discount" is measured off a fake number

Here is the mechanic the network plan is built to keep quiet.

A hospital's list price lives in a document called the chargemaster. Chargemaster prices are not tied to what care costs to deliver. They are set high on purpose, as an opening position. When your PPO network advertises that it negotiated a 40 or 50 percent discount, that discount comes off the chargemaster. You are getting a real percentage off an unreal number.

A markup study of fourteen common hospital services put the chargemaster ratios at roughly three to eleven times a reasonable cost basis, with negotiated prices still running several times higher than that basis. The discount is real. The price it discounts from is theater. Cut a fake number in half and you can still land far above what the service is actually worth.

This matters more than any other line in your health plan, because hospital and facility spend is the single heaviest line in almost every plan. The place you are most exposed is the exact place the pricing is most rigged.

## The card can cost you more than cash

Now the finding that should stop a busy administrator cold.

The cash price, the number a hospital posts for someone paying without insurance, is frequently lower than the price your insurance negotiated. A study published in Health Affairs, using the cash and negotiated prices hospitals are now required to disclose for seventy shoppable services across more than two thousand hospitals, found that for nearly half of those services, roughly 47 percent, the cash price was lower than or equal to the median insurance-negotiated price at the same hospital, for the same service, in the same setting.

Sit with what that means. In a large share of cases, a member who shows the insurance card pays a price the plan negotiated that is higher than the price they would have gotten by paying cash and never mentioning insurance. The plan pays more. The member's coinsurance is calculated off the bigger number, so the member pays more too. The card, the thing everyone assumes is protection, becomes the more expensive option.

For a self-funded school plan, that is not an abstraction. Every dollar of inflated negotiated price is your plan's dollar, which is your budget's dollar.

## The question worth actually reasoning through

Here is where it gets provocative, and I want to reason through it rather than hand you a slogan.

A big part of what a broad network buys you is access to negotiated rates and out-of-network coverage. But if the negotiated rate is frequently higher than the cash price, and if quality does not track price, then for some plans the honest question is what that broad network access is really buying. Are you paying a premium for access to genuinely lower prices, or paying for access to inflated prices dressed up as discounts?

I am not telling you to tear up your network tomorrow. Networks still do real work, especially for catastrophic and complex care where a single huge claim is the thing that matters, and out-of-network protection has value you should not casually throw away. But the reflex that broad network access is automatically worth its cost deserves a hard look with your own numbers. For some plan designs, paying transparent cash-level prices on the shoppable, high-volume services, while keeping protection where it counts, is the cheaper and cleaner path. That is a question to run the data on, not a thing to assume in either direction.

This is also where reference-based pricing enters the conversation, as one downstream tool. RBP anchors what the plan pays to a transparent benchmark like Medicare instead of a chargemaster discount. It is one way to act on everything above. It is not the only one, and it is a separate paper.

## Why you can finally see any of this

None of these numbers used to be visible. Hospitals kept their prices, insurers kept their negotiated rates, and the whole thing was a black box. That changed.

The federal Hospital Price Transparency rule took effect January 1, 2021. It requires every hospital in the country to publish a machine-readable file listing its standard charges, including gross charges, the discounted cash price, and payer-specific negotiated charges. On top of that, the Transparency in Coverage rule requires health plans and insurers to post machine-readable files of their in-network negotiated rates and out-of-network allowed amounts, with those files required for plan years beginning on or after July 1, 2022.

The data is messy and the files are huge, and a busy school leader is never going to parse them alone. But the point stands. The information that exposes price variance and the cash-versus-negotiated gap is now public and required by law. Somebody just has to actually look at it on your behalf.

## What a broker who's working would have shown you

A broker doing the job pulls the transparency data for the facilities your staff actually use, lays the cash price next to the negotiated price on your highest-volume services, and shows you where your plan is paying the big number for no extra quality. They treat the heaviest line in your plan as something to interrogate, not rubber-stamp.

A generalist does none of that. The easy-button renewal is to re-up the same network, repeat the line about the negotiated discount, pass along the increase, and move to the next account. The transparency files never get opened. The cash-versus-negotiated comparison never gets run. Your school keeps paying inflated prices off a chargemaster while the real numbers sit published on a public website nobody on your account bothered to read.

## Before your next renewal

You do not need to become a pricing analyst. You need one honest look at whether your plan is paying real prices or rigged ones.

So here is the question worth sitting with:

On the handful of procedures your staff use most, has anyone on your account ever pulled the published cash price and set it next to what your plan actually paid through the network? If no one has put those two numbers side by side, how would you know whether your network is buying you lower prices, or just charging you for access to higher ones?

If you cannot answer that, you do not have a verdict yet. You have a question nobody on your account has bothered to ask, on data that has been public for years.
