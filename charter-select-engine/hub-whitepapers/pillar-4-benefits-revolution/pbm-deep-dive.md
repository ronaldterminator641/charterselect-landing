# The Same Pill, Two Schools, A Price Gap Wide Enough To Hire A Teacher

*Pharmacy Benefit Managers, made to make sense. How the middleman in your drug benefit gets paid, why the same medication can cost wildly different amounts depending on who runs the program, and the levers a real strategy pulls to bring the number down.*

---

Two charter schools fill the exact same prescription in the same month. One plan gets billed a few dollars. The other gets billed a few hundred for the identical molecule, same manufacturer, same dose.

Neither principal saw the difference. Neither one was told it existed.

The thing that decided it wasn't the drug. It was the contract sitting behind the pharmacy counter.

## The middleman you've never met

Every drug benefit you offer runs through a company called a Pharmacy Benefit Manager, a PBM. The PBM builds your covered-drug list, negotiates with manufacturers, sets what each pharmacy gets paid, and decides what your plan gets charged. Three large PBMs control the majority of the prescription claims processed in the United States, so most plans, including the one stapled to your fully insured renewal, ride on one of a small handful of them.

The pitch is simple. The PBM is the buyer with scale, so it gets you better prices than you could get alone. Sometimes that's true. The problem is the way most legacy contracts are written, because under a traditional structure the middleman makes more money when your drug spend goes up, not down. SmithRx, a transparent PBM, has a name for this design. They call it inverse incentives: the vendor profits when your spending soars. The incentive points the wrong way, and it points straight at your budget.

Let me walk through the mechanics that do the damage, then the model that fixes them, then the levers a real pharmacy strategy uses to cut the bill.

## Mechanic one: spread pricing

Spread pricing is the cleanest example of the wrong incentive.

The PBM pays the pharmacy one price for a drug. It charges your plan a higher price. It keeps the difference. That difference is the spread, and you almost never see it, because the contract shows you one number, the price billed to the plan, and never the number paid to the pharmacy underneath it.

The math is small per claim and enormous in aggregate. If a pharmacy gets reimbursed two dollars for a generic and your plan gets billed two dollars and fifty cents, the PBM books fifty cents of pure margin. Run that across a staff of a hundred people filling maintenance prescriptions month after month, year after year, and the spread becomes real money that quietly leaves your plan with no line item attached to it.

You can't manage a number you can't see. That's the entire design.

## Mechanic two: rebate retention

Drug manufacturers pay rebates to PBMs to win favorable placement on the formulary, the list of drugs the plan covers and how it covers them. Better placement means more volume, so manufacturers pay to get there.

Here's where it bends. A rebate is usually structured as a percentage off the drug's list price, so the higher the list price, the bigger the rebate dollars. A traditional PBM that keeps a share of those rebates therefore has a quiet reason to favor the expensive drug over the cheaper one, because the pricier product can pay the larger rebate. The system can steer toward a high-cost brand when a lower-cost option would have worked fine, and the rebate that's supposed to be a discount for your plan becomes a revenue stream for the middleman instead.

So you can end up paying for a more expensive drug than your people needed, while a meaningful share of the rebate that justified it never reaches your school.

## Mechanic three: the fees you can't find on the invoice

Spread and rebates are the famous two. They are not the whole game. SmithRx catalogs a longer list of ways legacy contracts pull money out without showing it, and the pattern is worth knowing before you read your own contract.

There are clawbacks, where the patient's copay collects more than the drug actually cost the plan and the PBM recovers the excess. There are DIR fees, direct and indirect remuneration, charged to the pharmacy after the sale, so a price quoted at the counter quietly grows later. And there are the administrative fees themselves, which can look modest on page one and balloon once the surcharges are counted. SmithRx points to a tidy example: a fee advertised around four dollars per member per month that lands above ten once every ad-hoc charge is added in. Tie all of this together with the formulary, which in a traditional model isn't a neutral medical document, it's a profit instrument, and you have a system tuned to maximize what the middleman keeps rather than to land your members on the most effective drug at the lowest true cost.

## The model that fixes it: transparent, pass-through, aligned

There's a structurally different way to run a PBM, and once you see it, the legacy version stops making sense.

A transparent, pass-through PBM gets paid one way: a clear, disclosed administrative fee. It charges your plan exactly what the pharmacy was paid, so there's no spread to hide. It passes one hundred percent of manufacturer rebates back to the plan, so the discount actually lands with you. And it gives you real visibility into the true cost of every drug, which means you can finally manage the spend instead of guessing at it.

The founder's experience points to SmithRx as a strong example of this model, and it's worth being specific about what they actually do, because the specifics are the lesson. Their pricing reimburses each pharmacy for its real acquisition cost, using public market proxies like NADAC when a direct number isn't available, plus a fixed dispensing fee. Their revenue comes from a flat monthly fee per member, not a cut of the drug, and not a charge per claim. That last detail matters more than it sounds: a per-claim model quietly rewards more transactions, while a flat per-member fee gives the PBM no reason to want your people filling more prescriptions or pricier ones. They remit one hundred percent of manufacturer rebates and discounts to the plan. And the number they steer the whole program toward isn't the biggest rebate, it's the lowest net cost: total drug spend, minus every rebate, measured as a per-member-per-month figure you can actually watch. Chasing lowest net cost pushes a formulary toward low-cost generics and biosimilars instead of the brand that happened to pay the fattest rebate.

That's an example of the type, based on what the founder has seen work for clients, not a claim that one vendor beats every other on every metric. The point is the architecture. When the fee is fixed and disclosed, the spread is gone, the rebates flow through, and the scoreboard is net cost, the hidden revenue streams a traditional contract depends on simply aren't there, and the spend tends to drift down because nobody's being steered toward the expensive option anymore.

There's a quieter benefit too, the one your staff feels. A transparent program puts the data in reach: claims you can see, a member tool that finds the nearest pharmacy carrying a drug at the lowest covered price, prior-authorization status a member can actually track instead of waiting on a black box. Transparency isn't only a finance feature. It shows up at the counter.

## The levers a real strategy pulls

A good pharmacy strategy doesn't stop at clean pricing. It actively attacks the biggest line items, especially specialty drugs, where a single member's medication can cost more than several teachers' worth of premium. Three levers do most of the work. Each one is legitimate, and each one has a catch you have to respect.

**Manufacturer copay assistance.** Drug makers offer copay cards that cut a member's out-of-pocket cost on a brand-name medication. Used well, they ease the burden on the member and can pull dollars off the plan. The caution: copay cards tend to favor brand drugs over cheaper generics, and many plans run copay accumulator or maximizer programs that accept the manufacturer's payment but don't count it toward the member's deductible or out-of-pocket maximum. When the card's annual limit runs dry mid-year, the member can get hit with the full cost they thought they'd already met. Some states have started forcing plans to count that assistance, but the rules are uneven, so the tool only helps if your program is built to capture it cleanly.

**Patient assistance programs.** For high-cost specialty drugs, manufacturers and nonprofit foundations run patient assistance programs, or PAPs, that can provide the medication free or at deep discount to members who qualify. For a single expensive specialty case, a PAP can take a five- or six-figure annual drug off the plan entirely. A transparent PBM will often build this in as a service rather than leave it to chance: SmithRx, for instance, runs a specialty navigation program that helps members find and apply to advocacy and grant foundations when a high-cost drug isn't covered under the standard benefit. The caution: these programs carry income limits and eligibility rules, so they don't apply to everyone, and a strategy that leans on them has to handle the enrollment and qualification work for real, not just point at the program and hope.

**International and global sourcing.** Some programs source certain brand and high-cost specialty drugs from abroad, where the same medication can cost a fraction of the U.S. price. The potential savings on a handful of specialty drugs are large. The caution here is the heaviest of the three, so be precise about it. The FDA has flagged commercial importation of U.S.-approved drugs as a legal gray area at best, with real concerns about a medication's supply chain, labeling, storage conditions, and whether it was made at an approved facility. Reputable programs limit themselves: no controlled substances, no drugs under FDA REMS safety programs, supply caps, and they keep it voluntary for the member. This lever can work, but only inside guardrails, with eyes open to the regulatory and safety questions. Anyone who pitches it as a free lunch is not being straight with you.

## Why your school is overpaying right now

None of this is exotic, and none of it is reserved for big corporations. Schools your size run transparent pass-through programs today. The reason yours probably doesn't comes down to who's on your account.

Picture what your benefits broker actually does. The PBM came bundled inside the carrier's fully insured plan. The broker handed you that plan, renewed it every year, walked you through the premium increase, and called it service. Nobody ever explained what a PBM was, what spread pricing meant, or where the rebate dollars went. A generalist carrying restaurants and law firms and your school in the same book has no reason to crack open a pharmacy contract and dissect it. The easy button is to re-up what already exists and move on. So the bundled PBM keeps taking the spread, keeps the rebates that should have been yours, keeps the fees you'd never find on the invoice, and the school overpays for years without a single number on a statement to explain why.

Doing this right is a different job. It means carving the pharmacy benefit out, putting a transparent pass-through PBM behind it, holding the program to a lowest-net-cost standard, and layering in the copay, PAP, and sourcing levers where they fit and where they're safe. That work doesn't happen at a renewal meeting the night before the policy expires. It happens because someone decided your school was worth the dig.

## Before your next renewal

You don't need to become a pharmacy expert to find out where you stand. You need to ask one honest question and watch how quickly it gets answered.

So sit with this. When your plan pays for a prescription, does anyone on your account know the difference between what the pharmacy was paid and what your plan was billed? Can they show you, in writing, whether the rebates your drugs earned came back to your school or stayed with the middleman, and what every fee on that contract actually totals once the surcharges are added? And if the answer is a shrug, who decided that the bundled PBM nobody ever explained to you was the best your people and your budget could do?

If nobody ever sat down and built the pharmacy benefit on purpose, you don't have a drug-cost problem. You have a question nobody on your account has bothered to ask.
