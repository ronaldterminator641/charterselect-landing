# The Same Pill, Two Schools, A Price Gap Wide Enough To Hire A Teacher

*Pharmacy Benefit Managers, made to make sense. How the middleman in your drug benefit gets paid, why the same medication can cost wildly different amounts depending on who runs the program, and the levers a real strategy pulls to bring the number down.*

---

Two charter schools fill the exact same prescription in the same month. One plan gets billed a few dollars. The other gets billed a few hundred for the identical molecule, same manufacturer, same dose.

Neither principal saw the difference. Neither one was told it existed.

The thing that decided it wasn't the drug. It was the contract sitting behind the pharmacy counter.

---

## The middleman you've never met

Every drug benefit you offer runs through a company called a Pharmacy Benefit Manager, a PBM. The PBM builds your covered-drug list, negotiates with manufacturers, sets what each pharmacy gets paid, and decides what your plan gets charged. Three large PBMs control the majority of the prescription claims processed in the United States, so most plans, including the one stapled to your fully insured renewal, ride on one of a small handful of them.

The pitch is simple. The PBM is the buyer with scale, so it gets you better prices than you could get alone. Sometimes that's true. The problem is how a traditional PBM gets paid, because the way most legacy contracts are written, the middleman makes more money when your drug spend goes up, not down. The incentive points the wrong way, and it points straight at your budget.

Let me walk through the three mechanics that do the damage, then the model that fixes them, then the levers a real pharmacy strategy uses to cut the bill.

## Mechanic one: spread pricing

Spread pricing is the cleanest example of the wrong incentive.

The PBM pays the pharmacy one price for a drug. It charges your plan a higher price. It keeps the difference. That difference is the spread, and you almost never see it, because the contract shows you one number, the price billed to the plan, and never the number paid to the pharmacy underneath it.

The math is small per claim and enormous in aggregate. If a pharmacy gets reimbursed two dollars for a generic and your plan gets billed two dollars and fifty cents, the PBM books fifty cents of pure margin. Run that across a staff of a hundred people filling maintenance prescriptions month after month, year after year, and the spread becomes real money that quietly leaves your plan with no line item attached to it.

You can't manage a number you can't see. That's the entire design.

## Mechanic two: rebate retention

Drug manufacturers pay rebates to PBMs to win favorable placement on the formulary, the list of drugs the plan covers and how it covers them. Better placement means more volume, so manufacturers pay to get there.

Here's where it bends. A rebate is usually structured as a percentage off the drug's list price, so the higher the list price, the bigger the rebate dollars. A traditional PBM that keeps a share of those rebates therefore has a quiet reason to favor the expensive drug over the cheaper one, because the pricier product can pay the larger rebate. The system can steer toward a high-cost brand when a lower-cost option would have worked fine, and the rebate that's supposed to be a discount for your plan becomes a revenue stream for the middleman instead.

So you can end up paying for a more expensive drug than your people needed, while a meaningful share of the rebate that justified it never reaches your school.

## Mechanic three: formulary steering

Tie the first two together and you get the third. The formulary isn't a neutral medical document in a traditional model. It's a profit instrument. Drug placement, the tiers, the prior-authorization rules, the preferred products, all of it can be tuned to maximize spread and rebate capture rather than to land your members on the most effective drug at the lowest true cost. A rigid, locked formulary you can't see into or adjust is how that steering gets protected.

## The model that fixes it: transparent, pass-through, flexible

There's a structurally different way to run a PBM, and once you see it, the legacy version stops making sense.

A transparent, pass-through PBM gets paid one way: a clear, disclosed administrative fee per member or per claim. That's it. It charges your plan exactly what the pharmacy was paid, so there's no spread to hide. It passes one hundred percent of manufacturer rebates back to the plan, so the discount actually lands with you. And it gives you real visibility into the true cost of every drug, which means you can finally manage the spend instead of guessing at it.

The founder's experience points to SmithRx as a strong example of this model. SmithRx runs a one hundred percent pass-through structure, charges a flat administrative fee, passes rebates and discounts straight through to the employer, and keeps a flexible formulary rather than a rigid locked one. That's an example of the type, based on what the founder has seen work for clients, not a claim that one vendor beats every other on every metric. The point is the architecture. A pass-through contract with a flexible formulary removes the hidden revenue streams that a traditional contract depends on, and when the games stop paying, the spend usually drifts down on its own because nobody's being steered toward the expensive option anymore.

That flexibility matters for the next part, because a flexible program can plug in cost-lowering tools a rigid one tends to block.

## The levers a real strategy pulls

A good pharmacy strategy doesn't stop at clean pricing. It actively attacks the biggest line items, especially specialty drugs, where a single member's medication can cost more than several teachers' worth of premium. Three levers do most of the work. Each one is legitimate, and each one has a catch you have to respect.

**Manufacturer copay assistance.** Drug makers offer copay cards that cut a member's out-of-pocket cost on a brand-name medication. Used well, they ease the burden on the member and can pull dollars off the plan. The caution: copay cards tend to favor brand drugs over cheaper generics, and some plan designs use copay accumulator or maximizer programs that stop the manufacturer's payment from counting toward the member's deductible, so the savings can evaporate once the card runs out. The tool only helps if your program is built to capture it cleanly.

**Patient assistance programs.** For high-cost specialty drugs, manufacturers and nonprofit foundations run patient assistance programs, or PAPs, that can provide the medication free or at deep discount to members who qualify. For a single expensive specialty case, a PAP can take a five- or six-figure annual drug off the plan entirely. The caution: these programs carry income limits and eligibility rules, so they don't apply to everyone, and a strategy that leans on them has to handle the enrollment work and the qualification screening for real, not just point at the program and hope.

**International and global sourcing.** Some programs source certain brand and high-cost specialty drugs from abroad, where the same medication can cost a fraction of the U.S. price. The potential savings on a handful of specialty drugs are large. The caution here is the heaviest of the three, so be precise about it. The FDA has flagged commercial importation of U.S.-approved drugs as a legal gray area at best, with real concerns about a medication's supply chain, labeling, storage conditions, and whether it was made at an approved facility. Reputable programs limit themselves, no controlled substances, no drugs under FDA REMS safety programs, supply caps, and they keep it voluntary for the member. This lever can work, but only inside guardrails, with eyes open to the regulatory and safety questions. Anyone who pitches it as a free lunch is not being straight with you.

## Why your school is overpaying right now

None of this is exotic, and none of it is reserved for big corporations. Schools your size run transparent pass-through programs today. The reason yours probably doesn't comes down to who's on your account.

Picture what your benefits broker actually does. The PBM came bundled inside the carrier's fully insured plan. The broker handed you that plan, renewed it every year, walked you through the premium increase, and called it service. Nobody ever explained what a PBM was, what spread pricing meant, or where the rebate dollars went. A generalist carrying restaurants and law firms and your school in the same book has no reason to crack open a pharmacy contract and dissect it. The easy button is to re-up what already exists and move on. So the bundled PBM keeps taking the spread, keeps the rebates that should have been yours, and the school overpays for years without a single number on a statement to explain why.

Doing this right is a different job. It means carving the pharmacy benefit out, putting a transparent pass-through PBM behind it, building a flexible formulary, and layering in the copay, PAP, and sourcing levers where they fit and where they're safe. That work doesn't happen at a renewal meeting the night before the policy expires. It happens because someone decided your school was worth the dig.

## Before your next renewal

You don't need to become a pharmacy expert to find out where you stand. You need to ask one honest question and watch how quickly it gets answered.

So sit with this. When your plan pays for a prescription, does anyone on your account know the difference between what the pharmacy was paid and what your plan was billed? Can they show you, in writing, whether the rebates your drugs earned came back to your school or stayed with the middleman? And if the answer is a shrug, who decided that the bundled PBM nobody ever explained to you was the best your people and your budget could do?

If nobody ever sat down and built the pharmacy benefit on purpose, you don't have a drug-cost problem. You have a question nobody on your account has bothered to ask.
