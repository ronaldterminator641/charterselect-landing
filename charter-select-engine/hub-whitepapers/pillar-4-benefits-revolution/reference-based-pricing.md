# The Same Surgery Costs Your Plan Three Times What the Hospital Will Accept. You Just Paid the Big Number.

*A plain-English look at reference-based pricing: how a self-funded school can pay hospital bills off a transparent benchmark instead of an inflated chargemaster, what it really saves, and the tradeoff anyone honest has to tell you about.*

---

A staff member has surgery. The hospital bills your plan a number with a lot of zeros. Your plan pays it, minus the discount your network negotiated, and everyone moves on.

Now picture the same procedure at the same hospital, billed to Medicare. The hospital accepts a fraction of what your plan just paid. Same operating room, same surgeon, same supplies. A wildly different price, and the lower one still keeps the lights on at the hospital.

Your plan paid the big number. Most school leaders have never been shown there was another one.

---

## What you're actually paying for in a network plan

The health plan most schools carry pays providers through a PPO network. The pitch is simple and it sounds great. The network negotiates a discount off the hospital's billed charges, and your plan pays the discounted amount.

The problem hides in the words "billed charges." A hospital's list price lives in a document called the chargemaster, and chargemaster prices are not tied to what care costs to deliver. They are set high on purpose. A 40 percent or 50 percent discount off a number that was inflated to begin with can still land well above what the same service is worth.

So the network discount, the thing that feels like the win, is a discount off a price nobody should be paying in the first place. You can negotiate hard against a fake number and still overpay. That is the quiet design of network pricing, and it is the single biggest line in your health plan: hospital and facility costs.

## Reference-based pricing flips the starting point

Reference-based pricing, usually shortened to RBP, attacks that problem at its root. Instead of starting from the hospital's inflated list price and discounting down, the plan starts from a transparent, objective benchmark and pays up from there.

The benchmark most RBP plans use is Medicare. Medicare already calculates, service by service, a defined reimbursement rate that hospitals accept every day. RBP takes that rate and pays a set multiple of it. A plan might reimburse, for example, a fixed percentage above Medicare for a given procedure, commonly somewhere in the range of 120 to 200 percent of the Medicare rate depending on the plan design and the service.

Sit with the gap for a second. Industry analyses of commercial network pricing frequently put it at several times Medicare, sometimes 300 percent or more. An RBP plan anchored at, say, 150 percent of Medicare is paying the hospital well above its Medicare rate, which is a fair and sustainable price, while paying far less than the network would have. The savings come from anchoring payment to an objective cost basis instead of an opaque list price.

That is the whole idea. You stop arguing about the discount and start defining the price.

## What this can actually save

Be careful with savings numbers, because vendors love to wave around the best-case figure. Spoken honestly: published industry estimates commonly describe self-funded employers cutting total health plan spend by something in the range of 20 to 30 percent after moving to a well-run RBP arrangement. Your school's actual result depends on your claims, your region, and how the program is run. Treat any single percentage as a marketing number until someone runs your real data.

The reason the savings can be that large is simple. Hospital and facility spend is the heaviest line in almost every health plan. Cut the price basis on the heaviest line and the whole plan moves. Trimming around the edges of a network plan never touches that.

## This only works on a self-funded chassis

RBP is not a plan you bolt onto a fully insured policy. It belongs on a self-funded or level-funded plan, the same chassis covered in the funding conversation. The reason is structural. In a self-funded arrangement, the plan, meaning your school, decides what it pays for each claim. That authority is exactly what RBP uses. The plan defines its allowable amount off the Medicare benchmark, and a third-party administrator processes claims and pays providers at that defined amount.

If you are still fully insured, the insurer sets the price and keeps the upside, so there is no lever for you to pull. RBP is one of the concrete reasons the move off fully insured matters. It opens a door that stays locked otherwise.

## Now the tradeoff, said out loud

Anyone selling RBP as free money is not being straight with you, so here is the honest part.

Because an RBP plan pays off a benchmark instead of a signed network contract, there is no contract stopping a hospital from sending the member a bill for the difference between its list price and what the plan paid. That is called balance billing, and it is the real risk of this model. It is the thing the slick pitch decks gloss over.

This risk is manageable, and a serious RBP program manages it as a core feature, not an afterthought. Good programs build in member advocacy and balance-bill defense. When a provider sends a balance bill, a patient advocacy and legal team steps in on the member's behalf, engages the provider, and works to resolve the charge so the employee is not left holding it. Reputable administrators report that the large majority of claims end with no balance bill to the member, but "large majority" is not "never," and you should distrust anyone who promises never.

There are two more real frictions. Some providers, particularly large hospital systems, push back on RBP and may be less willing to treat plan members smoothly or may require more coordination up front. And the whole thing depends on employee communication. Your staff need to understand the plan before they get a surprising bill in the mail, and they need to know exactly who to call the moment one shows up. An RBP plan with weak member support is a complaint factory. An RBP plan with strong advocacy is a cost-control engine your staff barely notice.

## Why your school was never shown this

Here is the pattern. Reference-based pricing is more work to design, more work to explain to staff, and more work to support once it is running. It requires a broker who will sit with your people, set up the advocacy and defense services correctly, and stay in the room when a member calls about a bill.

A generalist benefits broker does none of that. The easy-button renewal is to re-up the same network plan, pass along the increase, and move on to the next account. RBP never comes up, not because it would not fit your school, but because it would ask something of the broker. So the heaviest, most attackable line in your entire health plan goes unexamined year after year, and you keep paying the big number while a fair number was sitting right there.

That is not a knock on every broker. It is a description of what happens when nobody on your account treats your health spend as something to actively manage.

## Before your next renewal

You do not need to commit to anything to find out whether this fits. You need one honest look at where your money goes.

So here is the question worth sitting with:

On your largest hospital and facility claims last year, has anyone ever shown you what those services would have cost benchmarked against Medicare, next to what your plan actually paid? If no one has ever put those two numbers side by side for you, how would you know whether you have a cost problem, or just a broker who never looked?

If you cannot answer that, you do not have a verdict yet. You have a question nobody on your account has bothered to ask on your behalf.
