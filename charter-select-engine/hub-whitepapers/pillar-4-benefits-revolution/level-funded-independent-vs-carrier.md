# Two Plans Both Called Level Funded, And Only One Lets You See Where Your Money Went

*A plain-English comparison of independent level-funded programs and the big carriers' level-funded products: same label, very different machinery, and why the broker only ever shows you one of them.*

---

Your broker says he found you a level-funded plan. Good. You should be glad he did.

Then ask him a second question. Which one? Because the words on the brochure can read identical while the engine underneath runs on opposite logic. One version is engineered so your nurse uses the plan more and pays less at the counter, hands you your claims data, and returns every unused dollar. The other bundles all of that inside the carrier and sends you a number once a year with no map of how it got there.

Most charter leaders only ever get shown the second one. Below is why, and the difference it makes.

---

## A quick reset on what level funding even is

The full story lives in our piece on alternative funding, so here is the short one. A level-funded plan lets a smaller employer self-fund with training wheels. You pay a steady monthly amount, split three ways: a claims fund to pay medical bills, an administrative fee, and the stop-loss premium that caps your downside if claims run hot. In a healthy year, the money you funded but did not spend can come back to you, instead of the carrier pocketing your good year by default.

That structure is the same no matter who builds it. The fight is over who builds it, and what they let you keep, see, and control while they do.

## Same label, two different machines

When a generalist broker says level-funded, he usually means a packaged product from one of the big medical carriers. Cigna, UnitedHealthcare, and the Blues all sell one. They are real plans, they are ACA-compliant, and under federal law they sit on the self-funded side with the employer carrying claims risk inside the stop-loss limits. Cigna, for example, rolls claims funding, the stop-loss premium, and admin fees into one preset monthly payment, and if claims run high you do not pay more that year. UnitedHealthcare markets its level-funded product as costing employers meaningfully less on average than a comparable fully insured plan. That is real, and it is fine work.

The other machine is an independent level-funded program. Two names worth knowing here are Gravie and Benecon. Same three-part funding structure, same stop-loss safety net, but the program is assembled from parts the employer can actually inspect and swap, and built around a different idea of who the plan is supposed to serve.

The distinction shows up in four specific places, and the independents each made a deliberate bet in one of them.

## One: the plan can be designed for the people using it, not just the employer buying it

A carrier level-funded plan is mostly a funding wrapper around a standard copay or HSA design. The shape of the benefit is the same thing your staff has always had: a deductible, copays, coinsurance, an out-of-pocket maximum. The savings story is aimed at the employer's renewal, not at the teacher standing at the pharmacy counter.

Gravie built its flagship plan, Comfort, on the opposite premise. It eliminates deductibles and copays on roughly 85 percent of the most common care: office visits, specialist visits, labs and imaging, generic prescriptions, urgent care, mental health, virtual care. Those carry zero out-of-pocket cost, paid first dollar. The tradeoff is honest and worth stating plainly: less common, expensive events like surgery or a hospital stay can cost the member more than they would on a traditional plan, up to an out-of-pocket maximum the employee chooses from a few options, after which the plan covers everything. The bet is that most people, most years, use the common stuff, so removing the price tag on routine care gets people to use it before a small problem becomes an expensive one. Gravie reported that for 2022, employers averaged a 15 percent premium reduction against their prior carrier renewal, and employees on Comfort spent meaningfully less out of pocket than peers on traditional copay or HSA plans.

That is the founder's read, not a slogan: a plan your staff will actually use early is a plan that bends the claims curve, and a plan that bends the claims curve is the one that keeps returning surplus.

## Two: who can see the claims data, and whether you can swap the pharmacy

Both kinds of plan are technically self-funded, so the employer is supposed to own the claims data either way. The difference is how usable it is when it reaches you.

A carrier-administered plan tends to hand you summary reporting formatted the way the carrier prefers. The pharmacy spend in particular often sits carved in, meaning drug pricing, rebates, and utilization stay folded inside the medical plan's reporting. One vendor, one bill, looks clean. The cost of that clean look is that you cannot easily see what your drugs cost or where the rebate dollars went, and the carrier's own pharmacy benefit manager is already bundled in. UnitedHealthcare's Optum Rx and Cigna's Express Scripts both arrive attached to their respective level-funded products.

Independent programs are built to be unbundled. You can read the cost drivers month over month, and you can carve the pharmacy out and shop it. Pass-through pharmacy pricing, where the PBM charges a clear fee and passes rebates back instead of hiding margin in the spread, used to be the independent advantage alone. The big PBMs are now being pushed the same direction: Optum Rx is shifting to clearly defined per-member fees, and Express Scripts is moving network reimbursement toward a cost-plus model in 2026. Gravie itself moved its PBM to Express Scripts effective January 1, 2026, replacing CVS Caremark. Forget which PBM has the cleanest contract this quarter. What matters is that an independent structure lets you re-shop the pharmacy when the market shifts, while a sealed carrier product makes you wait for the carrier to decide to shift it for you.

## Three: how much of a healthy year you actually get back

Surplus return is where the brochures blur together and the contracts do not.

Benecon's model is the clean illustration. Through its VERIS consortium, Benecon runs as an independent administrator, and the design is pay for what you use: each group keeps its own claim-fund account, risk is not commingled, no group subsidizes another, and the groups pool only to buy stop-loss together for better pricing. What you do not spend on claims, you keep, with Benecon advertising 100 percent surplus return alongside guaranteed renewals and rate caps. A healthy year is fully yours, and it is written that way on purpose.

Carrier level-funded products do return surplus too, but the terms vary and the fine print earns its keep. Cigna's level-funded settlement, as one published example, returns a share of the savings, often half or more, frequently as an administrative-fee credit rather than cash, and some carrier programs require you to renew to collect it at all. One wrinkle applies to everyone, independent or carrier: if employee contributions funded part of the premium, ERISA may treat a slice of the surplus as plan assets that must flow back to participants rather than to the school's general budget. A program built around surplus return handles that cleanly. A product where surplus is an afterthought handles it quietly.

## Four: advocacy and lock-in

Carrier plans route your staff to a general call center and tie you to that carrier's network and PBM economics, so leaving means rebuilding the whole arrangement somewhere else.

The independents tend to treat member help as part of the product. Gravie Care staffs licensed advocates who help members pick a plan, understand a bill, and sort out a claim year-round, and Benecon's ConnectCare3 arm runs nurse navigation and chronic-care support. For a charter staff with no HR department to absorb benefits questions, that hand-holding has real weight. And because an independent program is assembled from interchangeable parts, a network or administrator change does not force you to tear the whole plan down.

## The fair part, because pretending there are no tradeoffs is how brokers lie

The big carriers earn their scale honestly in two areas, and a school should weigh them.

Network breadth is the first. The national carriers have the widest provider networks in the country, and for a school with staff spread across a region, that reach has real value. An independent program has to prove its network covers your people before you trust it, and Comfort's richest savings show up only when staff actually use in-network common care.

Simplicity is the second. One carrier, one bill, one phone number is genuinely easier to administer than a program with separately chosen parts. That ease is worth something to a small school office, and it is the honest appeal of the packaged product.

So the verdict here is a point of view, not a law of physics. In Aaron's experience placing these plans, the independents take the cake for a charter school often enough that they should never be left off the table. A plan designed so people use routine care early, claims data you can actually read, a pharmacy you can re-shop, surplus terms that say 100 percent in writing, and advocates who pick up the phone tend to outrun the carrier's network-and-simplicity edge once you run the real numbers. Reasonable people can land differently on a given case. The real problem is that most schools never get to choose at all.

## Why you only ever saw one of them

A generalist broker sells the carrier's level-funded product for the same reason water runs downhill. It is the easy one. The carrier has a quoting portal he already knows, a sales rep who feeds him the proposal, and a familiar name the board will recognize. He clicks a few buttons and looks like he did something modern.

Showing you an independent program is work. He would have to know the administrators, understand consumer-first plan design, read a stop-loss contract he did not get handed, and compare pharmacy and surplus terms line by line. That is hours he does not bill on an account he already thinks is too small. So he shows you the carrier box, calls it level funding, and lets you assume that is the whole category.

It is not the whole category. It is the easy corner of it.

## Before you sign whatever he put in front of you

You do not have to become a benefits expert to protect yourself here. You just have to ask the one question that reveals whether you were shown the market or shown the shortcut.

So sit with this. When your broker brought you a level-funded plan, did he also bring an independent program to compare it against, show you a design built around what your staff actually uses, and put in writing who controls the pharmacy and the data and how much of a healthy year comes back to your budget? Or did one carrier's product show up alone, dressed as the entire option set?

If only one machine ever crossed your desk, you were not given a choice. You were given a default. And a default is a fine thing to accept on purpose and an expensive thing to accept by accident.
