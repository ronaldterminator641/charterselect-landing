# Two Plans Both Called Level Funded, And Only One Lets You See Where Your Money Went

*A plain-English comparison of independent level-funded programs and the big carriers' level-funded products: same label, very different machinery, and why the broker only ever shows you one of them.*

---

Your broker says he found you a level-funded plan. Good. You should be glad he did.

Then ask him a second question. Which one? Because the words on the brochure can read identical while the engine underneath runs on opposite logic. One version hands you your claims data, lets you bolt on your own pharmacy manager, and sends back every unused dollar. The other bundles all of that inside the carrier and sends you a number once a year with no map of how it got there.

Most charter leaders only ever get shown the second one. Here is why, and here is the difference it makes.

---

## A quick reset on what level funding even is

The longer version of this story lives in our piece on alternative funding, so here is the short one. A level-funded plan lets a smaller employer self-fund with training wheels. You pay a steady monthly amount, and that amount splits three ways: a claims fund to pay your people's medical bills, an administrative fee, and the premium for stop-loss insurance that caps your downside if claims run hot. In a healthy year, the money you funded but did not spend can come back to you. The carrier or insurer no longer pockets your good year by default.

That structure is the same no matter who builds it. The fight is over who builds it, and what they let you keep, see, and control while they do.

## Same label, two different machines

When a generalist broker says level-funded, he almost always means a packaged product from one of the big medical carriers. Cigna, UnitedHealthcare, and the Blues all sell one. They are real plans, they are ACA-compliant, and under federal law they sit on the self-funded side with the employer carrying claims risk inside the stop-loss limits. UnitedHealthcare publicly markets its level-funded product as costing employers roughly 22 percent less on average than a comparable fully insured plan. That is not nothing.

The other machine is an independent level-funded program. Think of administrators like Gravie or Benecon. Same three-part funding structure, same stop-loss safety net, but the program is assembled from parts the employer can actually inspect and swap, rather than welded shut inside one carrier's economics.

The distinction matters in four specific places.

## One: who can see the claims data, and how clearly

Both kinds of plan are technically self-funded, which means the employer is supposed to own the claims data either way. The difference is how usable that data is when it reaches you.

A carrier-administered level-funded plan tends to hand you summary reporting. You see what they choose to format for you. The pharmacy spend in particular often sits carved in, meaning drug pricing, rebates, and utilization trends stay folded inside the medical plan's reporting. One vendor, one bill, looks clean. The cost of that clean look is that you cannot see what your drugs actually cost or where the rebate dollars went.

Independent programs lean the other direction. Self-funded employers working through an independent administrator typically receive the full set of reports, and the program is built so you can read the cost drivers month over month: emergency room usage, prescription patterns, the handful of claims actually steering your renewal. You manage what you can see. You cannot manage what the carrier formats away.

## Two: whether you can plug in your own pharmacy manager and cost-containment tools

This is where the gap gets expensive. Pharmacy is one of the fastest-growing lines in any health plan.

A carrier product generally arrives with the carrier's pharmacy benefit manager already bundled in. You take their PBM, their formulary, their network economics, as a package. The rebates and spread inside that arrangement are hard to unbundle and harder to audit. The big carriers earn real money on the administrative and pharmacy side of these plans, and a bundled design is what protects that.

An independent program lets you carve the pharmacy out and run a transparent PBM, the kind that passes through rebates and charges a clear administrative fee instead of hiding margin in the spread. You can also layer in independent cost-containment tools and shop the stop-loss separately. Even the independents move their pharmacy arrangements around. Gravie, for example, shifted its PBM to Express Scripts effective January 2026. The point is not which PBM. It is that an independent structure is built to be unbundled and re-shopped, where the carrier structure is built to stay sealed.

## Three: how much of a healthy year you actually get back

Surplus return is where the brochures blur together and the contracts do not.

In an independent program, the surplus terms are a feature they advertise and will put in writing. Benecon's program, as one example, is structured so the group retains and controls 100 percent of the claim-fund dollars it does not spend. That is the whole pitch: a healthy year is yours.

Carrier level-funded products do return surplus too, but the terms vary and the fine print earns its keep. Some return a portion rather than the full amount. Some hand it back as a credit toward next year's funding rather than cash. One more wrinkle applies to everyone, independent or carrier: if employee contributions funded part of the premium, ERISA may treat some of that surplus as plan assets, which means a slice has to flow back to participants rather than to the school's general budget. A program built around surplus return tends to handle that cleanly. A product where surplus is an afterthought tends to handle it quietly.

## Four: portability and lock-in

A carrier product ties your plan to that carrier's network and PBM economics. Leaving means rebuilding the whole arrangement somewhere else. An independent program is assembled from interchangeable parts, so a network change or an administrator change does not force you to tear the entire plan down. You keep your structure and move the pieces that are underperforming.

## The fair part, because pretending there are no tradeoffs is how brokers lie

The big carriers earn their scale honestly in two areas, and a school should weigh them.

Network breadth is the first. The national carriers have the widest provider networks in the country, and for a school with staff spread across a region, that reach has real value. An independent program has to demonstrate that its network covers your people before you trust it.

Simplicity is the second. One carrier, one bill, one phone number is genuinely easier to administer than a program with separately chosen parts. That ease is worth something to a small school office, and it is the honest appeal of the packaged product.

So the verdict here is a point of view, not a law of physics. In Aaron's experience placing these plans, the independents take the cake for a charter school often enough that they should never be left off the table. The transparency, the swappable PBM, the cleaner surplus terms, and the lack of lock-in tend to outrun the carrier's network-and-simplicity advantage once you run the actual numbers. Reasonable people can land differently on a given case. The real problem is that most schools never get to choose at all.

## Why you only ever saw one of them

A generalist broker sells the carrier's level-funded product for the same reason water runs downhill. It is the easy one. The carrier has a quoting portal he already knows, a sales rep who feeds him the proposal, and a familiar name the board will recognize. He clicks three buttons and looks like he did something modern.

Showing you an independent program is work. He would have to know the administrators, understand transparent pharmacy, read a stop-loss contract he did not get handed, and compare surplus terms line by line. That is hours he does not bill for on an account he already thinks is too small. So he shows you the carrier box, calls it level funding, and lets you assume that is the whole category.

It is not the whole category. It is the easy corner of it.

## Before you sign whatever he put in front of you

You do not have to become a benefits expert to protect yourself here. You just have to ask the one question that reveals whether you were shown the market or shown the shortcut.

So sit with this. When your broker brought you a level-funded plan, did he also bring you an independent program to compare it against, walk you through who controls the pharmacy and the data, and show you in writing how much of a healthy year actually comes back to your budget? Or did one carrier's product show up alone, dressed as the entire option set?

If only one machine ever crossed your desk, you were not given a choice. You were given a default. And a default is a fine thing to accept on purpose and an expensive thing to accept by accident.
