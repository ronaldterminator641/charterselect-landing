## A plan is not a strategy

Most charter schools are handed a finished thing. One carrier, one network, one pharmacy manager, one set of rules, all welded together and sold as a single package. It is easy to buy. It renews itself. And it quietly costs your school more every year while handing your staff care that is slower and shallower than it should be.

The schools that break out of that cycle stop buying a bundle and start assembling a stack. Every piece gets chosen on its own merits, priced on its own, and swapped out when a better piece exists. Then they do the part nearly everyone skips. They wrap the whole thing in a support layer, so a regular teacher can use it without a benefits degree. This is the blueprint, the full build, top to bottom.

## The base: own the risk, then pool it

The foundation is the funding model. A fully insured plan hands your premium to a carrier, who keeps whatever your staff don't spend. A self-funded or level-funded plan flips that. Your school pays for the actual claims your people generate, with stop-loss insurance standing behind you to cap the catastrophic ones. In a good year, the savings stay with your school instead of padding a carrier's margin. The step up from there is a captive: a group of self-funded employers who pool their stop-loss risk together so no one school eats a brutal claims year alone. You get the cost control of self-funding with the stability of a much larger group. The base layer is a level-funded or self-funded plan with a clear path toward a captive as you grow.

## Unbundle it, then rent what you need

This is where the bundle breaks open. In a self-funded plan, you do not have to buy the carrier's all-in-one package. You unbundle it. You hire each function separately and keep only the ones that serve you.

The administrator is the engine room. An independent Third Party Administrator, a TPA that is not owned by a carrier or a hospital system, processes your claims and runs your plan while answering to you instead of to a parent insurer. Boon-Chapman is a clean example of that category. It has administered self-funded plans since 1961, the oldest TPA in Texas, and it has stayed deliberately independent of carrier and hospital ownership, which is the whole point: it is structurally free to act in the plan's interest because no carrier signs its paycheck. That independence is what unbundling actually buys you. You keep control of plan design. You get real access to your own claims data instead of a carrier's filtered summary. And you can plug specialized vendors straight into the plan, because an independent TPA is built to integrate and exchange data with outside partners rather than defaulting to whatever the carrier ships in the box.

The network is the next piece, and you do not have to marry a carrier to get one. You rent a national network through your TPA, so your staff keep broad provider access and in-network discounts without surrendering control of everything else to the carrier that owns it. Pair that rented network with reference-based pricing and price-transparency tools, which set fair payment limits against a public benchmark like Medicare rates instead of accepting a hospital's mystery chargemaster. Published savings on these models run real, often in the range of fifteen to thirty percent against traditional PPO claims, though the exact number depends on the group.

## The pharmacy: hire a PBM that works for you

I covered the pharmacy games in depth in the advanced-employee-care paper, so I will keep this short. Your drug benefit runs through a Pharmacy Benefit Manager. Most traditional PBMs profit from spread pricing and from keeping manufacturer rebates, which means the middleman wins when your plan overpays. The fix is an independent, transparent, pass-through PBM that charges a disclosed fee, passes the real drug cost straight through, and returns the rebates to your plan. Carved out and chosen on its own, not bundled in, it is one of the fastest ways to stop the bleeding at the pharmacy counter.

## The plan design: pay for primary care first

Now the design layer, the part your staff feel every week. The traditional plan lets everyone wander into the most expensive specialist and the most expensive facility by default. A high-value, accountable-care design points the other way. It anchors each member to a coordinated primary-care team that quarterbacks their care.

Centivo is a good example of this model in action. It is a self-funded health plan built around a primary-care-based Accountable Care Organization, with its own virtual primary care practice that earned the National Committee for Quality Assurance's Patient-Centered Medical Home recognition. The mechanism is worth understanding, because it explains why the savings are real and not a slogan. Centivo makes primary care free at the point of use. Members pay $0 to see their primary care team, and the result is that they use it, reportedly around 40 percent more than members on a traditional plan. More primary care up front means problems get caught early, fewer unnecessary specialist referrals, fewer avoidable imaging studies, fewer ER trips. Layer that on lower unit costs negotiated directly with high-value health systems, and Centivo reports savings commonly in the range of twenty percent or more versus traditional carriers, with one employer-coalition partnership reporting 23 to 36 percent. The logic is the same one behind Direct Primary Care, which I covered in the advanced-employee-care paper. Fund the front door of medicine, and the expensive failures downstream stop happening.

That is the cost-containment stack assembled. Independent TPA, rented network, reference-based pricing, transparent PBM, primary-care-anchored design, all sitting on a self-funded base headed toward a captive. Each piece earns its place. None of it is reserved for giant employers.

## The part nobody builds: protecting the members and the fund

A stack like this gives you control. Control means you are now responsible for things a carrier used to quietly handle, and getting them wrong is expensive. Two member populations decide whether your fund stays healthy.

**Medicare-eligible active employees.** When a teacher or staff member is 65 or older and still actively working, federal Medicare Secondary Payer rules decide who pays first. If your school has 20 or more employees, your group health plan pays primary and Medicare pays secondary. You cannot take that worker's Medicare eligibility into account, cannot push them off your plan, and must offer them the same benefits under the same conditions as everyone else. If you have fewer than 20 employees, Medicare generally pays primary. On a self-funded plan, getting this backwards is not a paperwork slip. It means your fund is paying claims Medicare should have covered, or denying claims it owes, and both carry real penalties and recovery actions. Someone has to get the order right and keep it right as your headcount moves across that threshold.

**COBRA continuants.** When an employee leaves, federal COBRA rules let them stay on your plan, and you can charge up to 102 percent of the full cost of coverage, the employee and employer share combined plus a two percent administrative load. On a fully insured plan, that is the carrier's problem. On a self-funded plan, it is yours, and there is a sharp edge here. The people who choose to keep paying that full freight are disproportionately the ones with active, expensive medical needs. That is adverse selection, and it can load your fund with high-cost claimants who no longer even work for you. State "mini-COBRA" laws that soften this for small fully insured groups do not apply to a self-funded plan, so the exposure sits squarely on your fund. The protection is managing it deliberately: tracking continuants, pricing COBRA so the fund is not quietly subsidizing it, and structuring your stop-loss so a known high-cost claimant is handled correctly rather than draining the plan in silence. Stop-loss tools like individual lasers exist for exactly these situations, and they have to be set with eyes open at renewal.

This is the layer a generalist never touches, because a bundled carrier absorbed it invisibly and the broker never had to learn it. Once you own the risk, somebody who actually understands it has to stand guard over it.

## The high-touch layer: support people through the hardest moments

Cost control protects the fund. The next pieces protect the human being. These are the point solutions and advocacy services that turn a plan into actual care, and the unbundled TPA above is what lets you plug them in.

Specialized support for a serious diagnosis is the clearest example. Aaron points schools toward an oncology-support benefit, the kind he describes as a CancerCARE-style program. The mechanism is what makes the category worth evaluating. A credible example is AccessHope, built on partnerships with NCI-Designated Comprehensive Cancer Centers like City of Hope, Dana-Farber, and Fred Hutchinson. When a staff member hears the word cancer, a program like this connects that member's own treating oncologist with sub-specialist expertise from one of those centers, remotely, so the patient does not have to relocate to get a top-tier read on the case. Why this matters for both the member and the fund shows up in AccessHope's own reported numbers: their experts recommended improvements to the treatment plan in more than 90 percent of cases reviewed, and those recommendations were adopted into care about 75 percent of the time. Serious diagnoses drive a large share of any plan's spend, so getting the diagnosis and the treatment right at the start protects the member's life and the fund's balance in the same motion.

Care navigation and advocacy is the everyday version of the same idea. Aaron describes this as a CareGuide-style layer. The category, exemplified by firms like Included Health and Health Advocate, gives every member one number to call and a real person on the other end. That person becomes the single point of entry for the whole confusing system. They steer members toward genuinely high-quality providers, the kind of steerage that matters because prices for the same procedure in the same town can differ by as much as 400 percent. They redirect care to the right site, untangle a baffling bill, and answer the questions that otherwise end in a panicked, overpriced ER visit. Steer one imaging study or outpatient procedure to the right facility and you can save hundreds to a couple thousand dollars on that single claim, and your member gets a guide instead of a maze.

## The delivery layer: none of it matters if nobody uses it

You can build the best stack in the country and waste it if your staff never understand what they have. I went deep on this in the open-enrollment-modernization paper, so here is the short version. Educate at open enrollment, in formats people actually open, well before the deadline. Run one-on-one meetings so a teacher with a new baby and a teacher nearing retirement each pick the plan that fits their real life. And put the consumerism tools directly in members' hands, the navigation number, the transparency tool, the decision support, so that in the moment they need care, they are pointed toward the high-value provider and the right site without having to figure it out alone. A benefit nobody understands is money you spent for resentment.

## Why your school doesn't have this

Every piece I just described is available to a school your size right now. Independent TPAs, rented networks, transparent PBMs, primary-care-anchored designs, oncology and navigation point solutions, real enrollment support. None of it requires being a Fortune 500 company.

So why doesn't your school have it? Because assembling a stack and standing up a support layer is a different job than selling a renewal. A generalist broker who carries restaurants and law firms and your school in the same book shows up once a year with the same bundle and a higher premium, walks you through the increase, and calls it service. That broker will never carve out a PBM, vet an independent TPA, rent and pair a network with reference-based pricing, anchor the design on primary care, set up the Medicare order of payment, manage COBRA adverse selection against your stop-loss, or wire in a cancer-support program. The job they signed up for is pressing the easy button before the deadline. Building this is slow, technical, ongoing work. It only happens when someone treats your school as a real client instead of a file to clear off the desk.

## Before your next renewal

You do not need to memorize any of this. You need to look honestly at one thing.

When you think about the plan your staff have right now, can you name who built each piece of it, who is watching the Medicare order and the COBRA exposure so your fund is not quietly paying claims it should not, and who a teacher actually calls the day she is handed a serious diagnosis? Or did the whole thing just renew itself again while everyone was busy running a school?

If you cannot answer that with confidence, you do not have a benefits problem yet. You have a stack nobody assembled and a support layer nobody built. Both can be built. The only question is whether anyone on your account ever intended to.
