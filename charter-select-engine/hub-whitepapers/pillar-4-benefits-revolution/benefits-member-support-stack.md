## A plan is not a strategy

Most charter schools are handed a finished thing. One carrier, one network, one pharmacy manager, one set of rules, all welded together and sold as a single package. It's easy to buy. It renews itself. And it quietly costs your school more every year while delivering your staff care that's slower and shallower than it should be.

The schools that break out of that cycle do something different. They stop buying a bundle and start assembling a stack. Every piece gets chosen on its own merits, priced on its own, and swapped out when a better piece exists. Then, and this is the part almost everyone skips, they wrap the whole thing in a support layer so a regular teacher can actually use it without a benefits degree.

I've written about the individual pieces elsewhere. This is where they come together. Think of this as the blueprint, the full build, top to bottom.

## The base: own the risk, then pool it

The foundation is the funding model. A fully insured plan hands your premium to a carrier, who keeps whatever your staff don't spend. A self-funded or level-funded plan flips that. Your school pays for the actual claims your people generate, with stop-loss insurance standing behind you to cap the catastrophic ones. In a good year, the savings stay with your school instead of padding a carrier's margin.

For a single charter school, the next step up is a captive: a group of self-funded employers who pool their stop-loss risk together so no one school eats a brutal claims year alone. You get the cost control of self-funding with the stability of a much larger group. The base layer, then, is a level-funded or self-funded plan with a clear path toward a captive as you grow.

## Unbundle it, then rent what you need

Here's where the bundle quietly breaks open. In a self-funded plan, you don't have to buy the carrier's all-in-one package. You unbundle it. You hire each function separately and keep the ones that serve you.

The administrator is the engine room. An independent Third Party Administrator, a TPA that is not owned by a carrier or a hospital system, processes your claims and runs your plan while answering to you instead of to a parent insurer. Boon-Chapman is one example of that independent-TPA category, the kind of firm built to administer a self-funded plan without a carrier's incentives baked in. Because they're unbundled, you keep control of plan design, you get real access to your own claims data, and you can plug in specialized vendors instead of accepting whatever the carrier ships in the box.

The network is the next piece, and you don't have to marry a carrier to get one. You can rent a national network through your TPA so your staff still get broad provider access and in-network discounts, without surrendering control of everything else to the carrier that owns it. Pair that with reference-based pricing and price-transparency tools, which set fair payment limits against a public benchmark like Medicare rates instead of accepting a hospital's mystery chargemaster. The published savings on these models run real, often in the range of fifteen to thirty percent against traditional PPO claims, though the exact number depends on the group.

## The pharmacy: hire a PBM that works for you

I covered the pharmacy games in depth in another paper, so I'll keep this short. Your drug benefit runs through a Pharmacy Benefit Manager. Most traditional PBMs profit from spread pricing and from keeping manufacturer rebates, which means the middleman wins when your plan overpays. The fix is an independent, transparent, pass-through PBM that charges a disclosed fee, passes the real drug cost straight through, and returns the rebates to your plan. Carved out and chosen on its own, not bundled in, it's one of the fastest ways to stop the bleeding at the pharmacy counter.

## The plan design: pay for primary care first

Now the design layer, the part your staff feel every week. ACO-style, high-value plan designs steer members toward a coordinated primary-care team that quarterbacks their care, instead of letting everyone wander into the most expensive specialist and facility by default. Centivo is one example of that model, a self-funded health plan built around a primary-care-first, accountable-care structure. The logic is the same one behind Direct Primary Care: catch problems early, reduce unnecessary specialist visits and ER trips, and the savings follow. Published results on these designs commonly land in that same fifteen-to-thirty-percent range versus a traditional fully insured plan.

That's the cost-containment stack assembled. Independent TPA, rented network, reference-based pricing, transparent PBM, ACO-style design, sitting on a self-funded base headed toward a captive. Each piece earns its place. None of it is reserved for giant employers.

## The part nobody builds: protecting the members and the fund

A stack like this gives you control. Control means you're now responsible for things a carrier used to quietly handle, and getting them wrong is expensive. Two member populations decide whether your fund stays healthy.

**Medicare-eligible active employees.** When a teacher or staff member is 65 or older and still actively working, federal Medicare Secondary Payer rules decide who pays first. If your school has 20 or more employees, your group health plan pays primary and Medicare pays secondary, and you cannot push that worker off your plan or treat them differently because they qualify for Medicare. If you have fewer than 20, Medicare generally pays primary. On a self-funded plan, getting this backwards isn't a paperwork slip. It means your fund is illegally paying claims that Medicare should have covered, or denying claims it owes, both of which carry real penalties and recovery actions. Someone has to get the order right and keep it right.

**COBRA continuants.** When an employee leaves, federal COBRA rules let them stay on your plan, paying up to 102 percent of the full premium. On a fully insured plan, that's the carrier's problem. On a self-funded plan, it's yours, and there's a sharp edge here. The people who choose to keep paying for COBRA are disproportionately the ones with active, expensive medical needs. That's adverse selection, and it can load your fund with high-cost claimants who are no longer even working for you. The protection is managing this deliberately: tracking continuants, structuring your stop-loss so a known high-cost claimant is handled correctly rather than quietly draining the plan, and pricing COBRA right so the fund isn't subsidizing it. Stop-loss tools like individual lasers exist exactly for these situations, and they have to be set with eyes open at renewal.

This is the layer a generalist never touches, because a bundled carrier absorbed it invisibly and the broker never had to learn it. Once you own the risk, somebody who actually understands it has to stand guard over it.

## The high-touch layer: support people through the hardest moments

Cost control protects the fund. The next pieces protect the human being. These are the point solutions and advocacy services that turn a plan into actual care.

Specialized support for a serious diagnosis is the clearest example. When a staff member hears the word cancer, a dedicated oncology support program, the category I'd point a school toward, something like a CancerCARE-style benefit, gives that member and their treating doctor remote access to top-tier cancer expertise, expert second opinions, and help mapping out the treatment plan. Serious diagnoses drive a large share of any plan's spend, so getting the diagnosis and the treatment right early protects both the member's life and the fund's balance. Better care and lower cost point the same direction here.

Care navigation and advocacy is the everyday version of the same idea. A navigation and advocacy service, the kind I'd describe as a CareGuide-style layer, gives every member one number to call and a real person on the other end. That person helps them find a high-quality in-network provider, steers them to the right site of care, untangles a confusing bill, and answers the questions that otherwise end in a panicked, overpriced ER visit. Steering one imaging study or outpatient procedure to the right facility can save hundreds to a couple thousand dollars on that single claim, and your member gets a guide instead of a maze.

## The delivery layer: none of it matters if nobody uses it

You can build the best stack in the country and waste it if your staff never understand what they have. The delivery layer is what makes the whole thing land.

Educate at open enrollment, in formats people actually open, well before the deadline. Do one-on-one enrollment meetings so a teacher with a new baby and a teacher nearing retirement each pick the plan that fits their real life. And give members built-in tools that guide smart consumerism, the navigation app, the transparency tool, the single phone number, so that in the moment they need care, they're pointed toward the high-value provider and the right site of care without having to figure it out alone. I went deep on this in the open-enrollment paper. The short version: a benefit nobody understands is money you spent for resentment.

## Why your school doesn't have this

Every piece I just described is available to a school your size right now. Independent TPAs, rented networks, transparent PBMs, ACO designs, oncology and navigation point solutions, real enrollment support. None of it requires being a Fortune 500 company.

So why doesn't your school have it? Because assembling a stack and standing up a support layer is a different job than selling a renewal. A generalist broker who carries restaurants and law firms and your school in the same book shows up once a year with the same bundle and a higher premium, walks you through the increase, and calls it service. That broker will never carve out a PBM, vet a TPA, set up MSP coordination, manage COBRA adverse selection against your stop-loss, or wire in a cancer-support program. That work was never part of the job they signed up for. The job they signed up for is pressing the easy button before the deadline.

Building this is slow, technical, ongoing work. It only happens when someone treats your school as a real client instead of a file to clear off the desk.

## Before your next renewal

You don't need to memorize any of this. You need to look honestly at one thing.

When you think about the plan your staff have right now, can you name who built each piece of it, who's watching the Medicare and COBRA rules so your fund isn't quietly paying claims it shouldn't, and who a teacher actually calls the day she's handed a serious diagnosis? Or did the whole thing just renew itself again while everyone was busy running a school?

If you can't answer that with confidence, you don't have a benefits problem yet. You have a stack nobody assembled and a support layer nobody built. The good news is that both can be built. The question is whether anyone on your account ever intended to.
