# The Health Plan That Eventually Owns Its Own Insurance Company

*A plain-English look at group medical stop-loss captives: the funding endgame for a charter school health plan, the path that gets you there safely, the real players who built the model, and the honest risk a captive can absolutely go wrong.*

---

Two charter schools, same size, same town, both self-funded.

One hands its stop-loss premium to a carrier every year and watches it disappear into someone else's profit. The other pools that same layer of risk with a few dozen employers like it. In a healthy year, a check comes back with the school's name on it, plus a share of the interest the money earned while it sat in reserve.

Same plan underneath. Same employees, same claims, same coverage. The only difference is who owns the insurance company sitting on top of it.

## Where this fits, and where it doesn't

You may have read the other two papers in this set. One walks the funding spectrum from fully insured to level-funded to captives. The other reads the stop-loss contract line by line. This paper goes deeper on the destination itself. A group medical stop-loss captive is, for the right school, the endgame. Let me show you what it actually is, who built the model worth joining, how you get there without getting hurt, and where it breaks if you skip the homework.

## What a group stop-loss captive actually is

Start with what sits underneath, because a captive does not replace your plan. It sits on top of it.

Your school self-funds. You pay your employees' medical claims out of your own claims account instead of buying a fixed premium and handing the risk to a carrier. To cap the downside, you buy stop-loss insurance, which reimburses you when one person's claims or the whole group's claims blow past a set point. That self-funded structure is what most schools should reach first.

A group captive changes who carries the stop-loss layer. Instead of buying it from a carrier that keeps every dollar of profit, a group of similar employers form a member-owned insurance entity and pool a slice of their stop-loss risk inside it. Picture it in layers. Each school funds its routine claims at the bottom, up to a set specific deductible. Above that sits a shared middle layer inside the captive, where mid-sized claims get pooled across all the members, so one school's rough year is cushioned by the others. Above that, the captive buys reinsurance to catch the truly catastrophic claims no pool wants to carry alone.

Here is the part fully insured never gives you. If the pooled layer collects more than it pays out, that underwriting surplus comes back to the members, generally pro rata based on what each put in, plus the investment income the money earned while it sat in reserve. You stop being a premium payer and start being an owner who shares in the result and the data. Roundstone, which pioneered the group medical captive model in 2005, describes the captive layer as a shock absorber that smooths the volatility of mid-sized claims while reinsurance handles the catastrophic end. That is an accurate way to hold it in your head.

## The founder's path: level-funded first, captive as the goal

I do not put a school into a captive on day one. I think that is reckless, and I think most brokers who do it are chasing a story instead of protecting a client.

The path I believe in starts with level-funded. You step off fully insured into a plan where a healthy year pays you back, you get real monthly claims data for the first time, and you prove out your own claims story over a year or two with the budget predictability of a set monthly payment. Stop-loss is still doing the heavy lifting, so your downside stays capped close to what you already budgeted while you get comfortable being a self-funded employer.

Then, when the data says you are a good risk and your leadership is comfortable, you graduate into a captive. That is the endgame for the right school: a clean claims history, the cash position to ride a swing, and an appetite for the ownership upside and leverage of a member pool. Going level-funded first is not a detour. It is the runway that lets you walk in with your eyes open and your numbers proven. Every serious captive operator says the same thing in their own materials: the employers who do best join with detailed claims data, engaged leadership, and a genuine multi-year commitment, not the ones who jump in cold.

## The players who built this, and what each one stands for

The operators in this space are not all the same kind of thing. Some are captives you join. Some are consultants who advise the structure. Here are the ones worth naming and the principle each one champions.

**ParetoHealth** applies the member-owned group self-insurance idea to healthcare and runs it at scale. Its structure, which it calls a Risk Shield, stacks the same three layers: each employer carries its claims up to a specific deductible, the Pareto captive layer absorbs the mid-sized claims across the membership, and stop-loss reinsurance catches the catastrophic end. Pareto pools employers in roughly the fifty to a thousand employee band, which is exactly where most charter schools sit, and that scale is the point. A bigger pool means real negotiating leverage with carriers and vendors, more stability when one member has a brutal year, and group purchasing power no single school could touch alone. Pareto leans hard into education and data transparency too, with members getting claims data and pharmacy rebate visibility so they see where the dollars go. In my experience, Pareto is the best of the group. That is my opinion as someone who has placed this coverage, not a guarantee, and you should pressure-test it against your own situation.

**Captive Resources** is the consultant model, not a captive you join directly. It advises and helps manage member-owned group captives, and the principle it has championed for decades is genuine member ownership. The members own the captive outright, each gets a seat and a vote on the board regardless of premium size, and member committees oversee the real levers: underwriting, investment, and risk control. Premiums are built off each member's own loss experience rather than a blended rate, and unused loss funds come back as dividends, along with the investment income earned on them. The whole philosophy ties dollars to behavior: control your losses, keep more of your own money.

**Roundstone** pioneered the group medical captive in 2005 and was the first to open the model to employers as small as twenty-five lives. Its banner is transparency: transparent claims data so you can find your own cost drivers, transparent pharmacy, and surplus returned to members as cash distributions when the pooled layer runs ahead of claims. Roundstone built the small-and-midsize on-ramp the rest of the field now competes on.

**Evergreen** works the alternative-funding and captive mid-market for the same fifty to five hundred employee band, building multi-year benefit strategies off deep claims analysis rather than one-year renewals. Its positioning is the long game: the plan as a project you actively manage, not a number you re-quote every fall.

The point of naming them is not to crown a winner for your school sight unseen. It is to show you the field is mature and the same principles run through all of it: member ownership, surplus and investment income flowing back to members, transparent data, and loss control as the thing that earns the return. Which one fits depends on your size, your state, and your appetite, and that is a conversation, not a brochure.

## Be honest: a captive can go wrong

Anybody who sells you a captive as guaranteed savings is lying to you. A captive moves real risk onto your school. In a genuinely tough claims year, your school can owe more than its best case, in the form of additional contribution to the pooled layer, before reinsurance takes over. The ceiling is defined in advance, which is the whole point of the structure, but it is higher than a flat fully insured premium in a bad year. If your staff hit a brutal run of claims and the program around you is sloppy, a captive can cost you more, not less.

So before I put a school anywhere near one, three guardrails are non-negotiable.

**A tight plan document.** Your plan document is the legal rulebook for what gets paid, who is eligible, and on what terms. In a self-funded captive, every dollar paid out is partly your dollar and partly the pool's. A loose or outdated plan document means claims get paid that should have been questioned, and that leakage comes straight out of your result and your fellow members' surplus. A document drafted and maintained on purpose is the difference between a plan that controls its own spend and one that pays whatever lands on the desk.

**An independent PBM.** Pharmacy is one of the fastest-rising costs in any health plan, and the pharmacy benefit manager controls a huge share of it. A PBM bundled in by the carrier can profit off spread pricing and retained rebates, which means it makes more when your plan spends more. An independent, pass-through PBM passes the actual drug cost through and charges a flat fee, so its incentives line up with yours. This is exactly why the better captives push pharmacy transparency and rebate visibility as a core feature. In a structure where you keep the savings, an independent PBM is one of the strongest levers you have.

**Real cost-control mechanisms.** A captive without active cost containment is a pool waiting to overspend. The whole member-owned premise is that controlling your losses is what earns the dividend back, so the controls are not optional. That means care management on high-cost claimants, specialty drug strategies, network and reference-based pricing decisions, and the freedom to carve out vendors and switch them without rebuilding the whole plan. These limit the damage when your staff hit a hard stretch, and the model is built to let you use them. The school that joins a captive and runs it passively gets the risk without the controls that justify taking it.

## Why your generalist never built the runway

Here is the broker behavior that keeps schools stuck. The generalist who runs benefits as a side dish does not build the path from level-funded to captive, because that path is work. It means analyzing claims data every month, proving out a story over years, drafting a tight plan document, carving out a real PBM, and managing the program instead of mailing a renewal.

So they keep you fully insured and pass along the increase, because re-quoting the same plan is the easy button. They will tell you a captive is too advanced for a school your size, when the operators who built this model open it to employers as small as twenty-five lives. The runway exists. They just never bothered to build it, and you were never told it was an option.

## Before you renew the same plan one more time

You do not have to commit to a captive to find out whether one is in your future. You need someone willing to start the runway and show you the numbers at each step.

So sit with this one.

Has anyone ever laid out a multi-year path for your school, level-funded first to prove your claims story, then a captive as the goal, and shown you in dollars what the ownership upside and the honest downside would look like at each stage? Or has every renewal just been the same fully insured plan with a bigger number on it, because building the path was more work than your broker wanted to do?

If the honest answer is the second one, you do not have a benefits problem yet. You have a destination nobody ever offered to drive you toward.
