# The Health Plan That Eventually Owns Its Own Insurance Company

*A plain-English look at group medical stop-loss captives: the funding endgame for a charter school health plan, the path that gets you there safely, the players worth knowing, and the honest risk a captive can absolutely go wrong.*

---

Picture two charter schools, same size, same town, both self-funded.

One of them spends years handing its stop-loss premium to a carrier and watching it disappear. The other one pools that same layer of risk with a few dozen schools and employers like it, and in a healthy year a check comes back with its name on it, plus a share of the interest earned on the money while it sat in reserve.

Same plan underneath. The difference is who owns the insurance company.

## What a group stop-loss captive actually is

Start with what sits underneath, because a captive does not replace your plan. It sits on top of it.

Your school self-funds. You pay your employees' medical claims out of your own claims account instead of buying a fixed premium and handing the risk to a carrier. To cap the downside, you buy stop-loss insurance. Specific stop-loss reimburses you when any one person's claims blow past a set deductible. Aggregate stop-loss catches the year where the whole pool runs over budget. That structure is the self-funded plan most schools should reach first.

A group captive changes who carries the stop-loss layer. Instead of buying that coverage from a traditional carrier that keeps every dollar of profit, a group of similar employers form a member-owned insurance entity, a captive, and pool a slice of their stop-loss risk together inside it. You can picture it in layers. Each school still funds its own routine claims at the bottom. Above that sits a shared middle layer inside the captive, where mid-sized claims get pooled across all the members, so one school's rough year is cushioned by the others. Above that, the captive buys reinsurance to catch the truly catastrophic claims that no pool wants to carry alone.

Here is the part fully insured never gives you. If the pooled layer collects more than it pays out in claims, that underwriting surplus comes back to the members, generally pro rata based on what they put in. The money also earns investment income while it waits in reserve, and that income flows to the members too. You stop being a premium payer and start being an owner who shares in the result and shares in the data. Roundstone, which pioneered the group medical captive model around 2005, frames the captive as a shock absorber that smooths the volatility of mid-sized claims while reinsurance handles the catastrophic end. That is an accurate way to hold it in your head.

## The founder's path: level-funded first, captive as the goal

I do not put a school into a captive on day one. I think that is reckless, and I think most brokers who do it are chasing a story instead of protecting a client.

The path I believe in starts with level-funded. You take the step off fully insured into a plan where a healthy year pays you back, you get real monthly claims data for the first time, and you prove out your own claims story over a year or two with the budget predictability of a set monthly payment. You learn what is actually driving your costs. You get comfortable being a self-funded employer in a low-stakes way, because stop-loss is still doing the heavy lifting and your downside is capped close to what you already budgeted.

Then, when the data says you are a good risk and your leadership is comfortable, you graduate into a captive. That is the endgame for the right school. The school that has shown a clean, manageable claims history, has the cash position to ride a swing, and wants the ownership upside and the leverage that comes with being part of a member pool. Going level-funded first is not a detour. It is the runway that lets you walk into a captive with your eyes open and your numbers proven.

## Be honest: a captive can go wrong

Anybody who sells you a captive as guaranteed savings is lying to you. A captive moves real risk onto your school. In a genuinely tough claims year, your school can owe more than its best case, in the form of additional contribution to the pooled layer, before reinsurance takes over. The ceiling is defined in advance, which is the whole point of the structure, but it is higher than a flat fully insured premium in a bad year. If your staff have a brutal run of claims and the program around you is sloppy, a captive can turn into a place that costs you more, not less.

So before I put a school anywhere near one, three guardrails are non-negotiable.

**A tight plan document.** Your plan document is the legal rulebook for what gets paid, who is eligible, and on what terms. In a self-funded captive world, every dollar paid out is partly your dollar and partly the pool's. A loose, vague, or outdated plan document means claims get paid that should have been questioned, and that leakage comes straight out of your result and your members' surplus. A tight document, drafted and maintained on purpose, is the difference between a plan that controls its own spend and one that pays whatever lands on the desk.

**An independent PBM.** Pharmacy is one of the fastest-rising costs in any health plan, and the pharmacy benefit manager controls a huge share of it. A PBM bundled in by the carrier or the administrator can profit off spread pricing and retained rebates, which means it makes more when your plan spends more. An independent, transparent, pass-through PBM passes the actual drug cost through and charges a flat fee instead, so its incentives line up with yours. In a captive, where you keep the savings, an independent PBM is one of the strongest levers you have. A captive-the-carrier-owns version of this, with their PBM baked in, quietly works against you.

**Real cost-control mechanisms.** A captive without active cost containment is a pool waiting to overspend. That means tools to manage the genuinely tough claims year: care management on high-cost claimants, specialty drug strategies, network and reference-based pricing decisions, and the freedom to carve out vendors and switch them without rebuilding the whole plan. These are what limit the damage when your staff hit a hard stretch, and the captive model is built to let you use them. The school that joins a captive and then runs it passively gets the risk without the controls that justify taking it.

## Why your generalist never built the runway

Here is the broker behavior that keeps schools stuck. The generalist who runs benefits as a side dish to everything else does not build the path from level-funded to captive, because that path is work. It means analyzing claims data every month, proving out a story over years, drafting a tight plan document, carving out a real PBM, and managing the program instead of mailing a renewal.

So they keep you fully insured and pass along the increase, because re-quoting the same plan every year is the easy button. They will tell you a captive is too advanced for a school your size, when the truth is it is too much work for a broker your size was never worth to them. The runway exists. They just never bothered to build it, and you were never told it was an option.

## Before you renew the same plan one more time

You do not have to commit to a captive to find out whether one is in your future. You need someone willing to start the runway and show you the numbers at each step.

So sit with this one:

Has anyone ever laid out a multi-year path for your school, level-funded first to prove your claims story, then a captive as the goal, and shown you in dollars what the ownership upside and the honest downside would look like at each stage? Or has every renewal just been the same fully insured plan with a bigger number on it, because building the path was more work than your broker wanted to do?

If the honest answer is the second one, you do not have a benefits problem yet. You have a destination nobody ever offered to drive you toward.
