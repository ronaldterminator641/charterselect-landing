# Your Workers' Comp Bill Has a Credit Score Built Into It, and Nobody at Your School Has Ever Checked It for Errors

*Two silent, expensive problems quietly inflate workers' comp premiums for charter schools: staff filed under the wrong job codes, and an experience modifier nobody manages. Here is how the number actually gets built, and the levers you control.*

---

Two schools, same size, same number of teachers, same payroll. One pays a workers' comp premium that is meaningfully higher than the other. Year after year.

Nothing about the building is different. The staff aren't more accident-prone. The work is identical.

The gap is a number most school leaders have never looked at, sitting on a worksheet they've never been handed.

---

## The number nobody hands you

You sign a workers' comp renewal the same way you sign everything else that arrives once a year. You check that the premium didn't lurch upward, confirm the headcount looks about right, and move on. There's a school to run.

Underneath that premium is a calculation with three moving parts, and you control more of it than anyone has told you.

Workers' comp premium starts with payroll. Then it sorts that payroll into job classification codes, each with its own rate, because the risk of injury for someone running a table saw is not the risk for someone grading papers. Then it takes one more step that catches everyone off guard. It multiplies the whole thing by a factor built from your own past claims. That factor is the experience modification, the e-mod, and it behaves almost exactly like a credit score for your insurance.

A clean claims history earns you a mod below 1.0, a discount on every dollar of premium. A rough one pushes the mod above 1.0, a surcharge on every dollar. A credit score follows you, it compounds, and it's frequently wrong in ways nobody catches until you look. Your e-mod is no different. Most schools have never pulled the worksheet to check it.

## How the e-mod actually works

Here is the mechanic in plain terms. The rating organization that calculates your mod looks at your claims over a multi-year window, usually a few years back, and compares your actual losses against what a school of your size and type would be expected to have. Perform better than expected, you drop below 1.0. Worse than expected, you climb above it.

The part that surprises people is the direction it runs. Your e-mod is backward-looking in how it's built and forward-looking in how it's applied. Last year's claims don't just cost you last year. They get baked into a multiplier that hits next year's premium, and the year after that, for the whole time they sit inside the rating window. A bad claim is a recurring charge on every renewal until it ages out.

And it's a multiplier, not an add-on. A mod of 1.25 means you pay 25 percent more on the entire base premium, across every class code, every payroll dollar. The bigger your payroll, the more a bad mod costs you in raw dollars. For a growing school, that's real money walking out the door on autopilot.

## Problem one: your staff are filed under the wrong codes

Before the mod ever enters the picture, your payroll has to get sorted into class codes, and this is where the first quiet leak starts.

Every job at your school maps to a classification, and each classification carries its own rate. Clerical and administrative work is rated low because the injury risk is low. Maintenance, grounds, food service, and similar hands-on roles are rated higher. The logic is sound. The execution is where schools bleed money, because when a code gets assigned wrong, it tends to get assigned high, and then it never gets revisited.

Picture an office administrator coded into a higher-rated category years ago when the policy was first set up, maybe by someone who didn't know the difference and grabbed the nearest code. That mistake doesn't announce itself. It quietly charges you the higher rate on that person's salary, every year, while the renewal sails through unexamined. Multiply one or two misclassifications across a full staff and several years, and you've overpaid a sum nobody ever names, because nobody ever audits the codes.

The year-end payroll audit is supposed to true this up. In practice it usually confirms how much you paid people, not whether each person was sorted into the right bucket to begin with. The codes carry forward from last year because that's the path of least resistance, and the error rides along untouched.

## Problem two: a mod that nobody is managing

The class-code leak is steady and dumb. The e-mod leak is sneakier, because it compounds, and because most of the damage happens in places a school never thinks to look.

Three things inflate a mod that nobody is steering.

First, claims that get handled passively. A claim left to drift, with no one from the school staying in contact with the adjuster or the injured employee, tends to run longer and cost more than one that's actively managed. Longer and costlier claims feed a higher mod.

Second, no return-to-work program. When an injured employee sits at home collecting wage-replacement benefits because the school has nothing for them to do, the claim's cost climbs, and that cost lands in the mod calculation. When the same employee comes back to modified or light duty, the indemnity portion of the claim shrinks, and so does the mod hit. Return-to-work is one of the most direct levers a school has, and most schools don't have a program at all.

Third, and this is the one that quietly does the most damage, reserves that never get reviewed. When a claim is open, the insurer sets aside an estimate of what it expects the claim to ultimately cost. That estimate is the reserve. Here's what almost no school leader knows: the reserve, not just the dollars actually paid out, is what feeds your e-mod. An open claim with a fat, conservative reserve can inflate your mod even if the claim eventually settles for far less. And the mod calculation pulls those figures as of a specific date each year. If a bloated reserve is sitting on an open claim when that snapshot gets taken, the inflated number locks into your mod and rides your premium for years, long after the claim closed cheap.

Nobody challenges the reserve, because the generalist broker who placed the policy treats the renewal as a form to process, not an account to manage. They don't know your open claims. They've never called the adjuster. They couldn't tell you what's reserved on what, because they've never looked, and they're not going to start the week your mod gets calculated.

## Why the errors live for years

Both leaks survive for the same reason. The system defaults to repeating last year, and no one with a stake in your money is checking the inputs.

Class codes copy forward. Reserves go unchallenged. The mod worksheet shows up, gets filed, and never gets read. A busy broker working a renewal at volume has no incentive to audit the codes line by line or to pick a fight with an adjuster over a reserve that's sitting too high. That's slow, unglamorous work, and it doesn't help them close the next account. So it doesn't happen, and the meter keeps running on your dime.

The maddening part is that every one of these is fixable, and the fixes aren't exotic.

## The levers you actually control

**Audit your class codes.** Pull the classification assigned to every position and confirm each one matches the real work that person does. An administrator filed under a hands-on code should be corrected, and depending on the rules in play, prior overpayments may be recoverable. This is a line-by-line read, and it's worth the hour.

**Manage claims like they cost you money, because they do.** Report injuries promptly, stay in contact with the adjuster, and treat every open claim as something to drive toward closure rather than something to forget. Passive claims are expensive claims.

**Stand up a return-to-work program.** Have light-duty and modified roles defined before you need them, so an injured employee can come back productively instead of sitting home while the claim and your mod both swell. This is the highest-leverage move most schools are missing entirely.

**Review open-claim reserves before the snapshot locks them in.** Know your unit statistical date, the cutoff when claim figures get pulled into your mod. In the weeks before it, review every open claim with your broker and adjuster. If a reserve is set higher than the facts justify, that's the moment to push for it to be revised down, before it freezes into your premium for the next several years.

That's the whole job. Read the codes, work the claims, build the return-to-work plan, and check the reserves before they harden. None of it is hard. It just requires someone who treats your school like a client instead of a renewal to clear off the desk.

## The question worth sitting with

Your e-mod is a score that follows your school, compounds across renewals, and is built from inputs that nobody on your account has verified in years.

So ask yourself this. When was the last time anyone actually pulled your e-mod worksheet, walked your class codes against your real staff roster, and reviewed your open-claim reserves before they got locked into next year's premium? Or has the renewal just been processing itself, quietly charging you for errors nobody went looking for?

If you can't answer that with confidence, you don't have a workers' comp problem yet. You have a number nobody's checked. And unlike the worst-case scenarios in insurance, this one isn't waiting for a bad day to cost you. It's costing you right now, on every renewal, in an amount you've never been shown.
