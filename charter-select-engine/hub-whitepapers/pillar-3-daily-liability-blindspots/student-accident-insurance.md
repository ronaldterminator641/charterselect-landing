# A Student Breaks a Wrist in PE and Nobody Did Anything Wrong. Guess Who the Family Sues Anyway.

*A plain-English look at student accident insurance: the no-fault coverage that pays a hurt kid's medical bills even when the school is not at fault, and quietly heads off the lawsuit that starts when those bills go unpaid.*

---

A seventh grader lands wrong during a normal PE drill and snaps a wrist. No bad equipment. No negligent supervision. Just a kid who jumped and came down at a bad angle, the way kids have for as long as there have been gym floors.

The family files on their own health plan. It runs through a deductible, leaves them a few thousand dollars in bills, and then the questions start. Why are we paying for something that happened at your school. You were supposed to be watching. Somebody owes us for this.

Three weeks later a demand letter shows up with the school's name on it.

Nobody did anything wrong, and the school is now the defendant.

---

## Why your liability policy does not help here

This is the part most school leaders have backwards, and it is not their fault, because nobody walked them through it.

Your general liability policy is fault-based. It is third-party coverage, meaning it responds when someone outside the school claims the school caused them harm and is legally responsible for it. The trigger is negligence. If a family can show the school did something careless, a broken railing left for months, a coach who ignored a known hazard, inadequate supervision, then liability coverage steps in to defend the school and pay what the school legally owes.

Read that again, because the gap lives in one word. Negligence.

When a kid gets hurt and no one was careless, and that is the majority of school injuries, sports, playground, PE, the ordinary collisions of childhood, there is no negligence to trigger the policy. The liability carrier looks at the claim and says, correctly, that the school is not legally liable, so there is nothing to pay. The policy did its job. It just was never built to cover this.

So the medical bills land on the family. And a family staring at a bill they did not budget for, for an injury that happened on your watch, does not care about the legal definition of negligence. They care that their kid got hurt at your school and they are out of pocket. That anger is where lawsuits come from. Not always because the school was careless, but because a lawsuit is the only lever a frustrated family has left to get those bills covered.

## The coverage built for exactly this

Student accident insurance is a different animal. It is first-party, no-fault medical coverage. Strip the jargon and it means this: if a covered student gets hurt in a covered activity, the plan pays toward their medical expenses regardless of who was at fault. No one has to be blamed. No negligence has to be proven. A kid got hurt at school, the plan pays toward the bills, full stop.

Put the two side by side and the difference is clean.

- **General liability** is fault-based and third-party. It pays only if the school is found legally negligent, and it pays the injured party on the school's behalf.
- **Student accident** is no-fault and first-party. It pays toward the student's own medical bills because the injury happened, period, with no finding of fault required.

That no-fault piece is the whole point. It is what keeps a family whole after an injury that was genuinely nobody's fault. And a family whose bills got handled rarely goes looking for a lawyer. You can see how this works as risk management and not just as a kindness. The coverage that pays the bill on the front end is often the same coverage that prevents the liability claim on the back end. Handle the medical expense early, and the demand letter frequently never gets written.

## The structures, and how to match them to your school

Student accident coverage is not one product. It comes in a few common shapes, and the right setup depends on what your school actually does day to day.

**Blanket coverage for all students.** The school buys a plan that covers every enrolled student for injuries during school hours and school-sponsored activities. The school carries the cost, every kid is covered, and there is nothing for families to sign up for or remember. This is the cleanest base layer for a school that wants a real answer the moment any student gets hurt.

**Interscholastic athletics coverage.** Sports carry the heaviest injury exposure a school has, by a wide margin, and athletic plans are written specifically for that. This is also where the most important number on the page lives: the catastrophic injury limit. A torn ACL is expensive. A spinal cord injury, a serious head injury, the rare catastrophic event in contact sports, can run into the hundreds of thousands of dollars or more, over a lifetime of care. A plan with a high catastrophic limit is what stands between a school's athletic program and the kind of loss that ends programs and pulls a family into court out of sheer financial desperation. If your school fields competitive teams, especially in higher-contact sports, this is not optional.

**Voluntary plans offered to families.** Some plans are offered to parents to buy directly, often at low cost, covering their child for school-time injuries and sometimes around the clock. The school is not paying the premium, it is making a sensible option available. These work as a supplement, not a substitute for a blanket plan, and they are most useful for filling the gap families themselves carry.

**How it coordinates with the family's own health insurance.** This is the detail that decides how much the plan actually does, and it is the one a busy school leader is least likely to ask about. A student accident plan is either primary or excess.

A **primary** plan pays first, from the first dollar of covered expense, before the family's own health insurance is touched. An **excess** plan pays second, picking up costs after the family's health plan has paid its share, things like the deductible, the copays, the coinsurance left behind. Both are legitimate. They are built for different jobs and they cost differently. The mistake is not knowing which one you bought, then assuming the plan will catch a bill it was never structured to catch. An excess plan does nothing for a family that has no health insurance to go first. A school serving a community where uninsured or underinsured families are common needs to understand that distinction before an injury, not after.

## Why the gap is sitting open in most schools

Here is the honest reason most charter schools do not have this coverage, or have the wrong version of it. A generalist broker placed the school's package, checked the box on general liability, and never raised student accident at all. It was not on the renewal. It was not in the conversation. The easy-button renewal rolls the same package forward every year, premium roughly flat, nobody reads the policy, and the absence of a coverage no one mentioned is invisible until a kid gets hurt and a family asks who is paying.

A broker who actually works with schools knows that student injuries are not a rare event, they are a Tuesday. They know the liability policy will not answer for the no-fault ones. And they know that an unpaid medical bill is the seed most school injury lawsuits grow from. Raising student accident coverage is not upselling. It is doing the part of the job that a school assumed was already being done.

## What it looks like when someone is actually paying attention

A broker who knows charter schools maps the coverage to the activities. They look at what the school runs, the athletic program and its contact level, the playground, the PE curriculum, the field trips, and they match the structure to it. They tell you in plain language whether the plan is primary or excess, and whether that fits the families you serve. They make sure the catastrophic limit on the athletics side is real and not decorative. And they frame the whole thing the way it should be framed, as the coverage that keeps families whole and keeps lawsuits from starting, not as a line item to shave off the quote.

None of this is exotic. It is the difference between a broker who treats your school like a real client with real kids in it and one who treats your account like a file to renew and forget.

## The question worth sitting with

Sometime this year a student is going to get hurt at your school doing something completely ordinary, and no one is going to have done anything wrong. When that family is sitting at the kitchen table with the medical bills, what answer does your school have for them?

Is there a plan that pays toward those bills no matter whose fault it was? Do you know whether it pays first or only after the family's own insurance? Or is the honest answer that you are not sure the coverage exists, and you would only find out the day you need it most?

If you cannot answer that with confidence, you do not have a problem yet. You have a question nobody has checked. The check takes one conversation. What it costs to skip it shows up as a lawsuit you could have prevented.
