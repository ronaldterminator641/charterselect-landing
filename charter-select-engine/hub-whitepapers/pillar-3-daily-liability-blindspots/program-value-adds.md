# You Are Probably Paying for a Toolbox You Have Never Opened

*A plain-English audit of the free and low-cost services bundled into school insurance programs, the ones you fund inside your premium every year and almost never use.*

---

Two schools buy the same coverage from the same kind of program. Same premium, roughly.

One of them spent six hundred dollars last year on an outside HR consultant to review a termination. The other school called a hotline that came free with its policy, talked to an employment attorney for forty minutes, and paid nothing.

Same money in. One school used what it bought. The other paid for it and let it sit in a drawer.

---

## The stuff you funded and forgot

Insurance programs built for schools and nonprofits are rarely just a stack of coverage. The carriers and program administrators that specialize in this space know the accounts they write are short on staff and long on risk. So they bundle in services. Risk-management portals, employment-law hotlines, training libraries, screening discounts, sometimes a free site visit. These are real, they are useful, and a chunk of their cost is already baked into the premium you pay.

You funded them. The question is whether anyone ever showed you the door.

Most school leaders I talk to have no idea what is included with their program. Not because they are careless. Because nobody walked them through it. The policy got placed, the binder got emailed, and the value-adds went unmentioned. That is the gap this piece is about, and below is a practical way to close it.

A quick word of caution before the list. These services vary by program, by carrier, and by the size and type of your account. Nothing here is guaranteed to be on your policy. The point of this paper is not to tell you what you have. It is to tell you what to ask about, and then how to actually turn it on.

## What tends to be in the box

Here is the range of value-added services that commonly ride along with school and nonprofit insurance programs. Treat this as a menu to ask about, not a promise.

**Risk-management and safety resource portals.** Many programs include a web portal stocked with checklists, safety guides, and incident-tracking tools. Login credentials get issued once, usually to whoever signed the application, and then the link dies in an inbox.

**HR and employment-law hotlines.** A phone or email line, often staffed by employment attorneys or HR professionals, that you can call before you discipline, demote, or fire someone. For a school without in-house counsel, this is one of the most valuable things in the bundle and one of the least used.

**Background-screening discounts.** Programs sometimes negotiate reduced rates with screening vendors for staff and volunteer checks. If you are paying retail for background checks, you may be leaving a discount you already qualify for on the table.

**Online compliance and staff training libraries.** Courses on harassment prevention, mandated-reporter duties, bloodborne pathogens, FERPA basics, and similar topics. These often come with completion tracking, which is exactly the documentation you want when a regulator or a plaintiff's attorney asks what training you provided.

**Sample policy and handbook templates.** Vetted templates for employee handbooks, social media policies, acceptable-use agreements, and the like. Not legal advice, but a far better starting point than a document you copied off the internet in 2019.

**Loss-control and site visits.** Some programs offer a free or subsidized walkthrough of your facility by a risk professional who flags hazards before they become claims. Schools often think of this as something the carrier does to them. It is closer to a free consultation you are entitled to.

**Employee assistance programs (EAP).** Confidential counseling and referral services for staff dealing with stress, grief, substance issues, or financial strain. Frequently bundled on the benefits side. Frequently invisible to the employees who need it because nobody promoted it.

**Telemedicine.** Virtual access to a clinician, sometimes attached to a health plan and sometimes to a standalone program, that can keep staff out of urgent care for routine issues.

**Cyber-hygiene tools and breach-response hotlines.** With cyber coverage you may get phishing-simulation tools, security training, and most importantly a breach-response hotline. That hotline is the number you call in the first chaotic hour of a suspected breach, and calling it correctly can change how a claim plays out.

That is a long list. You are unlikely to have all of it. You almost certainly have more of it than you are using.

## Why none of this reached you

Step back and ask why a school can pay for these services for years and never touch them. The mechanics of the insurance are not the problem here. The mechanics of the relationship are.

A generalist broker treats a charter school like a small commodity account. The school is one file among hundreds, and the economics of that broker's book reward placing the policy and moving on. So the value-adds get sold exactly once, in the proposal, as bullet points that make the program look richer. Then they never get onboarded. Nobody activates the portal login. Nobody circulates the hotline number to the leadership team. Nobody schedules the loss-control visit. Nobody tells the staff the EAP exists.

The services were never the point of the sale. They were decoration on the quote. Once the ink dried, the broker had no reason to come back and make sure you used them, because using them does not pay the broker anything. Onboarding takes time, and time spent onboarding a small account is time a commodity broker would rather spend chasing a bigger one.

So the perks sit there, funded and dormant, until your annual renewal recycles the same unread bullet points into next year's proposal.

## The perks audit: what to ask, and how to turn it on

You can fix this in an afternoon. Here is the audit. Pull your current policies and your most recent proposal, then work the categories.

**Ask for the full list, in writing.** Tell your broker or carrier you want a written inventory of every value-added service included with each policy you hold. Property, liability, management liability, cyber, benefits, all of it. Make them produce the list rather than reciting it from memory.

**Match the list to credentials.** For every portal, training library, or online tool, get the actual login URL, the account credentials, and the name of who holds the master account. Reset it if the credentials trace back to a staffer who left two years ago.

**Find the hotline numbers and put them where they belong.** The HR and employment-law line, the breach-response line, the EAP line. Write them on a single sheet, give it to every person who might need to dial in a crisis, and decide in advance who is authorized to call. A hotline you cannot find at 9 p.m. on a Friday is not a benefit.

**Run the screening-discount math.** Ask what background-screening rates the program offers and compare them to what you pay now. If there is a gap, switch vendors or claim the rate.

**Schedule the loss-control visit on purpose.** If a site visit or risk assessment is included, request it rather than waiting to be offered it. Treat the findings as a free roadmap for reducing the claims that drive your future premium.

**Assign each live perk an owner.** A portal nobody owns is a portal nobody uses. Put a name next to each active service so there is one person responsible for actually working it during the year.

**Promote the staff-facing perks.** The EAP and telemedicine only return value if employees know they exist. Announce them at a staff meeting, put them in the handbook, repeat it at open enrollment.

**Re-audit at renewal.** Programs change what they include. Make this list a standing item every renewal, so a perk that quietly disappears or quietly appears does not go unnoticed for a year.

Work that list once and you will likely find services worth real money that you have been funding and ignoring.

## What it looks like when someone actually does the job

A broker who knows charter schools does not stop at placing the policy. They hand you the inventory of included services without being asked. They walk the leadership team through the hotlines and confirm the portal logins work. They tell your staff the EAP is there. They schedule the loss-control visit and sit with you on the findings. At renewal, they show you what changed in the bundle, not just what changed in the premium.

That is not a heroic effort. It is onboarding the thing you already bought. The reason it so rarely happens is that the broker on the account decided your school was too small to bother walking through the box.

## The question worth sitting with

Picture your most recent insurance proposal, the one with the bullet points listing everything the program "includes." You signed it. The premium reflects those bullets.

So ask yourself honestly. Of all the value-added services on that list, how many have you actually logged into, called, scheduled, or told your staff about in the past year? And if the honest answer is closer to none than to all, was that because the services had no value, or because nobody ever opened the box and showed you what you already paid for?

If you cannot name what is in your program, you do not have a coverage problem. You have a toolbox sitting unopened, and the only cost of opening it is the afternoon it takes to ask.
