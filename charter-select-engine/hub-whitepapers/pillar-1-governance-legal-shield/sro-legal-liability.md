# You Put an Officer on Campus to Make Kids Safer. You May Have Just Bought a Lawsuit Your Policy Won't Touch.

*A plain-English look at law enforcement legal liability for charter schools with a School Resource Officer, and the gap that opens the moment a sworn officer starts working your hallways.*

---

A student gets taken to the ground in a hallway. An arm gets twisted. A parent records the last ten seconds on a phone.

Eighteen months later, a federal civil rights complaint names the officer, the police department, and your school.

You call your broker, confident this is what insurance is for. Then you find out the policy you assumed covered it has language that carves law enforcement activity right out.

---

## The decision that quietly changed your risk profile

Bringing a sworn officer onto campus feels like a safety upgrade, and in a lot of ways it is. A School Resource Officer can build relationships, de-escalate, and respond fast when something real happens.

What rarely gets discussed in the same meeting is what an SRO does to your insurance exposure. The moment an armed, sworn officer is acting under color of law inside your building, your school is no longer just an educational operation. It is now adjacent to law enforcement, and law enforcement carries a category of claim that schools almost never see otherwise: use of force, an arrest gone wrong, a search that crossed a line, a detention a court later calls unreasonable. These get litigated as civil rights claims, often under the federal statute that lets people sue government actors for constitutional violations, commonly referenced by its code section, 1983.

Here is the part that catches school leaders flat. The way most SROs are structured does not keep that exposure off your doorstep.

## How SRO arrangements are usually built

There are two common setups, and the distinction matters less than you would hope.

In the most common arrangement, the officer is an employee of a local police department or sheriff's office and gets assigned to your campus under a written agreement. They wear that department's badge, answer to that department's chain of command, and are disciplined through that department.

In the other arrangement, the school employs the officer directly, sometimes through its own security or police function.

You might assume the first setup keeps you clean, since the officer technically works for someone else. It does not, at least not reliably. When an SRO uses force on a student, courts analyze it the same way they would any officer using force on any person, under the same constitutional standard. A plaintiff's attorney looking at that incident does not name only the officer. They name the department, and they name the school, because the officer was operating inside your walls, on your students, in connection with how your school runs. Whether the school ultimately owes anything is a question for the litigation. Getting pulled into the litigation is close to automatic.

So you can be drawn into a law enforcement claim even when you do not employ the officer. That is the exposure. Now look at whether your insurance answers it.

## What your standard policies actually say

A school typically carries two main liability lines. General liability covers ordinary bodily injury and property damage, the slip-and-fall side of running a building. Educators legal liability, often called ELL, covers wrongful acts tied to the educational job: failure to educate, discipline disputes, the professional decisions a school and its board make.

Neither one was built for law enforcement, and the standard forms tend to say so.

General liability is written for the risks of owning and operating a normal business. Law enforcement activity is a recognized professional exposure that gets excluded from these policies, frequently by a specific endorsement that removes coverage for the named insured's law enforcement activities. ELL has its own set of exclusions, and claims arising from intentional or criminal acts, the territory a force or false-arrest claim lives in, often sit outside it.

Read those two facts together and the gap comes into focus. The two policies you rely on most are each drafted to push law enforcement claims away. A use-of-force suit can land squarely in the seam between them, where neither one was designed to respond.

This is the line that built the problem: the coverage you assume protects you against an SRO incident is usually the exact coverage written to exclude it.

## The coverage that was actually built for this

The line designed for this exposure is Law Enforcement Legal Liability, sometimes shortened to LELL or sold as police professional liability.

It exists precisely because general liability shuts law enforcement out. LELL is built to respond to claims arising from law enforcement activities: excessive or improper use of force, wrongful arrest or detention, improper search and seizure, and the civil rights claims that get filed under that 1983 framework. It typically funds defense costs, which in a federal civil rights case can run high long before anyone reaches a verdict or settlement.

Like any line, it has its own limits and carve-outs. Coverage for the conduct itself generally does not extend to truly willful, wanton, or criminal acts, and these policies commonly exclude punitive damages. The specifics vary by carrier and by how the policy is written, so the structure matters and a careful read matters more. The headline, though, is straightforward. If a sworn officer is working your campus, this is the line that was engineered for the claim, and it is usually not riding along inside the policies you already have.

## The document everyone signs and nobody coordinates

When the officer belongs to a police department, the relationship runs through a written agreement, usually a Memorandum of Understanding, an MOU. This is the single most important risk document in the whole arrangement, and it routinely gets signed without the one person who should read it ever seeing it.

A well-built MOU spells out the officer's role, who supervises them, how complaints get handled, and, critically, how responsibility and insurance get allocated between the school and the department. The common pattern is that each party stays responsible for the acts of its own people, with each side agreeing, to the extent the law allows, to indemnify the other for claims arising from its own conduct. Many MOUs also state who has to carry insurance and at what level.

Sit with what that means. The MOU can obligate your school to indemnify the department in certain situations, or require your school to carry insurance for this exposure, and those obligations are only as good as the coverage standing behind them. A school administrator and a police chief sign the document. The school's broker, the person who would know whether the indemnity promise is backed by an actual policy, is often never in the room. The agreement gets executed, the officer reports for duty, and the insurance side of the deal stays an open question until a claim tests it.

Every path to this exposure runs through one place: the agreement that put the officer on campus and the policies that are supposed to answer for what the officer does. If those two are never lined up against each other, you do not have a program. You have a signature and a hope.

## Where the generalist broker let this slip

A broker who treats charter schools as small accounts to renew quickly is not reading your MOU. They were not at the table when it was signed, they did not ask whether a sworn officer now works your halls, and they renewed your general liability and ELL the same way they renew everyone's, without a thought about the law enforcement exclusion sitting inside both.

That is how the gap forms. Not through some exotic failure, but through the ordinary inattention of a broker who never asked the one question that changes the analysis: is there an officer on this campus, and if so, what happens when a claim comes out of what that officer does?

A broker who actually knows schools asks it on the first call. They want to know whether you have an SRO, how the arrangement is structured, and whether there is an MOU. They read that MOU against your policies. They check what your general liability and ELL forms exclude. And if there is a real law enforcement exposure with no line built to catch it, they tell you plainly, before a parent's phone ever starts recording, instead of after.

## The question worth sitting with

You made a deliberate choice to put a trained officer on your campus. That was a real decision about safety, and it deserves a matching decision about risk.

So here is the one worth asking yourself, today, while it is still a quiet question and not a lawsuit:

If an incident involving your officer turned into a civil rights claim next month, do you actually know which policy responds, and have you confirmed it isn't one of the two that are written to exclude it? Or has nobody on your side ever lined up the MOU you signed against the coverage you're paying for?

If you can't answer that with confidence, you don't have a coverage problem yet. You have a question no one has checked. The check is a conversation. The cost of skipping it shows up on the day you can least afford a surprise.
