# The Claim No School Leader Wants to Picture Is Often Protected by the Smallest Number on the Policy

*A plain-English look at sexual abuse and molestation coverage, why it is so often buried as a tiny sublimit inside general liability, and what real protection for this exposure looks like.*

---

There is one category of claim that can end a charter school. Not strain it. End it.

It is also the claim most leaders never want to say out loud, so it rarely gets discussed at renewal. And because nobody brings it up, the coverage for it tends to be the thinnest, most overlooked figure on the entire policy.

Let me walk through this carefully, because the subject deserves care, and the mechanics deserve to be correct.

---

## Why this exposure exists at all

Any organization that brings adults and children together carries some risk of abuse. That is simply the nature of the work. Schools supervise children for hours a day, across classrooms, hallways, bathrooms, locker rooms, buses, field trips, and after-hours programs. The exposure is not a sign that something is wrong with a school. It is a structural reality of caring for kids.

Treating that reality seriously is not pessimism. It is the responsible posture, the same way a hospital plans for infection or a bank plans for fraud. The goal is twofold: do everything reasonable to prevent harm, and make sure that if an allegation ever surfaces, the school can respond, defend itself fairly, and stand behind any survivor without the financial side of it threatening the institution's survival.

Both halves of that goal touch insurance. And that is where most schools have a quiet problem they have never been shown.

## Why these claims are among the most severe a school can face

Three things make abuse claims uniquely dangerous to a school's finances.

First, the dollar figures. Settlements and verdicts in abuse cases can run very high, often well beyond what comparable claims in other categories reach, because of the lasting harm involved and the way juries weigh it.

Second, the timeline. Abuse allegations frequently surface years, sometimes decades, after the events in question, because of how and when survivors are able to come forward. Many states have widened or reopened the windows for these claims to be filed. So the exposure is what insurers call long-tail. The claim that arrives next year may concern a period long past.

Third, the cost of simply defending. Even an allegation that is ultimately found to have no merit must be investigated and defended, and that legal work is expensive on its own. A school does not get to choose whether to respond. It has to.

Put those together and you have a claim type that is severe, slow to surface, costly to defend, and capable of pulling in multiple claimants at once. This is exactly the kind of exposure insurance is supposed to be built around.

## How the coverage is usually structured, in plain English

Coverage for this risk is generally referred to as Sexual Abuse and Molestation coverage, shortened to SAM, or sometimes SAML or sexual misconduct liability. It tends to show up in one of three ways.

**As a small sublimit inside the general liability policy.** This is the most common, and the most quietly dangerous. The school's general liability policy carries a large limit, and tucked inside it is a separate, much smaller cap that applies specifically to abuse claims. The headline limit might be a million or more, while the abuse sublimit is a fraction of that. Many general liability carriers are reluctant to take on this exposure at all, so when they include it, they restrict it to a low figure.

**As an outright exclusion.** Some general liability policies simply carve abuse and molestation out entirely. The coverage that everyone assumes is there is, in plain reading, not there.

**As standalone coverage, or a properly built endorsement.** Here the abuse exposure is insured on its own terms, with meaningful limits, broader policy language, and defense built to handle this specific kind of claim.

The trap is the first one. A school reads "general liability, $1 million" and feels covered for everything underneath it. The abuse sublimit hiding inside might be a small slice of that, and against the size these claims can reach, a small slice can be the difference between an insured event and an institutional collapse.

## What adequate coverage actually looks like

A few features separate real protection from a box that was technically checked.

**Meaningful limits, structured for more than one claimant.** Properly built SAM coverage typically carries both a per-victim limit and an aggregate limit. The per-victim figure applies to each individual claimant. The aggregate is the most the policy will pay across all claims in the period. That structure matters, because these situations can involve more than one person, and a single shared pool can be drained by one severe claim before others are addressed.

**Defense costs that sit outside the limit.** This one is easy to miss and enormously important. When defense costs are paid inside the limit, every dollar spent on lawyers reduces the dollars left to resolve the claim. Given how expensive defending these matters can be, defense inside the limit can quietly consume the coverage. Stronger policies pay defense outside the limit, so the full limit remains available for resolution.

**Attention to how the policy is triggered.** Some SAM coverage is written claims-made, meaning it responds based on when the claim is reported rather than when the event occurred. Some is written occurrence, meaning it responds based on when the event happened, regardless of when the claim eventually surfaces. Because abuse claims are reported so long after the fact, the trigger and, for claims-made policies, the retroactive date are not technicalities. They decide whether a claim about a past period is covered at all. This deserves a deliberate conversation, not a default setting.

## The part that surprises people: the underwriting makes the school safer

Here is the quiet upside of insuring this exposure properly. To write meaningful SAM coverage, an underwriter generally wants to see real abuse-prevention controls in place. So the act of getting good coverage tends to push the school toward the practices that reduce the chance of harm in the first place.

The controls underwriters commonly look for are the same ones child-safety experts recommend:

- Background checks and screening on employees, contractors, and volunteers, including checks against sex-offender registries, refreshed on a regular schedule rather than just at hire.
- A written abuse-prevention policy that everyone is trained on and held to.
- Recurring, documented staff training on recognizing warning signs, maintaining appropriate boundaries, and reporting.
- A two-adult rule, so children are not left alone with a single adult where it can reasonably be avoided.
- Clear reporting procedures that follow mandatory-reporting law, with documentation of how reports are handled.

Schools with stronger controls generally see better terms and pricing, because they genuinely are lower risk. So the coverage and the prevention reinforce each other. Buying the protection seriously tends to make the underlying harm less likely. Few lines of insurance work that way.

## Why the gap is usually the broker's doing

So how does a school end up with a tiny abuse sublimit, or an exclusion, and never know? Almost always the same way.

A generalist broker, the kind who treats a charter school as a small account to process quickly, builds the program off a standard package. The abuse sublimit comes in at whatever the package defaults to, often a low figure, sometimes an exclusion. The broker does not raise it, because raising it means starting a hard, uncomfortable conversation, and the easy-button renewal does not require one. Year after year the policy renews on autopilot. The smallest, most consequential number on the page is never examined, never explained, never benchmarked against what these claims actually cost.

It is not that the school chose to underinsure this risk. It is that nobody put the choice in front of them. The default got accepted because checking it required someone to slow down and bring up the one topic generalists most prefer to skip.

A broker who actually knows schools does the opposite. They name the exposure plainly and respectfully, they pull the policy and read the abuse provisions out loud in plain language, they show the sublimit against what these claims realistically reach, they walk through whether it is sublimit, standalone, or excluded, and they connect the coverage conversation to the school's prevention practices so both get stronger together.

## The question worth sitting with

You do not have to imagine the worst day to take this seriously. You only have to look at one line.

So the question is this. If an abuse allegation surfaced at your school next month, possibly about a period years in the past, do you actually know what limit would respond, whether it sits inside your general liability or stands on its own, whether your defense costs would eat into that limit, and whether anyone has ever weighed that figure against what these claims really cost? Or has that line simply renewed itself, untouched, because it was easier for everyone not to bring it up?

If the honest answer is that you are not sure, you do not have a crisis. You have a line on a page that nobody has read closely. Reading it now, before it is ever tested, is a very different thing than discovering it afterward.
