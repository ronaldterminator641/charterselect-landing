# Your Board Protection Belongs Inside One School Program, Not Bolted On From Three Directions

*A plain-English look at where board protection actually comes from for a charter school, and why the clean answer is School Leaders coverage placed with your package carrier and extended under a program built for it.*

---

A lawsuit names one of your board members. Personally.

Not the school. Her.

The board chair calls the broker, calm at first, because everyone remembers being told the school carries a big umbrella and the board is covered.

The broker goes quiet.

Here is what that pause is really about, and what should have been in place instead.

---

## The reassurance that was never true

Somewhere along the way, a broker pointed at your program and said a sentence that sounded final. "You have a sizable umbrella sitting on top of everything, so the board is protected."

That sentence is comforting and, for most schools, wrong. A generic commercial umbrella is built to add excess limit over a specific, scheduled set of policies, almost always Commercial General Liability, Commercial Auto, and Employers Liability. It follows the form of those policies. It can only stretch a limit higher on a coverage that already sits underneath it.

Line that up and the problem is obvious. If the only things under that umbrella are GL, Auto, and Employers Liability, there is no board protection down there for it to extend. A claim that names a trustee for a governance decision is not bodily injury or a fender bender. It lives in a different category of coverage entirely, and a bolt-on commercial umbrella was never built to reach it.

So the board chair reaches for the umbrella, and in most cases it never had a thing to do with protecting the board. The word made it sound like a blanket over the whole organization. A generic one is not.

## What actually protects your board

The protection your board is reaching for in that moment has a name. Management liability. For a school it usually travels as one coverage part, and the good markets brand it around exactly who it protects. School Leaders Risk Protection, Educators Legal Liability, the school-built version of directors and officers coverage.

This is the line that responds when the claim is about decisions and conduct, not a slip-and-fall. It bundles the pieces that matter for a charter:

- **Directors and officers protection**, for the personal exposure of your trustees, officers, and the entity when someone alleges a wrongful act in how the school was governed or managed.
- **Employment practices**, for staff claims like wrongful termination, discrimination, harassment, and retaliation, which a people-heavy charter faces constantly.
- **Educators legal liability**, the piece a generic nonprofit form skips, built for the claims that are uniquely yours: failure to educate, denial of special-education services, student civil-rights and discipline disputes.

That last piece is why a plain nonprofit D&O form is the wrong tool for a school. It may not contemplate student and parent claims at all. School Leaders coverage does, because it was written for schools in the first place.

## The clean way to place it, and the messy way generalists reach for

Here is where the approach matters, and where most brokers go wrong in one of two directions.

Direction one is the myth above. They point at the generic umbrella and call the board covered. That protection was never there.

Direction two looks more sophisticated and is usually just as clumsy. The broker who knows the umbrella will not answer overcorrects and starts bolting things on from the side. A standalone D&O policy from one carrier. A separate excess tower from another. A standalone Side-A difference-in-conditions layer from a third. Three placements, three carriers, three sets of terms and retroactive dates that have to be reconciled by hand, and a premium load to match. For a large public company that architecture has its place. For a charter school it is over-engineered, expensive, and fragile at exactly the seams where the pieces are supposed to meet.

The clean answer for a school is neither. Place your management liability with the package carrier as part of one School Leaders program, and extend the limit under a program umbrella that is actually built to follow that coverage. Not the generic GL-and-Auto umbrella that excludes it, but a school program umbrella written to sit over the School Leaders line. One carrier that understands schools, one program, one set of terms that already agree with each other because they were written together, and excess limit that sits over the board coverage instead of missing it entirely.

That is the difference that matters. The problem was never that an umbrella cannot extend board protection. The problem is that the wrong umbrella cannot, and a generic one bolted over GL and Auto is the wrong umbrella. Put the coverage in a program built for schools, under an umbrella built to follow it, and the limit lands where the claim actually is.

## Why this lands harder on a charter

For most organizations a board coverage gap is serious. For a charter school it reaches further, for reasons specific to how charters are built.

Your board is volunteers. Parents, local professionals, community members serving unpaid. A management liability claim names those people personally, which puts a trustee's own net worth on the line for serving your school. The "you have an umbrella" reassurance is the cruelest version of the trap, because it tells volunteers they are safe while the coverage that would protect them was never placed.

Charters are people-heavy employers, which means real employment-practices exposure. Budget-driven staffing decisions are a documented driver of rising employment claims across the sector.

And the exposure that is purely yours, the student claims, is the one a generic nonprofit D&O ignores and a School Leaders program is built to answer. Failure to educate, special education, student rights and discipline. Funding pressure sharpens all of it, because charter money follows enrollment, and authorizer renewals, financial strain, and closure decisions are exactly the board actions that generate claims. The risk and the thin coverage tend to peak together.

The quiet reason this slips through is structural. Most charter business offices are lean, with no in-house risk manager to catch that the generic umbrella excludes board protection, or that the coverage got scattered across three carriers instead of placed cleanly in one program. So the error sits there, invisible, until a claim makes it visible.

## What handling this properly looks like

This is fixable, and none of it is exotic. It is a placement done with care instead of on autopilot. Here is what to confirm:

Management liability placed as School Leaders coverage with a carrier that writes for schools, with directors and officers, employment practices, and educators legal liability all in one program. The form should reach the claims that are uniquely yours, the failure-to-educate and student-rights and special-education matters, and the insured definition should actually cover your trustees, officers, employees, volunteers, and committee members by name.

Excess limit built by extending that School Leaders coverage under a program umbrella written to follow it, not a generic commercial umbrella scheduled over GL and Auto that has nothing to sit on. If more limit is needed, it stacks over the coverage the board actually relies on.

Claims-made details reconciled inside the program, retroactive date and prior-acts coverage confirmed, so the whole thing agrees with itself because it was written together instead of stitched from three directions.

## What a broker actually doing the job would do

A generalist gives the board a sentence that sounds like protection. "You have a five-million umbrella, you are covered." It is easy to say, it ends the conversation, and it is wrong in a way that will not surface for years. The broker who knows better, but not well enough, swings the other way and bolts on a standalone tower from carriers who have never seen a charter, then hands you the reconciliation problem.

A broker doing the job for a school does neither. They show you, in plain language, that your board protection comes from School Leaders coverage placed inside your program, and they can point to the exact line that responds when a trustee is named. They confirm the form contemplates student claims instead of assuming a nonprofit D&O is close enough. And they build any excess limit by extending that coverage under an umbrella actually written to follow it, so the protection is one clean program instead of three invoices that only pretend to connect.

## Before you trust the umbrella again

You do not have to take anyone's word for this, this page included. You just have to check one thing.

If a board member were named personally in a lawsuit next month, does their protection come from School Leaders coverage your program was actually built to place and extend, or from a generic umbrella someone pointed at, or a standalone tower someone tried to sell you from the outside? And has anyone confirmed, in writing, which one is really in place?

If the honest answer is the umbrella, you do not have protection there, you have a misunderstanding. The good news is that it is a placement problem, and placement problems get fixed before the claim, never during it.
