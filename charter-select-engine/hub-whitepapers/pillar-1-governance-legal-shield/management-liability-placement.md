# Your Board Was Told The Umbrella Has Them Covered. The Umbrella Almost Never Does.

*A plain-English look at where board protection actually comes from, and why the commercial umbrella has nothing to do with it.*

A lawsuit names one of your board members by name. Personally.

Not the school. Her.

The broker goes quiet.

## What you were probably told

Somewhere along the way, a broker pointed at your program and said a sentence that sounded final. "You've got a sizable umbrella sitting on top of everything, so the board is protected."

It's a comforting thing to hear. It's also one of the most common misunderstandings in how a school's coverage actually fits together, and it's the kind of thing nobody catches until a director is staring at their own name on a complaint.

So let me walk through what protects your board, what doesn't, and why the umbrella you're counting on is built to sit somewhere else entirely.

## What "management liability" actually is

The protection your board is reaching for in that moment has a name. It's management liability.

Management liability isn't one policy. It's a category, a bundle of the coverages that respond when the claim is about decisions and conduct rather than a slip-and-fall or a fender bender. For nonprofits and private organizations it's usually sold as a single package combining a few distinct lines:

- **Directors & Officers (D&O)**, which protects the personal assets of your directors, trustees, and officers, along with the entity, against claims alleging wrongful acts in how they governed or managed. It pays defense costs, settlements, and judgments.
- **Employment Practices Liability (EPLI)**, for staff claims like wrongful termination, discrimination, harassment, and retaliation.
- **Fiduciary liability**, for breaches in how employee benefit plans get managed, things like imprudent investment choices or mishandling plan assets.
- **Crime/fidelity coverage**, often folded in or attached, covering employee theft, fraud, and the ERISA bond a plan sponsor may need.

That's the world your board lives in legally. None of it is bodily injury or property damage, which is the world general liability covers. Different exposure, different policies.

One more detail matters before we get to the umbrella. D&O is almost always claims-made coverage. It responds to claims made during the policy period, and it carries a retroactive date. Hold onto that, because it's part of why the umbrella can't help.

## Why the umbrella has nothing to sit over

A commercial umbrella does a real job. It adds excess limit on top of a specific, scheduled set of underlying policies. In almost every case those underlying policies are Commercial General Liability, Commercial Auto liability, and Employers Liability, the part of workers' comp that handles employee injury suits.

The key word is "follow-form." An umbrella follows the form of the policies underneath it. It inherits their coverage and their exclusions. It can only stretch a limit higher on a coverage that already exists below it.

Now line that up. If the policies under your umbrella are GL, Auto, and Employers Liability, then there is no D&O underneath the umbrella. No EPLI. No fiduciary. No management liability line of any kind. The umbrella can't sit on top of a coverage that isn't in its tower. There's nothing for it to extend.

On top of that, many umbrella forms carry their own exclusions for professional services, employment-related practices, and benefits-related exposures, which is the market's way of saying out loud that these risks are routed somewhere else on purpose. Industry guidance is blunt about it. An umbrella is not a substitute for D&O, and D&O typically needs its own separate excess policy when higher limits are required.

So when the board chair reaches for the umbrella, in almost every case it never had a thing to do with protecting the board. The word made it sound like a blanket over the whole organization. It isn't.

I say "almost always" on purpose. A bespoke or specialty program can occasionally be built to wrap management lines, and some package products combine coverages under one form. But the standard commercial umbrella does not, and assuming yours is the rare exception is exactly how boards get surprised.

## Where the extra limit actually comes from

If not the umbrella, then where? When a school needs more management liability limit than the primary policy provides, it gets built as a dedicated excess tower. Two pieces.

First, follow-form excess D&O, sometimes called excess management liability. These are layers stacked directly on top of the primary management liability policy, each one adding limit, each one written to follow the primary form. This is the real version of what people mistakenly think the umbrella is doing.

Second, and this is the piece generalists almost never place, **Side-A DIC** at or near the top of the tower. DIC stands for difference-in-conditions. Side-A coverage protects the individual directors and officers specifically, not the entity. It does two jobs. It provides additional dedicated limit reserved for the individuals, and it drops down to fill gaps when an underlying insurer can't or won't pay, like an insurer becoming insolvent or wrongfully refusing a claim, or the limit getting exhausted. The exact triggers and terms vary by carrier, so treat that as the concept rather than fixed wording, but the purpose is consistent across the market.

Why does that matter so much? Because of how a standard D&O policy is structured. It has three insuring agreements, called sides. Side A pays individuals directly when the school can't or won't indemnify them. Side B reimburses the school when it does indemnify. Side C covers the entity itself. And here's the part that bites: Sides A, B, and C usually share one aggregate limit.

Picture a lawsuit that names the school and several board members together. The entity's own defense, the Side C piece, starts drawing down that shared limit. In a hard-fought case, the limit can be eaten before the individual trustees are ever protected. Dedicated Side-A and Side-A DIC exist precisely to close that gap, giving the individuals their own protected limit that can't be burned by the entity's side of the fight.

## Why this lands harder on a charter

For most organizations this is a serious gap. For a charter school it reaches further, for a few reasons that are specific to how charters are built.

Your board is volunteers. Parents, local professionals, community members serving unpaid. D&O claims name those people personally, which means a trustee's own net worth is on the line for serving your school. The "you have an umbrella" reassurance is the cruelest version of this trap, because it tells volunteers they're safe while the coverage that would actually protect them was never placed.

Charters are people-heavy employers, too, which means real EPLI exposure. Budget-driven staffing decisions are a documented driver of rising employment claims at charter schools, and harassment, discrimination, and wrongful-termination suits are common across the sector.

Then there's the exposure that's purely yours. Some of the claims a school faces aren't about governance or employment at all. They're about students. Failure to educate, denial of special-education services, student civil-rights and discipline disputes. A generic nonprofit D&O policy may not contemplate those at all. The line that does is **Educators Legal Liability**, a hybrid of D&O and E&O built for schools, which responds to those student and parent claims and often bundles employment coverage with it. A board sitting on a plain nonprofit D&O can have a wide-open gap right here and never know it.

And funding pressure makes all of it worse. Charter money follows enrollment. Authorizer renewals, financial distress, closure decisions, these are exactly the kinds of board actions that generate D&O claims in the first place. The risk and the thin coverage tend to peak at the same time.

The quiet reason this slips through is structural. Most charter business offices are lean. No in-house risk manager to catch that the umbrella excludes management liability, or that the tower was never built, or that the form is the wrong one for a school. So the error sits there, invisible, until a claim makes it visible.

## What handling this properly looks like

This is fixable, and none of it is exotic. It's a placement done with care instead of on autopilot. Here's what to ask for and confirm:

Management liability placed as its own program, separate from the GL/Auto/umbrella package. A nonprofit or management liability package combining D&O, EPLI, and fiduciary, with crime where it fits.

The school-appropriate form. Confirm in writing that coverage reaches Educators Legal Liability exposures, the failure-to-educate and student-rights and special-education claims, and that the insured definition actually covers your trustees, officers, employees, volunteers, and committee members.

A right-sized primary limit, set with the knowledge that defense costs typically erode the limit rather than sitting on top of it.

A real excess tower when more limit is needed, built from follow-form excess D&O layers. Never the commercial umbrella.

Dedicated Side-A or Side-A DIC, so your individual trustees have protected limit that can't be drained by the entity's side of a shared-limit claim.

Fiduciary coverage carried deliberately, with the ERISA bond requirement for any benefit plan confirmed as satisfied.

And claims-made details reconciled across the whole tower: matching retroactive dates, prior-acts coverage, consistent terms layer to layer.

## What a broker actually doing the job would do

A generalist gives the board a sentence that sounds like protection. "You've got a five-million umbrella, you're covered." It's easy to say, it ends the conversation, and it's wrong in a way that won't surface for years.

A broker doing the job doesn't reach for that sentence. They show you, in plain language, that your board's protection comes from a management liability program and not from the umbrella, and they can point to exactly which policy responds when a trustee is named. They check whether there's an excess tower or just a thin primary. They ask whether Side-A is in place. They confirm the form actually contemplates student claims instead of assuming a nonprofit D&O is close enough.

That's the difference between a broker who treats your board like real people with real assets on the line, and one who treats your account like a file to clear before the deadline.

## Before you trust the umbrella again

You don't have to take anyone's word for this, this page included. You just have to check one thing.

So here's the question worth sitting with:

If a board member were named personally in a lawsuit next month, which policy would defend them, and has anyone confirmed it's actually in place, with its own limit, or did someone just point at the umbrella?

If the answer is the umbrella, you don't have protection there, you have a misunderstanding. The good news is that it's a placement problem, and placement problems get fixed before the claim, never during it.
