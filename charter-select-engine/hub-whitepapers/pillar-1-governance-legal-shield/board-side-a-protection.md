# The Day Your Board Member Finds Out She Was Sharing Her Protection With The Whole School

*A plain-English look at how an individual director gets left exposed when one D&O limit covers everyone at once, and why the fix is a school program built to protect the individual, not a separate policy bolted on from the outside.*

---

A volunteer joins your board to help a school. Parents, a local accountant, a retired principal, people who serve for free.

Years later one of them is named, personally, in a lawsuit over a board decision.

She assumes the school's policy has her. It did. Right up until the entity's own legal bills drained the pot she was counting on.

Here is how that happens, and how it should have been built instead.

---

## The reassurance every volunteer is given

Nobody agrees to sit on a charter school board expecting to bet their house on it. They show up because they care about the school, they have a skill the board needs, and somebody told them the school carries Directors and Officers insurance, so they are protected.

That is usually true, as far as it goes. The school does carry D&O, and most of the time it works exactly the way everyone assumes. A claim comes in, the policy defends the board, the volunteers never feel a thing.

The problem is the handful of situations where a standard D&O policy quietly stops protecting the individual and starts protecting everyone but the individual. Those situations are rare, and they are the exact ones that pull a board member's personal savings into a courtroom. Most boards never learn that "the school has D&O" and "I am personally protected no matter what" are two different statements.

## The three sides, and the one detail that matters

A standard D&O policy is really three coverages in one, and the industry calls them sides.

Side A pays the individual directors and officers directly, when the organization cannot or will not reimburse them. This is the part that protects a person's own assets. Side B reimburses the school when it does step in and cover its people. Side C covers the entity itself when the school is named as a defendant.

That structure is fine. Here is the detail that matters more than any other: all three sides almost always share one combined limit. One pot of money for the individuals, the reimbursement, and the entity, all at once.

Hold that thought, because the shared limit is where the trouble starts.

## Where the individual gets left exposed

There are four ways the gap opens, and a charter can hit any of them.

The shared limit gets eaten. Picture a serious lawsuit that names the school and several board members together. The school's own defense starts drawing down that single shared pot. Defense costs usually erode the limit rather than sitting on top of it, so a hard-fought case can burn through the money before the individual trustees are ever made whole. The entity and the individuals drink from the same glass, and the entity often drinks first.

The school cannot indemnify. For charter schools this is not hypothetical. Funding follows enrollment, authorizer renewals can go sideways, and a school in real financial distress, or one that has closed, may have no money to defend a former board member. The trustee who governed during the hard years is the one most likely to get named, and the least likely to have a solvent school behind them.

The school is legally barred from indemnifying. In some situations the law itself prohibits an organization from covering an individual. The school might want to help and be told it cannot.

The policy itself fails to respond. The main D&O policy could be rescinded over something in the application, or the carrier could refuse a claim or become insolvent. When the policy that was supposed to protect the individual is the one that falls apart, the individual holds the bag.

In every one of these, the entity has options. The individual volunteer does not. And the protections people assume cover them, state volunteer-immunity laws and the federal Volunteer Protection Act, are thinner than they sound. They offer limited immunity from certain claims, but they generally do not pay the cost of defending a lawsuit, and they fall away for gross negligence or conduct outside the volunteer's role. A baseless claim still has to be fought, and fighting it costs real money.

## The fix generalists get wrong in two directions

So the individual exposure is real. The mistake is in how brokers respond to it.

Direction one is the common one. The generalist sells the base D&O policy and stops there. Nobody pulls the structure apart, nobody asks whether the individuals have protected limit or whether they are sharing one pot with the entity, and the conversation about insolvency and rescission never happens. The gap sits there unseen until a claim makes it visible.

Direction two looks more sophisticated and is usually just as clumsy. The broker who knows the shared-limit problem overcorrects and bolts a standalone Side-A difference-in-conditions policy onto the program from a separate carrier. For a large public company with a deep excess tower, that architecture has its place. For a charter school it means a second carrier, a second set of terms, and drop-down wording that has to reconcile by hand against a primary policy it was never written alongside. It is the same scattered, fragile structure that leaves a school exposed at exactly the seams where the pieces are supposed to meet, sold as if bolting on more policies were the same thing as building protection.

## The clean answer: protection built into the program

The individual director's protection should come from how the School Leaders program is built and placed, not from a policy stapled on after the fact.

The right carriers, the ones that write for schools, build the individual safeguard into the management liability program itself. Dedicated limit reserved for the individual directors and officers, so the entity's side of a shared-limit fight cannot drain what the people rely on. Terms written to respond when the school cannot indemnify. Coverage placed with one carrier whose forms already agree with each other, because they were written together instead of stitched from two directions.

That is the difference that matters. The volunteer's protection is not a separate invoice from an outside insurer that may or may not drop down cleanly on the worst day. It is a designed feature of the school program you already carry, confirmed in writing, sitting inside a structure built to hold together under a claim. One program that protects the entity and the individual, rather than a base policy for the school and a bolt-on for the people that meet for the first time in a courtroom.

## Why this lands harder on a charter board

For a large company with deep reserves, some of these gaps are remote. For a charter school they cluster together in a way almost tailor-made to catch a volunteer.

The board is unpaid. Parents and community members serving for free are the ones whose homes and savings are on the line, and a claim names those people by name. The financial fragility is real and it correlates with claims: closures, layoffs, authorizer fights, and restructuring are the board decisions most likely to generate a lawsuit, and they happen when the school has the least money to defend anyone. The exposure and the inability to indemnify peak at the same moment.

And the business office is lean. Most charters have no in-house risk manager to notice that the D&O limit is shared, or that the individuals have no protected layer, or that the program was never built to hold the people up when the entity's bills come first. So the gap sits there unseen until a claim makes it visible, which is the worst possible time to find it.

## What a broker actually doing the job would do

A broker who takes a charter board seriously starts from the individual, not the entity. They walk the board through the four ways a shared-limit policy can leave a person exposed, in plain language, before anything goes wrong. Then they place management liability as a School Leaders program built to protect the individuals within it, with dedicated limit for the directors and officers and terms that reach the situations a charter is most likely to face. They confirm it in writing, in one program, instead of selling a separate policy and handing you the reconciliation problem.

That is the difference between a broker who treats your trustees like real people with real assets on the line, and one who treats the account like a file to clear before the deadline.

## Before you tell your next recruit they are protected

You do not have to take anyone's word for this, this page included. You just have to check one thing on behalf of the people who serve you.

So here is the question worth sitting with. If your school went insolvent tomorrow and a former board member were sued personally for a decision the board made years ago, whose money defends her, and does her protection come from a program actually built to hold up the individual, or is she quietly sharing one limit with the entity because nobody built it right?

If you cannot answer that with confidence, the volunteers on your board do not have a coverage problem yet. They have a question nobody has asked on their behalf. The check happens long before any claim. What it costs to skip it only shows up on the day someone you recruited finds out the protection she counted on was never built.
