# The Day Your Board Member Finds Out The School's Insurance Stopped Protecting Her

*A plain-English look at Side-A DIC coverage, the layer that protects an individual director's own money when the main D&O policy runs dry, gets refused, or never responds at all.*

---

A volunteer joins your board to help a school. Parents, a local accountant, a retired principal, people who serve for free.

Years later one of them is named, personally, in a lawsuit over a board decision.

She assumes the school's policy has her. It did. Right up until the moment it didn't.

Here is the moment.

---

## The reassurance every volunteer is given

Nobody agrees to sit on a charter school board expecting to bet their house on it. They show up because they care about the school, they have a skill the board needs, and somebody told them the school carries Directors and Officers insurance so they're protected.

That's usually true. The school does carry D&O, and most of the time it works exactly the way everyone assumes. A claim comes in, the policy defends the board, the volunteers never feel a thing.

The problem isn't the normal case. It's the handful of specific situations where a standard D&O policy quietly stops protecting the individual and starts protecting everyone but the individual. Those situations are rare, but they're the exact ones that pull a board member's personal savings into a courtroom. And there's a coverage built for nothing else but plugging them.

So let me walk through how a D&O policy is actually wired, where it leaves an individual exposed, and what closes the gap.

## The three sides of a D&O policy, plainly

A standard D&O policy is really three coverages in one, and the industry calls them sides.

**Side A** pays the individual directors and officers directly, when the organization can't or won't reimburse them. This is the part that protects a person's own assets. It usually pays from the first dollar, with no deductible, because it exists for the moment the individual has nobody else standing behind them.

**Side B** reimburses the organization when it does step in and cover its people. The school pays the defense, the policy pays the school back.

**Side C** covers the entity itself, the school as an organization, when the school is named as a defendant.

That structure is fine. Here is the detail that matters more than any other on this topic: all three sides almost always share one combined limit. One pot of money for the individuals, the reimbursement, and the entity, all at once.

Hold that thought, because the shared limit is where the trouble starts.

## Where the individual gets left exposed

Most board members never learn that "the school has D&O" and "I am personally protected no matter what" are two different statements. There are four ways the gap opens, and a school can hit any of them.

**The shared limit gets eaten.** Picture a serious lawsuit that names the school and several board members together. The school's own defense, the Side C piece, starts drawing down that single shared pot. Defense costs typically erode the limit rather than sitting on top of it, so a hard-fought case can burn through the money before the individual trustees are ever made whole. The entity and the individuals are drinking from the same glass, and the entity often drinks first.

**The school can't indemnify.** Side A exists for when the organization is financially unable to back its people. For charter schools that is not a hypothetical. Charter funding follows enrollment, authorizer renewals can go sideways, and a school under real financial distress, or one that has closed, may simply have no money to defend a former board member. Insolvency and bankruptcy are among the most common reasons an organization cannot indemnify. The board member who governed during the hard years is the one most likely to get named, and the least likely to have a solvent school behind them.

**The school is legally barred from indemnifying.** In some situations the law itself prohibits an organization from covering an individual, certain derivative suits and public-policy limits among them. The school might want to help and be told it cannot.

**The policy itself fails to respond.** The main D&O policy could be rescinded, voided over something in the application, or the carrier could refuse a claim or become insolvent. When the policy that was supposed to protect the individual is the policy that falls apart, the individual is the one holding the bag.

In every one of these, the entity has options. The individual volunteer does not. And the protections people assume cover them, state volunteer-immunity laws and the federal Volunteer Protection Act, are far thinner than they sound. They offer limited immunity from certain claims, but they generally do not pay the cost of defending a lawsuit, and they fall away for gross negligence or conduct outside the volunteer's role. A baseless claim still has to be fought, and fighting it costs real money.

## The layer built for exactly this: Side-A DIC

There is a policy designed to sit above the regular D&O program and do one job: protect the individuals when the underlying coverage runs out, refuses, or fails. It's called Side-A DIC. DIC stands for difference in conditions.

It does two distinct things, and both matter.

First, it adds **excess Side-A limit**. This is dedicated money reserved for the individual directors and officers, sitting on top of the main policy. When the shared limit underneath gets drained by the entity's side of a fight, this dedicated layer is still there for the people. The entity cannot touch it.

Second, and this is what separates it from a plain excess policy, it **drops down**. "Difference in conditions" means the policy fills the gaps where the underlying coverage does not respond. If the main insurer becomes insolvent, refuses to pay, or tries to rescind, or if the school cannot indemnify, the Side-A DIC policy drops down and pays in place of the coverage that failed. It steps into the hole rather than waiting politely on top of it.

Two more features make it the strongest personal protection a board member can have. These policies are typically written with far fewer exclusions than a standard D&O form, which means they respond in situations where the underlying policy would have denied. And they are usually **non-rescindable**, meaning once the policy is written the insurer cannot later void it over something in the application. For an individual director, non-rescindable is everything, because it removes the risk that a mistake someone else made on a form wipes out their personal protection.

One honest caveat. The exact triggers, exclusions, and drop-down wording vary from carrier to carrier, so treat all of this as the concept rather than fixed contract language. The purpose is consistent across the market even when the fine print isn't.

And to be clear about where this fits: Side-A DIC complements the base D&O and management liability program, it does not replace it. The main policy still does the everyday work. This is the safety net under the trapeze, for the day the everyday work isn't enough.

## Why this lands harder on a charter board

For a large company with deep reserves, some of these gaps are remote. For a charter school they cluster together in a way that is almost tailor-made to catch a volunteer.

The board is unpaid. Parents and community members serving for free are the ones whose personal homes and savings are on the line, and a D&O claim names those people by name. The money they're risking is their own, earned somewhere else, put at stake to help a school.

The financial fragility is real and it correlates with claims. The board decisions most likely to generate a lawsuit, closures, layoffs, authorizer fights, financial restructuring, are the same decisions that happen when the school has the least money to defend anyone. The exposure and the inability to indemnify peak at the same moment. That is precisely the moment Side-A exists for.

And the business office is lean. Most charters have no in-house risk manager to notice that the D&O limit is shared, or that there's no dedicated Side-A layer, or that a single rescission could unravel the whole thing. So the gap sits there unseen until a claim makes it visible, which is the worst possible time to discover it.

## Why the gap is usually nobody's fault but the broker's

Here is the part that should bother any board that has been coasting on "we have D&O."

A generalist broker, the kind who treats a charter school as a small account to renew quickly, sells the base D&O policy and stops there. The conversation about what happens to an individual director when the shared limit is exhausted, or when the school is insolvent, or when the policy gets rescinded, never takes place. Not because it's complicated to explain, but because raising it means doing more than processing a renewal.

The easy-button renewal rolls the same policy forward every year. Nobody pulls the structure apart. Nobody asks whether the individuals have their own protected limit or whether they're sharing one pot with the entity. Nobody mentions that Side-A DIC exists, because mentioning it invites a question the broker would then have to answer.

So your volunteers walk around believing they're fully protected, based on a policy nobody has examined from the individual's point of view. The reassurance is free. The gap is invisible. And the only person who pays for the silence is the board member whose name ends up on a complaint.

## What a broker actually doing the job would do

A broker who takes a charter board seriously starts from the individual, not the entity. They look at the D&O program and ask whether the directors and officers have limit that's protected from the entity's side of a shared-limit claim. They walk the board through the four ways a standard policy can leave a person exposed, in plain language, before anything goes wrong. They size a dedicated Side-A DIC layer to the real risk the school carries, and they confirm in writing that it's non-rescindable and that its drop-down terms reach the situations a charter is most likely to face.

That's the difference between a broker who treats your trustees like real people with real assets on the line, and one who treats the account like a file to clear before the deadline.

## Before you tell your next recruit they're protected

You don't have to take anyone's word for this, this page included. You just have to check one thing on behalf of the people who serve you.

So here's the question worth sitting with:

If your school went insolvent tomorrow and a former board member were sued personally for a decision the board made years ago, whose money defends her, and has anyone confirmed she has protected limit that the school's own legal bills can't drain first?

If you can't answer that with confidence, the volunteers on your board don't have a coverage problem yet. They have a question nobody has asked on their behalf. The check happens long before any claim. What it costs to skip it only shows up on the day someone you recruited finds out the protection she counted on was never built.
