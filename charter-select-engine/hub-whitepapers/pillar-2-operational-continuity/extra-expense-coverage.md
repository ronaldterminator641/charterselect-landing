# The Fire Doesn't Close Your School. The Eight Months Without a Classroom Does.

*A plain-English look at Extra Expense and Business Income coverage: the two parts of your property policy that decide whether a building loss is a hard year or the end of the school.*

---

A pipe bursts over a holiday weekend, or a fire takes the gym wing, or a storm peels the roof back and the rain ruins everything under it.

The building is the obvious loss. Your property insurance writes a check to rebuild it, and that part works mostly the way you'd expect.

Here's what nobody put in front of you. The rebuild takes seven, eight, maybe twelve months. Your school does not get to pause for twelve months. The kids still need a room, the staff still need paychecks, and your funding still depends on those kids showing up and being counted. The check that rebuilds the wall does nothing about any of that.

That gap, the cost of staying open while you can't use your building, is what this paper is about.

---

## Two different coverages doing two different jobs

People lump these together and then carry the wrong one. They are separate, and a school needs both.

**Business Income** replaces the money you stop bringing in while operations are interrupted. If a covered loss shuts down part or all of your school and your revenue drops, Business Income is meant to fill that hole, plus keep covering the expenses that don't stop just because the building did. Payroll, your loan or lease payments, utilities you're still on the hook for.

**Extra Expense** pays the added costs you take on specifically to keep operating, or to get back operating faster, after that same loss. Renting alternate space. Bringing in temporary or portable classrooms. The moving costs. Setting up phones, internet, servers, and student devices in the new spot. Busing students to wherever you've relocated them. None of these are costs you'd normally have. You're spending the money precisely so the school doesn't go dark.

One backfills lost revenue. The other funds the scramble to keep running. A serious property loss triggers both at once, and they work as a pair.

## Why a generic school misses this and a charter school can't afford to

For an ordinary business, Business Income is the headline and Extra Expense is the smaller sidekick. Customers go elsewhere for a few months and the revenue line takes the hit. So most commercial policies are built and sold with that shape in mind, Business Income front and center, Extra Expense as a modest add-on.

A charter school does not work like an ordinary business, and this is where the standard package quietly fails it.

Your revenue is per-pupil funding. It follows enrolled students who are actually attending and being counted. That changes the whole calculation, because you have a problem a coffee shop never faces: if you can't give families a classroom, those families don't wait. They enroll their kids somewhere else, and the funding that student carried leaves with them. It does not automatically come back when your building reopens.

So the real threat of a long closure isn't only the rebuild bill. Every week without a room is a week families are deciding whether to stay. Lose enough of them and you don't just lose this year's funding. You lose next year's enrollment and the financial footing the whole school stands on.

That flips the priority. For you, Extra Expense, the money that gets students back into a room somewhere fast, is often the coverage that saves the institution, because keeping students enrolled is the same thing as protecting your funding.

## The clock that controls all of it: the restoration period

Both coverages run on a timer, and hardly anyone gets shown how that timer is set.

The **restoration period** (sometimes called the period of restoration or indemnity period) is the window the insurer will pay for. It generally starts at the date of the loss and runs until the property is repaired or replaced, or should reasonably have been, with normal operations restored. Once that period closes, the payments stop, even if your school hasn't fully recovered.

Two things about that window catch schools off guard.

First, it tracks how long it should reasonably take to restore the property, not necessarily how long it actually drags out. Permitting delays, a contractor backlog, custom work, scarce materials: those can push your real timeline well past what the policy treats as reasonable. If your coverage assumed an eight-month restoration and the rebuild honestly takes fourteen, those last six months can land on you.

Second, on many policies the standard restoration period is shorter than what a full school rebuild requires. Between insurance adjusting, design, permits, construction, and inspection, large losses routinely run past a year. If the policy's clock runs out before your doors are truly back open, the coverage stops carrying you at the worst possible point.

There's an extension worth knowing by name. **Extended Business Income** keeps coverage running for a defined stretch after you reopen, while you're rebuilding enrollment back to where it was. For a school clawing students back after a long displacement, that tail can matter as much as the main coverage.

## Where the limit gets set too low, or skipped entirely

Two failures show up over and over on school policies, and both trace back to nobody modeling the actual scenario.

**Extra Expense is missing or token.** The policy was sold around Business Income, and Extra Expense came along as a small flat limit or got left off. Then a loss hits, the school needs portable classrooms and a leased annex and busing on day thirty, and the limit covers a fraction of it. The coverage built for the exact emergency you're in turns out to be the thinnest line on the page.

**The Business Income limit was a guess.** Someone picked a round number years ago, it renewed on autopilot every year since, and nobody ever tied it to your real budget, your real payroll, or how long you'd actually be down. A school running on per-pupil funding with months of payroll and fixed costs to cover can burn through a casually chosen limit fast.

The fix is not exotic. It's a number you build instead of guess.

## Best practice: model the displacement before it happens

Stop thinking about the limit as a number on a form and start thinking about a question. If we lost our main building tomorrow, how would we keep students in a room somewhere, how long would it realistically take, and what would every step cost?

Walk it through.

- **How long until students are back in a room somewhere?** Not the full rebuild. The interim. How fast could you stand up portable classrooms or lease nearby space, and what's the genuine lead time on each?
- **What does the interim cost, month by month?** Rent on alternate space, the portables, moving, rewiring IT and phones and student devices, busing students to the temporary site. Add it up across the months you'd realistically be displaced.
- **What revenue is exposed, and for how long?** Tie the Business Income limit to your real budget and per-pupil funding, and stress it against a restoration period long enough for a serious rebuild, not a hopeful one.
- **What happens to enrollment, and how long to win it back?** This is where Extended Business Income earns its place, covering the stretch after you reopen while families come back.

Run that exercise and the right limits stop being a guess. You set Extra Expense to fund a real relocation, Business Income to your actual exposure, and a restoration period that matches how long a school rebuild honestly takes. The exact figures depend on your budget and building, so they get built with you, not pulled off a shelf.

## What a broker who knows charter schools actually does

A generalist sells you the property policy, points at the building limit, and renews it every year without ever asking the one question that matters: how would this school keep operating if it couldn't use this building. So Extra Expense stays thin, the Business Income limit stays a guess, and the restoration period stays whatever the carrier defaulted to.

A broker who works with schools starts from the displacement. They map how you'd relocate students and how long it'd take, size Extra Expense and Business Income to that reality, check the restoration period against a real rebuild timeline, and make sure something like Extended Business Income is there for the enrollment you're rebuilding after you reopen. Then they show you the math in plain language instead of a flat limit you're told to trust.

The point isn't to make you fear a fire. It's to make sure that if one comes, the loss is a building, not the school.

## The question worth sitting with

Picture your main building unusable tomorrow morning. Families are calling, staff are waiting, and the funding clock is running on every student who can't find their classroom.

Do you know, with any confidence, how many months your current policy would actually keep this school operating and how much it would pay to get students back in a room somewhere? Or has that limit just renewed itself for years while everyone assumed the building check was the whole plan?

If you can't answer that, you don't have a coverage problem yet. You have a number nobody has ever checked against the day it would have to work.
