# You Lent the Gym for a Saturday. Now Their Player's Lawyer Is Calling Your Insurer.

*A youth league used your space for a weekend tournament. A kid got hurt. The claim landed on your school's liability policy, not theirs. Here is the paperwork that decides who actually pays.*

A travel basketball league rents your gym for a Saturday tournament. Forty kids, a couple hundred parents in the bleachers, refs you have never met running the games.

In the third game, a player goes down hard chasing a loose ball, hits the wall padding wrong, and breaks a wrist that does not heal right.

The family hires a lawyer. The lawyer sues everyone with a name on the building, and that includes your school.

The league had its own insurance. You assumed that meant the league's insurance would handle it. So why is your carrier the one opening a claim file?

## The thing nobody walks you through

Your building rarely sits empty. A church holds Sunday services in your auditorium. A youth soccer club runs clinics on your field. A summer camp takes over the place in July. A vendor sets up a fall festival in your parking lot. Maybe your own team practices at the city rec center while your gym is being refinished.

Every one of those is a risk transfer waiting to happen, or one that quietly failed. The difference is almost entirely paperwork, and the paperwork is the part a generalist broker almost never raises with a school.

Here is what happens when an outside group uses your space and something goes wrong. An injured person, or their attorney, names every party they can find. The school owns the building, so the school gets named. From that moment, the only question that matters is whose insurance defends and pays. Do the paperwork right and the answer is the user's insurer. Skip it and the answer is yours.

Let me walk through how to make sure it is theirs.

## Risk transfer, in plain English

When you let an outside group use your facility, you want the risk of their activity to ride home with them, not stay parked on your policy. That is the entire goal. Four pieces make it work, and they only work together.

**A written facility use agreement.** The contract that says who is using the space, when, for what, and on what terms. It is where the rest of the protections live. A handshake and a key is not a facility use agreement.

**A current certificate of insurance from the user.** The certificate, often called a COI, is proof that the outside group carries its own general liability insurance, with the limits and dates shown. You want this in hand before they set foot in the building.

**Additional insured status naming your school on the user's policy.** The piece that does the heavy lifting, and the one most people think they already have and do not. More on this in a second, because the distinction is where schools get hurt.

**A hold-harmless and indemnification clause inside the agreement.** The contractual promise that if their activity causes a loss, they cover the school for it, including defense costs. It is the legal backbone behind the insurance pieces.

Skip any one and the structure leaks. Most schools have one or two, usually the wrong ones.

## A certificate of insurance is not the same as being additional insured

This is the misunderstanding that costs schools the most, so read it twice.

A certificate of insurance proves the outside group has a policy. That is all it does. It is a snapshot, a piece of evidence. It does not give your school any rights under their policy. If you are only holding a COI when a claim hits, you are a bystander looking at proof that someone else has coverage you cannot actually use.

Additional insured status is a different animal. When the user's policy is endorsed to name your school as an additional insured, your school becomes a protected party on their policy for claims arising out of their use of your space. Their insurer steps in to defend and pay first, on their dime, for a loss tied to their activity. Their policy, their limits, their lawyers, ahead of yours.

The actual additional insured status comes from an endorsement on the user's policy. The certificate may reference it, but the certificate is not the endorsement. A COI that simply lists your school in the description box, with no endorsement behind it, can leave you holding nothing when it counts. When you can, ask to see the endorsement itself, not just the certificate that claims it exists.

Put simply: the certificate tells you they have insurance. The endorsement is what puts that insurance to work for you.

## Why this lands harder on schools

A business that lends out a conference room takes on some risk. A school takes on more, for two reasons.

First, the activities are higher-energy and involve kids. Sports, camps, performances, festivals. More motion, more people, more ways for someone to get hurt on your floor.

Second, schools say yes a lot. Being a community hub is part of the mission. The church needs a space, the league needs a court, the scouts need a room, and a school leader who cares wants to help. That generosity is good. Without the paperwork behind it, it means your liability policy is quietly subsidizing every outside group that walks through the door.

When the loss comes, it does not sort itself by whose fault it was. It chases the building, the deep pocket, the named insured on file. That is you, until the paperwork says otherwise.

## How the gap got there

Walk into most schools and ask who reviews the insurance on the groups using the building. The honest answer is usually nobody. A front-office staffer hands over a key, the facilities calendar fills in, and the insurance question never comes up.

That is not really the school's failure. It is the broker's. A broker who knew charter schools would have asked, years ago, a simple question: who uses your building when students are not in it? Then they would have handed you a facility use process and a checklist so the answer was handled every time, not improvised by whoever was at the front desk.

The generalist does not ask, because asking means understanding how your building actually gets used, and that is more work than processing a renewal. So the renewal gets processed and the gym keeps getting lent out on a handshake. The gap is not bad luck. It is the predictable result of an account nobody is minding.

## What to require before you hand over the keys

This is the part you can act on. Treat it as a standing checklist that every outside use clears before access is granted.

1. **A signed facility use agreement** for the specific dates and activity. No agreement, no access.
2. **A current certificate of insurance** showing general liability, with limits your school sets in advance, and dates that actually cover the use. An expired COI is the same as no COI.
3. **Additional insured status** naming your school on the user's policy, confirmed by endorsement, not just a line typed into the certificate. For sports leagues and camps especially, this is non-negotiable.
4. **A hold-harmless and indemnification clause** in the agreement, written so the user defends and indemnifies the school for losses arising from their use.
5. **The right scope and limits for the activity.** A chess club is not a tackle football camp. Match what you require to the risk in front of you.
6. **A named, responsible contact** for the using organization, on the agreement, who is accountable for their people.
7. **A file that someone actually keeps.** Agreement, COI, and endorsement stored together, refreshed when a group returns next season. Coverage that lapsed in March does you no good in October.

Run a group through that list before they get a key, and the risk of their activity rides home on their policy where it belongs. Skip it, and you find out what you were missing on the worst day, in a deposition.

## The flip side: when your school is the guest

It runs the other direction too. When your team practices at the city rec center, or your students perform in a rented hall, or you hold an offsite event in someone else's building, expect that organization to ask you for the exact same things. A use agreement. A certificate of insurance. Additional insured status naming them on your policy. An indemnification clause.

That is not them being difficult. That is them doing precisely what you should be doing. A broker who knows schools makes issuing those certificates and endorsements quick and routine, so your programs never get stalled at someone else's front desk because your paperwork was not ready.

## The question worth sitting with

Think about the last outside group that used your building. The league, the church, the camp, the vendor in the parking lot.

Did your school sign an agreement with them, hold a current certificate, and get named as an additional insured on their policy by endorsement? Or did someone just hand over a key because they seemed legitimate and the calendar said the space was open?

If you cannot answer that with confidence for the groups in your building right now, you do not have a coverage problem yet. You have a question nobody has checked. The check is cheap. The Saturday it goes wrong without it is not.
