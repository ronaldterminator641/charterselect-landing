# The Disaster Blueprint: Why One School Reopens in Three Weeks and the One Next Door Fights Its Carrier for a Year

*A pre-loss claims-readiness playbook for charter schools. The claim you win is the one you prepared for before the building ever flooded.*

---

Two schools take the same storm. Same county, same week, same kind of water through the same kind of roof.

One is back in session in under a month. The other is still arguing with its insurer the following spring, classes scattered across a borrowed church basement and two trailers, payroll bleeding, families pulling kids.

Same damage. Same coverage on paper. The difference between those two outcomes was decided long before the wind picked up.

---

## What actually separates them

People assume the fast recovery is about the policy, that one school bought better coverage. Sometimes that's true. More often, both schools had adequate policies and only one was ready to use it.

A property claim is a documentation contest. The carrier doesn't pay because the building is wrecked. It pays because you can prove what you had, prove what it was worth, prove the damage, and prove you did your part to limit it. The school that hands all of that over in the first week gets paid in the first month. The school reconstructing its records from memory while the mold spreads gets a fight.

You build the winning side of that contest before the loss, or you don't build it at all. After the alarm goes off is too late to start photographing equipment that's already underwater.

So here is the blueprint. None of it is expensive, and most of it is an afternoon of work you can start this week.

## 1. A current asset and property inventory, with photos and values

This is the foundation, and it's the one almost everyone skips.

You need a running list of what the school owns: furniture, technology, kitchen and HVAC equipment, science and shop gear, instruments, library and curriculum materials, playground and athletic equipment, anything bolted down and anything that isn't. For each significant item or category, capture a photo, a rough age, and a value. Serial numbers on the expensive electronics. Receipts where you have them.

Walk every room with a phone, closets and storage areas included. Video works too, narrate as you go. Then redo it once a year, because the inventory you took three years ago doesn't include the forty Chromebooks you bought last fall.

When a fire takes a wing of the building, the adjuster will ask what was in it. "A lot of expensive stuff" is not a number they can cut a check against. A dated photo and a value is.

## 2. Policies and the declarations page, stored off-site

Your insurance documents cannot live only in a filing cabinet inside the building they're insuring. If the building burns, so do they.

Keep a full copy of every policy, and especially the declarations page, somewhere the disaster can't reach. A cloud drive. A personal email to yourself. A folder your broker holds for you. The dec page is the one-page summary of what you bought: limits, deductibles, named insureds, and the coverage forms attached. In the first chaotic hours after a loss, that page tells you what you're working with.

Put the claims phone number and your broker's direct line in that same folder. When the office is taped off, you don't want to be hunting for a number on a sticky note inside it.

## 3. Know your deductibles and sublimits before you need them

This is where schools get blindsided, and it's pure homework you can do on a quiet afternoon.

Pull the dec page and read three things.

Your deductible. The amount you eat before coverage kicks in. Property deductibles can be a flat dollar figure, but for wind, hail, and named storms many policies use a percentage of the building's insured value instead, which can be a much larger number than people expect. Know which kind you have.

Your sublimits. A policy can carry a healthy overall limit while quietly capping specific things far lower. Flood and water backup, debris removal, certain types of equipment, and other categories can each have their own ceiling buried in the forms. A sublimit is the most you'll get for that category no matter how big your total limit looks.

Your extra expense and business income terms, if you have them. These are what fund the trailers, the temporary lease, the generator rental that keeps you operating while the building gets rebuilt. Knowing what's there before the loss tells you how fast you can move.

If you can't explain your wind deductible and your top three sublimits in plain language right now, that's the assignment for this week.

## 4. A documented contact tree

In the first hour after a loss, confusion costs you. People stand around assuming someone else made the call. Three different staff give three different stories to a reporter. Nobody starts the photos.

Write down, on one page, who does what:

- Who calls the broker and the carrier, and who is the backup if that person is unreachable.
- Who documents the damage with photos and video before anything gets touched.
- Who handles arranging emergency mitigation, the board-up, the water extraction, the tarp.
- Who is the single voice to families and to media. One voice. Everyone else routes to them.
- Who pulls the off-site policy folder and inventory.

Put names and cell numbers on it. Keep a copy off-site with the policies. A loss is going to happen on a Saturday or at 2 a.m., not during a calm staff meeting, and the plan only helps if it exists before the phone rings.

## 5. Mitigate immediately, because the policy requires it

Here is a piece of the contract most school leaders have never been told. Your property policy obligates you to take reasonable steps to protect the property from further damage after a loss. It's a standard duty, and ignoring it can reduce or jeopardize what the carrier pays.

In plain terms: if a pipe bursts, you're expected to shut the water and get the standing water out, not let it sit for a week and then bill the carrier for the mold that grew because you waited. Tarp the open roof. Board the broken window. Move the salvageable equipment to dry ground.

Two things make this work. First, keep the receipts, reasonable emergency mitigation is itself usually a covered cost. Second, and this is the part people miss, document the damage before you start cleaning it up. Mitigate fast, but photograph first.

## 6. Document everything, during and after

The cleanup instinct is strong and it works against you. Once the wreckage is hauled to a dumpster, your proof goes with it.

Photograph and video the damage from every angle before anyone moves anything. Keep damaged property where it is until the adjuster has seen it or has told you in writing it's fine to discard. Start a simple loss log the day it happens: a running record of what occurred, who you spoke with, what they said, and what you spent. Keep every receipt, the mitigation crew, the temporary equipment, the extra staff hours, all of it.

That log is boring to keep and decisive to have. Months later, when the details have gone fuzzy and the claim is in negotiation, the school with the contemporaneous record is the one whose version holds up.

## 7. Understand the timeline and the proof of loss

A property claim runs on a clock, and the clock has your name on it, not the carrier's.

Most policies require prompt notice of a loss, so report it quickly even before you know the full scope. Many then require a sworn proof of loss, a formal document where you state the amount you're claiming, typically due within a set window after the carrier requests it. There are deadlines for submitting your documentation and, often, time limits on how long you have to sue if it comes to that. Missing these isn't a paperwork hiccup. It can be the carrier's clean reason to deny.

You don't have to memorize the windows. You have to know they exist and have someone watching them, which is exactly what the next section is about.

## Where the generalist broker leaves you standing alone

Most of what's above never gets mentioned to a school because the broker who sold the policy treats the account like a renewal to process, not a client to protect. They emailed the dec page once, two years ago, buried in a PDF nobody opened. They never explained the wind deductible or the flood sublimit, and they certainly never walked the building to talk about an inventory.

Then the loss happens, and the difference shows. The generalist is hard to reach, slow to call back, and absent from the room where the adjuster decides what your damage is worth. They didn't prepare you before, and they don't advocate for you during. You're negotiating a six-figure claim alone, against a professional who does this every day, with records you're rebuilding from memory.

A broker who actually does the job builds this blueprint with you on a calm Tuesday. They make sure you understand your deductibles and sublimits before a storm tests them. And when the loss comes, they show up, push back on a lowball adjustment, and keep the timeline and the proof of loss from quietly running out on you. That presence at claim time is the whole reason a broker exists. It's also the moment the cheap, absent ones vanish.

## The question worth sitting with

Picture the worst Tuesday. A fire, a flood, a storm that opens the roof. Tonight.

Could you, tomorrow morning, hand someone a current inventory of what the building held, a copy of your policy from outside the building, and a one-page plan of who does what? Or would the honest answer be that you'd be starting all of it from scratch, in the wreckage, on the worst day your school has had?

Because that gap, between what you could prove today and what you'd wish you'd done, is the exact gap your carrier will price the claim inside of. Better to close it on a quiet afternoon than discover it the morning after.
