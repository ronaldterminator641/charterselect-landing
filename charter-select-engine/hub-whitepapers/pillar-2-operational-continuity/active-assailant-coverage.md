# You Were Sold an Active Assailant Policy. Do You Know What Actually Triggers It To Pay?

*Why the honest question is not whether to buy active assailant coverage, but what you are trying to protect against, what your package already covers, and how much residual risk you would rather self-fund than insure.*

---

A school leader buys an active assailant policy. In their mind, they just protected the organization. If the worst happens and the school gets sued, this is the policy that defends them.

Then a careful reading of the form tells a different story. The trigger is physical damage to the property. The coverage is built to respond to a first-party loss, not to defend the organization against a lawsuit. What they thought they bought and what they actually bought are two different things.

That mismatch is common, and it is the real problem with how this coverage gets sold. Not that it is worthless. That people are handed it as an easy answer without anyone explaining what it does, what they already own, or what they are actually trying to protect.

---

## First question: what are you trying to protect against?

"Active assailant coverage" is a label, not a single product. The forms behind it vary widely, and the most important variable is the trigger.

Some are first-party policies. They respond to a violent event or the physical aftermath of one, and they fund extra expense: crisis response, security, remediation of the space, business interruption from the disruption. Some are written narrowly around actual property damage. Others are third-party liability forms, built to defend and indemnify the school when it gets sued for negligent security or failure to protect. A few bundle pieces of both.

These are not interchangeable. If your fear is a lawsuit against the organization and the board, a property-triggered extra-expense form does not answer that fear. If your fear is the operational bill after an incident, a liability form does not answer that one. Buying without knowing which risk you are insuring is how a school ends up with a policy that pays for the wrong thing on the worst day.

So the first move is not shopping. It is naming the exposure you actually want to close. Lawsuit defense for the school and its leaders. Medical bills for the injured. Death benefits for families. Counseling and trauma care. Public relations and crisis communications. Physical remediation of the space. Lost enrollment. Those are different risks, and they land in different policies.

## Second question: what does your package already cover?

Here is the part a generalist skips, because it involves reading your program instead of selling on top of it. A lot of what an active assailant policy advertises may already sit in coverage you carry.

A well-built school package often already funds crisis counseling and therapists, and includes public relations or crisis communications coverage for exactly the kind of event that puts press trucks in your parking lot. Your student accident policy is written to pay medical expenses and death benefits for students, which is a meaningful share of what families need in the aftermath. And if the concern is the board or the school being sued, that defense belongs in your School Leaders management liability program, which was built to respond when the organization and its leaders are named.

Stack those up and a picture emerges. Counseling, PR, student medical and death benefits, and lawsuit defense may already be answered across your package, your student accident coverage, and your management liability program. The standalone active assailant policy is often sold as if none of that exists, when for a properly built school program much of it already does.

That does not make the standalone useless. It changes the question. Instead of "do I need active assailant coverage," the real question becomes "what is genuinely left uncovered once I account for everything I already carry?"

## Third question: what is your risk tolerance, premium versus out of pocket?

Once you subtract what the package, student accident, and management liability already handle, you are left with a residual. Usually it is some combination of physical remediation of the space and the enrollment-driven business interruption that is unique to a school funded by attendance.

That residual is a decision, not an emergency. It comes down to how much you would rather pay in premium now versus carry out of pocket if the event ever happens.

A school leader who has looked honestly at the numbers may decide to self-fund the property and remediation piece rather than pay a recurring premium for a low-probability loss. Another may want the enrollment protection insured because a funding cliff is the one thing the budget cannot absorb. For some campuses, terrorism coverage is a separate lever worth pricing, since certain events can intersect with a terrorism form and its own trigger. There is no universal right answer. There is only the answer that fits your exposure and your appetite for risk, made with the full picture in front of you.

The generalist version of this conversation skips all three questions and lands on "here is a quote." The honest version walks the exposure, subtracts what you own, and puts the residual in front of you as a choice you get to make.

## Why the generalist reaches for the standalone anyway

Selling a new policy is a clean transaction. It has a premium, a proposal line, and a renewal. It looks like action.

Doing the actual work is harder. It means reading your property form, your extra-expense and business-interruption terms, your student accident policy, and your management liability program, then telling you in plain language which risks are already handled and which are not. It means explaining that the active assailant form on the table is triggered by property damage, or by an actual event, or by a lawsuit, and matching that against the fear that made you ask. It means being willing to say "you may already be covered for most of this, and the rest might be worth self-funding," which is a sentence that does not earn a commission.

A broker who hands a school an active assailant policy as the answer, without walking what the school already owns and what it is trying to protect, is selling a product, not managing a risk. The gap that leaves is not a missing policy. It is a decision nobody helped you make with the facts.

## What handling this properly looks like

Start with the exposure, written down. What specifically are you trying to protect: the organization from suit, the injured from medical cost, families from loss, the space from closure, the budget from an enrollment drop.

Then inventory what responds today. Read the package for crisis counseling and PR coverage. Confirm what the student accident policy pays for medical and death benefits. Confirm the School Leaders management liability program covers the board and the entity for a negligent-security or failure-to-protect suit. Mark each exposure as covered, partially covered, or open.

Only then look at any standalone active assailant form, and read its trigger against the open items. If it closes a real gap the package cannot, at a limit that matches the actual cost, it earns its place. If it duplicates coverage you already carry, that is premium spent twice. And whatever residual is left after all of it becomes a clear-eyed risk-tolerance call, premium against out of pocket, made by the school leader and not defaulted into by a broker.

## Before you assume the policy you bought does what you think

You do not need to dwell on the scenario to answer the question that matters. You just need to know what your coverage actually does, and what you were actually trying to buy.

So here is the one to sit with. The active assailant policy on your desk, or the one someone is trying to sell you, what triggers it to pay, and is that the same thing you were trying to protect against? And once you account for the counseling, the PR, the student medical and death benefits, and the board defense you may already carry, what is genuinely left for it to cover that you could not just decide to self-fund?

If nobody has walked you through those two questions, you do not have a coverage answer yet. You have a quote, and a decision that was never really put in your hands.
